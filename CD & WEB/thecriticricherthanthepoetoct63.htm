THE CRITIC RICHER THAN THE POET!

 

K. VISWANATHAM

Reader in English, Andhra University

 

Ernest: You have explained to me that criticism is a creative art. What future has it?

 

Gilbert: It is to criticism that the future belongs.

–O. Wilde

 

It is not often realized that the critic is as important as a poet, that he should be intellectually better equipped and judicially more balanced, variously experienced and maturely catholic to a greater extent than a poet. ‘A poet is born, not made’ a time-honoured truism and like all truisms partially wrong; there is no poet who is not made. ‘I lisped in numbers for the  numbers came’ or ‘I sing as the linnets sing’ are statements to be taken with a pinch of salt; the numbers do not come nor does a poet sing as the linnets sing; the craft of words is not so easily come by; it demands the sweat of the brow with brains behind it. Who has taught the cuckoo its cry and the spider its skill?, we say disarmingly. But word craft is obtained with labour and comment on it demands more sensitive and varied labour; there is no unpremeditated verse; we can lay the road to every singer’s Xanadu and note the drops of water that make the mighty ocean and the pebbles that make the mighty mountains. The composition of verse, wrote Wordsworth, is infinitely more of an art than men are prepared to believe; and absolute success in it depends on innumerable minutiae...Milton talks of pouring ‘easy his unpremeditated verse’. It would be harsh, untrue, and odious to say there is anything like cant in this, but it is not true to the letter, and tends to mislead (Letters: Later Years).

 

This essay combats in Section I the time-honoured statement that the poet is more important than the critic, assuming that the poet and critic are separate and distinct; in Section II this very assumption is questioned; the poet and the critic are said to be one; Section III harks back to Section I and topsy-turvily states that the critic is richer than the poet, as against the generally accepted view that the poet is more precious than the critic. If it is said that the poet is born, we can as well lay down that a critic too is born. Arnolds and Eliots are as rare as Shelleys and Keatses. We hesitate even to state that a critic is as important as the poet. We take the poet’s importance for granted and we are niggardly in according an equally high place to the critic. If we say cabbage is as important as potato in the kitchen, the implication is that the potato’s importance is above dispute (hence the Essay on Milton under the caption Potatoes) and the claims of the cabbage, in the writer’s view, are high though not generally recognized; the statement is like special pleading. The critical power is, Arnold declared roundly, of lower rank than the creative. Some may relent towards the critic grudgingly and concede that he is important but not as important as the poet. If the child were to say: My father is important in the house when mother is away, the father need not feel aggrieved; he should thank God for having at least secondary importance when there was the risk of complete annihilation as in Gardiner’s essay The Carriage Window.

 

The adorer of poets may even argue that the critic is not only not important but is even a drag, a nuisance. Then comes the rub: You praise one as a Hercules and your opponent denies that he is even a human being and remarks that he is a rat and you retort: If he is a rat, you are a mouse. An academic controversy becomes an interesting Bestiary of abuse. A champion of criticism opposing the advocate of poets may assert that the critic is more important than the poet. No criticism, no poetry–is his war cry and he is ready for scalping. We have funny notions about a critic and funnier ones about a poet. We envisage a kind of blood feud between the critic and the poet. Creative activity is defiant of critical ‘bossing’; hot blood leaps over a cold decree. It is even suggested that great periods of creation are followed by periods of criticism; the ebb of creation is the tide of criticism and we forget Wordsworth, Coleridge, Keats, Shelley, Arnold and others.

 

We hear such statements: Has any one succeeded in writing a competent tragedy by a study of Aristotle’s Poetics? Does one become an excellent cook by reading a book on cookery or obtain the love of a woman by memorising Vatsayana? Quintilian said long ago that a treatise on agriculture does not yield a bumper harvest and a booklet ‘How to do it yourself’ on swimming does not keep one afloat on the waters. We are reminded of Richards’ observation that we become poets by ordering our experiennce, not by mastering a technique. We thus reduce the critic to a portentous budget of Do’s and Dont’s which invariably make or mar poetry. To be polemically successful we forget the Poetics is derived from the Greek Drama and that rules by themselves are not the forbidden fruit that brings death into the world of art. In the age of science we are not justified in criticising critical canons. P. A. W. Collins points out justly: This process of formulating rules is analogous to the scientist’s generalizing after observing many particular instances….No literary critics denied that poets needed nature (inborn abilities) as well as Art (which included a knowledge of the rules) but they thought that in poetry as in science ‘Well begun is half done’. The rules by themselves do not part the waves as a treatise on music does not make one sing. Addison wrote: (Spectator; 592) who would not read one of Shakespeare’s plays where there is not a single rule of the stage observed than any production of a modern critic where there is not one of them violated. Shakespeare is the great In Spite Of. This attitude is ungenerous and meaningless.

 

The critic is also described as a frustrated poet who wreaks vengeance by damning all poets just as a male scorned by a woman damns all women as riotous mares. Such a frustrated poet will do well to follow Wilde’s advice and join the conspiracy of silence. The story of the tailless fox is enacted and the limping Hephaistos jeers at the straight-legged Ares. There is one sort of criticism, says Maugham, that is evidently futile. This is that which is written by the critic to compensate himself for humiliations he has suffered in his early years.

 

The function of the critic is viewed in various ways. Criticism is a spiritual adventure, and is creation based on creation, and it has autonomous existence. It is valuable in itself as secondary or derivative inspiration. “Who, again, cares whether Mr. Pater has put into the portrait of Mona Lisa something  that Leonardo never dreamed of...And so the picture becomes more wonderful to us than it really is and reveals to us a secret of which, in truth, it knows nothing and the music of the mystical prose is as sweet in our ears as was the flute player’s music that lent to the lips of La Gioconda those subtle and poisonous curves.” (Oscar Wilde: The Critic as Artist) It is dangerous for the critic, writes Maugham, to look upon his activity as creative. His business is to guide, to appraise and to point to new avenues of creation. (The Summing Up)

 

Criticism is the free play of the mind, the broadcasting of what has been best thought and said in the world enabling the full flowering of genius. Criticism is the midwife that brings to birth many that might have aborted or been born ugly freckled whelps of unlicked thought. Arnold points out the shaping spirit of criticism in his well-known essay on the Function of Criticirm. Criticism creates the intellectual atmosphere of the age. It is the star

 

To every wandering bard whose worth is unknown

Although his height be taken.

 

It does not murder to dissect but dissects to create. It is the Control Tower to the navigators of the skies.

 

The critic is the interpreter of literature. Honestly speaking, not many have the capacity of grasping a great poet; as has been caustically said, we have a tongue in our mouth, not in our minds and that is unfortunate. A great critic is as rare as a great poet and Dissociation of Sensibility is as capital a contribution to the vocabulary of criticism as ‘It beggared all description’ is to the vocabulary of poetry. The jocular statement is made that Shakespeare failed in the paper on Shakesperian Tragedy because he did not study Bradley. There is the popular saying: What does the father know of his daughter? It is son-in-law who knows her. There is close similarity between this popular saying and Shaw’s retort when asked to comment: How should I know? I am only their author. Browning once asked by a friend to explain his poem, as the story goes, observed: At the time of composing the poem two persons knew its meaning: God and Browning. Now God alone knows its meaning. It is not a funny anecdote but an important statement made by Prof. Abercrombie in his Principles. He begins with the story of Socrates approaching some poets for an explanation of their poems and they expressed their inability. These anecdotes posit a separate critical activity. Criticism is the last reward  of consummate scholarship. It is harder to be a critic than to be a poet. At twenty one can afford to be a poet but it is only at forty one can achieve criticism. In the words of Dr. Johnson in the Preface to Shakespeare: As among the works of nature no man can properly call a river deep or a mountain high without the knowledge of many mountains and many rivers. So in the production of genius nothing can be styled excellent till it has been compared with other works of the same kind.” The poet can be viewed either as a deliberate and conscious artist or as a reed rough which music blows, that is, an unconscious artist who does not know what he is doing. That is why poetry is defined as an irrational faculty, as even madness. Not even the greatest poet can say: I will compose poetry at 8 a. m., just as he can say: I will have my breakfast at 8 a. m. How the smell of the cooking egg, the hum of the typewriter and a page of Spinoza get unified is a mystery and lies on the Road to Xanadu.

 

II

 

So far we have assumed that the critical and the creative are two separate and distinct activities. The critic may be as important as the poet or the poet may be as important as the critic; the assumption is presumptuous. What should be emphasized is the truth that the poet and critic are one; the poet is the critic and the critic is the poet. The first critic of a poem is the poet himself. The first critic of Lycidas is the author of Lycidas; the second critic is the chorus of Samson Agonistes; the third critic is the total poetry of Milton and still the fourth critic is the Greek and Hebrew poetry; Tillyard is perhaps the fifth critic. The best commentary on Keats’ The Nightingale Ode is not Middleton Murry but Keats’ Ode on the Grecian Urn; the Nightingale and the Grecian Urn are one soul in two bodies. Eliot profoundly remarked that the critical function operating in poetry is far in advance of the critical function operating on poetry. This is in complete agreement with what we find in Samskrit. Samskrit Alamkara sastra refuses to draw any distinction between the two functions; ‘pratibha’ is bifurcated into karayitri and bhavayitri. The poet is a maker and the critic a man of discernment, as the etymology points out. How can one be a maker if one is not a Person of discernment? Kavi is a seer, a singer; sahridaya is one who is identical with the poet. The best description of a critic is the oft-quoted: yesham kavyanusilana abhayasavasat visadibhute manomukure varnaniyatan-mayibhavanayogyata te svahridayasamvadabhajah sahrdayah. By the habit of contemplating great poems the critic’s mind is enlarged and spotless like a mirror, meaning that his mind is free from various prejudices that conflict with connoisseurship; he acquires the capacity of being moved by what is described and finally becomes ‘oned’ with the poet’s heart. A great Samskrit scholar salutes this synthesis of Sivaa and Siva, mother and father, woman and man, poet and critic, poetry and criticism, charm and response, genius and taste, kavi and sahridaya in a sloka:

 

paraspara samasvada prathamana satatvayoh

kavita budhayoh yogam namami sivayoh yatha,

 

That is why, one surmises, Sanskrit literary criticism has always considered a poem not as the unique product of a unique mind but as a unique object of unique, enjoyment; that is, the emphasis is not on What mind created this poem (as Western criticism always elaborates) but on How is this poem enjoyed; the dish as the expression of the personality of a cook is ignored: dish as one meant for the guest is discussed.

 

Congreve makes one of his lovers tell a lady: “Your beauty is my creation.” No lover, no beauty. Whether ladies approve of that impudence or not, their figure is neutral in the absence of a lover. Similarly no ‘prakhya’ without ‘upakhya’. When there is identity of heart (samana dharma) in the poet and critic, there is fair assessment. That is why it is asserted that poets can be judged by poets only. Otherwise the swan is judged as the ugly duckling by the ducks waddling in ignorance.

 

In another sense poets are said to be the worst judges of other poets. Wilde writes: “The gods are hidden from each other. They can recognize their worshippers. That is all.” Creation limits; contemplation widens. Endymion was a pretty piece of paganism to Wordsworth and to Byron Wordsworth was one

 

Who both by precept and example shows

That prose is verse and verse is prose.

 

It is wrong of Eliot to state that poets alone are the jury of judgement. Prof. C. S. Lewis makes delightful mincemeat of this remark in The Preface to Paradise Lost: “It is for cooks to say whether a given dish proves skill in the cook; but the product on which this skill has been lavished is worth eating or no is a question on which a cook’s opinion is of no particular value.” (p. 11)

 

III

 

From time to time, every hundred years or so, it is desirable that some critic shall appear to review the past of our literature and set the poets and the poems in a new order, so wrote Eliot in the essay on Arnold. Today don’t we live within the curve of Eliot’s critical idiom? He is entirely responsible for our present attitude to the early poetic Drama, the Metaphysicals, Milton and Dryden. And who can do this except a major critic and poet? The critic is not the enemy of the poet. Bagehot in his classic The English Constitution remarked that England coined the remarkable expression: Her Majesty’s Opposition, calling with incredible political generosity even the Opposition as Her Majesty’s. Such is the role of the critic in the parliament of letters. The critic even adds to the poet. He gives no less than he receives, lends as much as he borrows. “The poem,” wrote Eliot, “if it is of any depth and complexity, will have meanings in it concealed from the author and should be capable of being read in many ways and with a variety of emotional emphasis.” There is liberty of interpreting. In this sense the critic is said to be richer than the poet. Wilde, of course, exaggerates this liberty, ignoring blissfully the duty of looking at the object as it really is: “Criticism treats the work of art simply as a starting point for a new creation. It does not confine itself to discovering the real intention of the artist and accepting that as final.” And in this it is right, for the meaning of any beautiful created thing is, at least, as much in the soul of him who looks at it as it was in his soul who wrought it. Nay, it is rather the beholder who lends the beautiful thing its myriad meanings and makes it marvellous for us and sets it in some new relation to our age so that it becomes a vital portion of our lives…..For when the work is finished it has as it were an independent life of its own and may deliver a message far other than that which was put into its lips to say.”

 

These two citations, the one sober and the other exaggerated, prove that the critic is not a derivative parasitical hanger-on. Criticism can be as creative as poetry. “It is undeniable,” writes Arnold, “that the exercise of a creative power, that a free creative activity is the true function of man; it is proved to be so by man’s finding in it his true happiness. But it is undeniable also that man may have the sense of exercising the creative activity in other ways than in producing great works of literature or art; if it were not so all but a very few men would be shut out from the true happiness of all men; they may have it in well-doing, they may have it in learning, they may have it even in criticizing.” Who shall say that Dr. Johnson’s Preface is less creative than his Vanity of Human wishes? To deny creativity to criticism is to deprive creativity of its vast estate. Just as in Language the word is said to be more and less than the object for which it is a symbol, the critic too is more and less than the poet–less that he fails to capture the first fine careless rapture, more that he reacts in his unique way. The power to create, the power to enjoy, the power to criticize are three in One and one in Three:

 

Nayakasya kaveh srotuh samano anubhavastatah

 

They are blended in the sahridaya who is lapped up by poetry as gallopingly as fire devours dry wood: sariram vyapyate tena sushkam kashtamivagnina. Criticism is considered in various ways, the enemy of creation, or itself creation, or the pioneer of creation, or the interpretation of creation, diachronically and synchronically, subjective or objective. The great critic, writes Maugham in The Summing Up, should have sympathy as wide as his knowledge is universal He must be a psychologist and physiologist...he must be a philosopher. He must be familiar not only with the literature of his native land...He must support himself on tradition. In fact a great critic must be a great man. Max Eastman remarked that one who attempts literary criticism without a diploma in psychology should be jailed at once; it shows how well-equipped the critic has to be. As Forster points out, a critic has no right to the narrowness which is the frequent prerogative of the creative artist (Aspects of the Novel).
