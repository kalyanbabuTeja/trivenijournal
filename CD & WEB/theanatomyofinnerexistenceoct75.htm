THE ANATOMY OF INNER EXISTENCE

 

An outline survey of the subjective experience with special reference

to Sri Aurobindo.

 

ROMEN PALIT

Sri Aurobindo Ashram

 

English poetry is varied in its moods, depth, heights, approach and expression. The Saxon element has endowed it with strength, the French with lyrical subtlety and grace and the Celtic influence has engraved the inlay-undertone of the spiritual motif. All these and many other divers trends have lent the richness to the tapestry of English poetry.

 

From the objective to the subjective, from the material to the spiritual and from the romantic to the abstract are some of the noticeable trends. We have traversed a long way from the crude verse of the old English to the fullness of modern poetry and the growth would appear almost incredible. Specially in contrast to the French poetry, for example, which has not revealed any noticeable dramatic change across the passage of these three centuries except for a few brilliant exceptions.

 

Early English poets had no scope to turn inwards or scrutiny the working of the mind or the emotions and the passions and their ways. They were too preoccupied with the joy of freedom the shackles of the Norman or Saxon influences, and the harsh, crude restrictions of the dwarfing dark ages. They were concerned either with abstractions as in Spencer or with life as a great vehement tide as in Shakespeare or again with the majestic grandeur of thought that swept through Milton. They had no patience or time to plunge within or touch the inner chords of existence. Perhaps the time had not yet come.

 

It was when the large afflatus of the renaissance had passed through English poetry in the late seventeenth century, that poets settled down to a quieter life and were in a mood to throw a glance within. This came with the metaphysical poets. But that regard was superficial, hesitant and far from a deliberate plunge. It was rather a side-tracking from life into a sort of mental curiosity to know something of the ‘inner’ but managed to become at its best a hazy and abstract theological quest. But this trend was not a repetition of the pre-Elizabethan wave of abstraction but sought a new mode of religious sentiment and profound personal thought.

 

Donne revealed that change. This was his background: the creative afflatus, with its exuberance and clinging to life which the Renaissance had brought in its wake, had ebbed. The greater crowning glory of the romantic poetry was yet to come. Donne and others who came after him filled up this poetic vacuum. But Donne did not actually usher in a new age, but revealed a new mood, struck a new accent which was a turning away from the external glamour of life to some sort of awaking into a new religious awareness. And this was a definite breaking away from the renaissance spirit.

 

Donne had a complex personality. He was moved by the dying surge of the renaissance, not in its aspect of creativity, but in its sensuous enjoyment on one hand and the religious contemplative bent on the other. He was different from Milton with his ardent puritanical zeal or from Wordsworth with his contemplative profundity. He was not a hedonist like Shakespeare and far removed from Spencer’s non-intellectual sincerity. Between the mass of poetry he wrote on love, we discover an undercurrent of a few poems on religion which marks him off as a new type of poet, the Metaphysical one.

 

His interest in religion was intellectual and had no emotional appeal. His followers like Vaugham or Crashaw pursued the same way of thinking and writing. It would be difficult to call the trend an inward-moving current. But certainly this could be termed as a dim forerunner or a faint overture of a greater age to come.

 

Yet this religious temperament was a bit unnatural for his amorous trait, so much so, Joan Bennett comments in her book Five Metaphysical Poets, “His love for God was a dropsy.” Donne himself acknowledges this in his Holy Sonnets No. 18:

 

“But though I have found thee and thou

My thirst hast fed;

A holy thirsty dropsy meets me yet.”

 

He speaks of the church which was traditionally believed to be the bride of Christ:

 

“And let my amorous soul court thy holy Dove.”

(Holy Sonnets No. 18)

 

But the insistence of his amorous nature plagued him and this strife formed the substance of his ‘metaphysics’. Therefore he cried to Christ,

 

“Take me to you, impress on me, for I

Accept you, enthrall me, never shall be free

Nor ever change, except you ravish me.”

(Holy Sonnets No. 19)

 

Here the three words, “imprison”, “enthrall” and “ravish” are used in a special sense. He implored Christ to imprison him in his theological bond which freedom in the real sense, so that he did not fall back into his old nature, his amorous cravings. Not only that, the poet yearned to be completely under Christ’s spell so much so we may take this ravishment, a total overpowering by this higher influence. Moreover the poet sought to make it abundantly clear that this was an inner subjugation in place of carnal captivity.

 

Herbert was not so dominant a voice among the metaphysical poets. But his place was nevertheless significant. He, with his unsophisticated nature, was akin to John Bunyan of religious simplicity. This candid approach was a sharp contrast to Donne’s sophisticated and many-mooded personality. Herbert bore a simple devotion for Christ. But if this is all, then he could fill an insignificant place among the throng of such writers. When he speaks:

 

“Where is that ancient heat towards thee where with whole Showls of martyrs once did burn beside other flames?”

 

we cannot dismiss him easily. The flame here bears a two-fold, meaning and may seem ambiguous at the first sight. The inner fire of zeal which consumed the seekers of old “beside other flames” made them unique in self-sacrifice. The external flame of torture that gaves them martyrdom is referred to only as a metaphor. There is a note of regret in the poet’s tone. Addressing Christ, he said:

 

“Thou art my loveliness, my life, my light,

Beauty alone unto me,

Thy bloody death undeserved make thee

Pure red and white.”

 

The expression is candid, the approach is spontaneous. The last line denotes a symbol: the red stands for sacrifice and the white for purity.

 

Both Donne and Herbert or even other metaphysical poets were not concerned with the inner realities and subjective truths as such. But from the hedonism of the preceding era that made extroverts of the poets, this religious spirit and metaphysical turning made the poets to turn within. Religion marks the first step towards this inner discovery. Theology forms the portal to this secret plunge.

 

Opposite in approach and psychological make-up was Henry Vaugham, one of the true spokesmen of this era. He marked a curious poetical phenomenon. At the outset the influences of the other poets were quite perceptible, specially that of Herbert. He could very easily be termed as an eclectic with his borrowed ideas and trends. A greater scrutiny would reveal that he possessed certain aloofness of mental temperament, an intellectual unconcern, on one hand and a Wordsworthian outlook of regarding Nature as the source of revelation in the other. In both he showed no emotional commitment or participation.

 

Listen :

 

Fair order’d lights (whose

motion without noise

Resembles those true joys

whose spring is on that hill where you do grow

And we here cast below)

Now beneath and now above.

And in your vast progression overlook

The closest night, the closest nook.

(The constellation)

 

He regarded the whole gamut of creation with its complex movement, which in its macrocosm and microcosm bore the same unitary rhythm. The key to this unity was God. Here he was referring to God as an intellectual idea rather than a vast overseering presence and this entity was limited by Christian theological concept. And surely this concept was different from Donne’s or Herbert’s. His mind was not veiled by emotions or moved sufficiently by Christ’s presence, but attempted to review the whole thing dispassionately. He said:

 

“Thus doth God disord’d man

(which none else can)

Tuning his breast to rise or fall;

And by a sacred, needful art

Like strings, stretch every part

Making the whole most musical.”          (Affliction)

 

In other words his concept of God differed from Milton’s which is marked by grandeur, or Crashaw the source of love. We can sum up Vaugham in Joan Bennett’s words: “From first to last Vaugham laid himself open to other poets, but he emerged with a way of perceiving and expressing perceptions which bore his own hall-mark.” (Five Metaphysical Poets. P. 89)

 

The concept of God in Crashaw was yet different. He was perhaps nearer to Herbert than Vaugham. The personality of Christ dominated his idea and he equated God’s love for man with maternal love. He identified true love and physical pain. He told us:

 

“O soft self-wounding Pelican

Whose breast weeps Balm for wounded man

And this way bend thy benign flood

To a bleeding heart gasps for blood.”

 

(“The hymn to Saint Thomas in adoration of the blessed sacrament” by Richard Crashaw.)

 

The whole is a symbol. Pelican standing for Christ, Blood bears two-fold meanings, in Christ, it is grace, in man it is life and is salvation.

 

He was also aware of the acute division between the soul and the body, not as psychological problems nor as a philosophical enigma of idealism and hedonism, but as a Christian theological issue. Theology was the profoundest element one could conceive at that period. Man whose components were soul and body, was:

 

“A little world made cunningly

Of elements, and Anglicke spright.”

(dialogue between the soul and the body)

 

The body was accused by the soul of ushering in ills of pain, disease and death. While the body accused the soul of not delivering it of these. The primal sin was the cause. The nett result of this was unmitigated distress for man.

 

Marvell regarded the issue in a naive and even squint-eyed manner over-simplifying the complex question that had many factors. The human existence was not so elemental or crude that could be reduced to such simple terms. Perhaps as a metaphysical poet he could have offered us a more acceptable solution.

 

The succeeding ages made no attempts at a subjective probing or sounding. Pope, Dryden, Cowper and many others were mainly concerned with man’s social and moral conditions. Religious outlook gave way to social and politico-economical awareness which in its turn, gave raise to the drama and the fiction. Poetry was thus pushed to a secondary position in literature.

 

England had to wait for another century when a deeper and more sensitive consciousness could be born as a result of the nation’s inner maturity and expressional perfection. And the first voice we hear is that of Blake.

 

Blake was a poet who did not move out of the Christian background. And he was endowed with an occult perception which gave rise in him an authoritative tone. A commentator writes, “Blake is undoubtedly convinced that he is the prophet of Christ who is the only God. But he is also the Bard and so must identify the Poet and the Prophet.” (The Divided Image by Margaret Budd, Pp. 4 and 5)

 

We are justified to come to this conclusion when we hear from him:

 

“Hear the voice of the Bard!

Who present, past and future sees;

Whose ears have heard

The holy word

And walked among the ancient trees.”

(Blake Complete Works. P. 898)

 

Blake pierced the veil of mental haziness, of abstract figuration, of roundabout symbolic imagery and reached a point of religious experience, leaving behind him the awe of distance and adoring humility of the devotee. Instead he had come to a height (which may almost seem a height of religious conceit) of prophetic vision. Therefore Sri Aurobindo says, “Blake stands out among the mystic poets of Europe.” (Sri Aurobindo Centenary Vol. 9. P. 529)

 

There is an incongruity and division between the poet and the prophet. The poet usually seeks to know the world by plunging into it. The prophet on the other hand escapes from it to know the secrets of the Beyond. Blake attempted to unify this contradiction by telling us, “A poet, a painter, a musician, an architect, the man or woman who is not one of these is not a Christian (Blake - Complete Works. P. 989)

 

The statement would seem only half true in its face value. But this denotes Blakes’ standpoint and revealed his mental attitude. A poet and a Christian cannot go beyond the pale of his vision. This would mean falsifying his standpoint and in fact all he stands for. He asserted further, “Let every Christian as much as is in him lies, engage himself openly and publicly before all the world in some mental pursuit for the building up of Jerusalem.” (Ibid, P. 1026)

 

Here mind in Blake’s terminology is imagination and Jerusalem for Albion or Heaven on Earth. This narrows down the scope of the vision and gives a special colouring to the attitude adopted.

 

Shelley too had a vision of the future. But he was a rebel, an iconoclast. Blake on the other hand went deeper and his Christian faith coloured his future’s sight where Shelley had no religious bias and moral scruples. Shelley on the other hand was not a mystic which Blake was, in spite of the theological background. Prometheus was Shelley’s image, the breaker of past conventions. Blake did not go to mythology to bring alive his idea, which was neither a symbol nor an allegory. His vision was direct, shorn of any round-about poetic efflorescence. He revealed:

 

‘In futurity

I prophetic see

That the earth from sleep

(Grave the sentence deep)

Shall arise and seek

For her maker meek

And desert wild

Become a garden mild.’

(Blake - Poems and Prophecies. P. 23)

 

In the face value this would seem a statement of the Christian idea of heaven on earth. Behind stands the truth of the earth’s renaissance into light, the sleep being the symbol of ignorance to which man was at present subject to with its concurrent falsehood, error, pain and death. The desert stood for uncreative emptiness of human existence which shall, when the earth was reborn, becomes a flowering expanse, a land of beauty, harmony and peace.

 

The image of child-Christ as the embodiment of God is also expressed by Blake, just as in the Indian scriptures, the infant Krishna. He said that God

 

“Smiles on thee, on me, on all

Who became an infant small;

Infant smiles are his own smiles

Heaven and earth to peace beguiles.”

(Ibid P. 14)

 

Sri Aurobindo sums up Blake thus: “Blake lives ordinarily far up in this middle world of which Coleridge only catches some glimpse or at most stands just over the border. His seeing teems with its images, he hears around him the echoes of its sounds and voices. He is not only a seer, but almost an inhabitant of other planes and other worlds......” (Sri Aurobindo Centenary Vol. 9. p. 124)

 

Margaret Budd commenting upon Blake does not strike the nail upon its head as does Sri Aurobindo, yet her comment is not quite invalid. She says: “Blake is concerned with the drama of inner life. To an extent this is true of all poets.” (Divided Image, p. 189)

 

This can be taken as a general statement. Other romantic poets like Keats, for example, was too sensuous to be drawn into inner fields or subtler domains. Shelley was a poet of beauty. But he did not take the inner plunge. Wordsworth was a contemplative poet concerned with thoughts rather than actual subjective experiences. Byron was flamboyant, an extrovert. He would not be bothered by such things, which he might label as mere moonshines.

 

Coming to the Victorian age, we hear only one significant subjective voice, that of Francis Thompson among the din of other voices that of Rossetti, the pre-Raphaelies, or Morris who attempted to recreate the Homeric tradition or Tennyson busy with Arthurian romances. These poets had other interests than to live or see the inner world.

 

Francis Thompson gives an authentic experience of God, not as a sweet, or merciful presence too high for human frailty, a suffering, tortured Christian deity. His God bears no religious stamp but has an universal character. His godhead is a demon of passion, a power of overwhelming strength and love, who pursues the human soul. We get a reverse picture of the common concept that is the human soul who sought after God, and who somehow remains aloof and above the grooves of man’s ignorance. He cries out:

 

Naked I wait Thy love’s uplifted stroke

My harness piece by piece Thou hast hewn from me

And smitten me to my knee;

I am defenceless utterly;

I slept, me thinks, and woke

And, slowly gazing, find me stripped in sleep

In the rash lustihood of my young powers,

I shook the pillaring hours

And pulled my life upon me grimed and smeared

I stand amid the dust O’ the mounded years

My mangled youth lies dead beneath the heap.

(Francis Thompson, Poetical Works. p. 92)

 

The symbol is unmistakable. The harness is the yoke of man’s bonds, desires and other elements of lower nature. This, God smites and breaks down, which perhaps could be taken as the stroke of doom. Further, man’s defence against God is his weakness, his ego, his wickedness and his pride. So God smites, those it loves for the human soul’s escape and denial is in fact is an escape and denial of the deeper truth of his own self. Lastly God’s assault is the gesture of its love. For otherwise man could not be saved and he would for ever meander in the blind alley of his lower existence and there would be no progress.

 

In another poem apart from the Hound of Heaven, which we have quoted, he speaks of human existence as a cycle.

 

“My little worlded self–the shadows pass

In this thy sister-world as in a glass

Of all processions that revolve in thee

Not only of cyclic man

Thou hast discerned the plan

Not only of cyclic man, but of cyclic me.”

(Ibid. p. 210)

 

The evolution of the human soul is here hinted clearly. This perhaps is not the strict Christian concept, nevertheless it denotes Thompson’s intuitive seizing of the spiritual truth.

 

In the late Victorian age comes Yeats, whose real flowering occurs in the last part of the nineteenth and the first three decades of the present century. A Celtic in the soul of him, Yeats is Blake’s follower and his spiritual disciple. He confesses this in his autobiography:

 

“I had an unshakable conviction arising how or whence, that the invisible gates would open as they did for Blake.” (Autobiography. p. 218)

 

That is, he sought to be both a poet and a prophet with the added element of necromancy. And here in the point is the cardinal point of difference between the master and disciple. For Blake had no faith in magic which he termed as “Beautiful witchcraft”.

 

There is a point of meeting between them. Yeats declares, “There is an old saying that God is a circle whose center is everywhere. If this is true the saint goes to the centre, the poet and the artist to the ring where everything comes round again.” (Yeats, Cutting the Agate. p. 94)

 

But Yeats is conscious of Blake’s superiority, in spite of the latter’s faulty expression and poetical technique. For the former is a matchless craftsman in word-music and irreproachable verse technique. Not only that, Yeats is envious of Blake’s “seeking and finding” of truth, while he, as a poet, is a “systematic mystic” which all poets must be, using symbolism in any guise, personal or traditional. That is the opinion of Yeats. The point of view is partially true. For all poets are not mystics and symbolism is not necessarily a hallmark of mysticism. Mysticism must have behind it a spiritual vision, an experience. Thus any occult finding or touching of a supra-physical fact is not mysticism. Sri Aurobindo says, “Yeats never got beyond a beautiful mid-world of the vital Antariksha, he has not penetrated to spiritual heights.” (Sri Aurobindo Centenary Vol. 9. p. 532)

 

His Celtic sensibility makes him aware of hidden supra-physical realities and he attempted to garb these in mental images which more often than not made it all the more vague. For example, he speaks of the Rosy Cross, the cross for the body, Rose for the soul. He says,

 

“Yet one joy have I, hid close,

He who measures gain and loss

When he gave the Rose

Gave me alone the Cross.”

(A Song of the Rosy Cross)

 

His creation abounds with Irish heroes, animals, birds, mythical islands, woods and cities all drawn either from Celtic mythology or from his imagination. In his later years he evolved a symbol of a “great wheel with twenty-eight phases from dark to full and back to dark and the four symbols of flower cup, fruit and the sceptre which are related to the four elements.” (The Identity of Yeats. p. 153)

 

Passing from Yeats to A. E., another mystic, Sri Aurobindo says, “A. E., had an unequal profundity of vision and power and range in the spiritual and psychic field.” (Sri Aurobindo Centenary Vol. 9. p. 532)

 

To him thoughts are definite entities. That is, all thoughts are forms which are a spiritual phenomenon. He says:

 

“And all my thoughts are throngs of living souls

They breathe in me, heart to heart allied,

The joy undimmed, though when the morning tolls

The planets may divide.” 

(Selected Poems of A. E. p. 10.)

 

He speaks of a reality behind this physical world:

 

“But I have touched the lips of clay,

Mother, thy rudest sod to me

Is thrilled with fire of hidden day

And haunted by all mystery.”

(Ibid. p. 17)

 

His Celtic temperament has imbibed the Indian spirit, where Yeats is typically Irish. For example, here in the above example he speaks of earth as the mother, which is an Indian concept. Elsewhere he has spoken of Krishna, the Indian god-child.

 

He voices another true spiritual and psychological experience, the loneliness of man’s soul:

 

“Our true hearts are for ever lonely

wistfulness is in our thought,

Our lights are like the dawn which only

Seem bright to us and yet are not.”       

(Ibid. p. 30)

 

Like a natural outflowering and inevitable phase of poetical evolution comes Sri Aurobindo in the sphere of subjective, spiritual and mystic poetry. Where the utterances of the past English poets were merely fragmentary or like early dawn-breaks, the first signs of promise, he comes not only fulfilling this promise but brings with him a plenitude of direct, authentic and majestic spiritual utterance. But it is surprising that he should be so little known or else brushed aside and considered primarily as a Yogi or a revolutionary with no importance given to his outstanding poetic genius. A critic exclaims, “How shall we crown Sri Aurobindo? Is he greater as a Yogi than as a philosopher? Does the literary critic in him out-top the sociological thinker? Does he shine brighter as a politician or as a poet? It is difficult to decide. Everywhere Mount Everest seems to face Mount Everest.” (The Poetic Genius of Sri Aurobindo by K. D. Sethna. p. 1)

 

But in the present context we shall study neither his philosophy, which is outside the pale of this study, nor his entire poetical works which shall need an encyclopaedic treatment, but we shall limit ourselves to the review of some of his later sonnets, which is both personal and of a revealing character.

 

This choice we have made is deliberate, for Sri Aurobindo, though he is multitudinous in his poetical expression, never strikes a personal note.

 

At the outset let us see what he says about himself, his mission and his Yoga:

 

“He who would bring the heavens here

Must descend himself into clay

And burden of earthly nature bear

And tread the dolorous day.”

(God’s labour, Sri Aurobindo Centenary Vol. 5. p. 99)

 

This is the incarnation, who is both a man and a god; a man raised to godhood and God leaned in man and in the fusion is the Avatar. But the labour and sacrifice of this embodiment is colossal. The cross, the betrayal, the utter callousness of man is all he encounters in his attempt to bring down a higher consciousness here. He is also a man. And how he emerged into a larger consciousness is a process of Yoga–a way and example for those who come after him.

 

The first step is the growing awareness of the inner world which Sri Aurobindo calls “the kingdom within”:

 

“Wider behind the vast universe

Our spirit scans the drama and the stir,

A peace, a light, an esctasy, a power

Waiting at the end of blindness and the curse

That veils it from its ignorant minister

The grahdeur of its free eternal hour.”               

(Ibid. p. 129)

 

This awareness comes as one stands back from external mind’s “swirl of thought” or “life’s stuff of passionate unease,” or “heart’s unsure emotion frailty wrought” and “the body’s solid transciences.” This ushers in another significant experience. The ramparts of consciousness are shattered and he triumphantly exclaims:

 

“I contain the whole world in my soul’s embrace:

In me Acturus and Belphegor burn,

To whatsoever living form I turn

I see my own body with another face.”

(Ibid. p. 131)

 

This is not a symbol, an abstraction. There is no hestitancy or veil of any kind. For this is a candid expression of an undeniable experience. It would appear that he is reporting some inner occurrence without any embellishment or poetical efflorescence. At the same time there is no accent of bravado, something colossal he has achieved or attained to some matchless heights of spiritual becoming. The whole thing comes like a spontaneous record of an inner fact. That is all.

 

But the unique condition is not the outcome of toil and growth of a few years, it is the culmination and result of millenniums his soul had passed. The difference is, he is aware of the steps he has crossed, the divers grades of consciousness he has traversed. Therefore he says:

 

“I saw my soul a traveller through Time;

From life to life to cosmic ways it trod,

Obscure in the depths and on the heights sublime,

Evolving from the worm into the God.”

(Ibid. P. 147)

 

This chain of evolution is both a physical fact in front and a hidden line of soul-growth at the rear.

 

Though he is one with this line of ascent, he also stands back and watches–“the time-gallopings of the deathless horse” (Ibid. p. 131) which is the symbol of infinite power working in Time and this Energy–Whirls

 

“The stars and nebulae in its long train,

Like a huge serpent through my being curls

With its diamond hood of joy and fangs of pain.”

 

And this puissance

 

“Rises from the dim inconscient deep

Upcoiling through the minds and hearts of men,

Then touches on some height of luminous sleep

The bliss and splendour of the eternal plane.”

 

And yet he is the watcher of this power which in its sweep touches the inconscience of the world in its base and the eternal Bliss of the Absolute at its height, passing through the cosmic space of the human existence in the middle. This is the one Power that is behind life, mind and body, it is behind them as their support, below them as their origin and above them as the cosmos transcends. Therefore he says:

 

“All this I bear in me untouched and still

Assenting to Thy all-wise inscrutable will.”

 

He is not only the watcher, he is also worker. Not the common labourer, a puppet in the hands of fate, but a conscious and divine toiler. He tells us of his adventure:

 

“On the waters of a nameless Infinite

My skiff is launched; I have left the human shore.

All fades behind me and I see before

The unknown abyss and one pale pointing light.

(Ibid. p. 137)

 

This adventure has two-fold aims: to find the source of hidden light lost in the “unknown abyss”–in the inconscience and call down the sudden blaze of God” and the “marvel and rapture of the Apocalypse” from above so that “the gulf twixt the depths and heights is bridged.”

 

The result of all this labour is the descent of the “golden light”, the gnostic afflatus and the poet reveals to us the steps of this great descent. The first step of the descent is in the mind;

 

Thy golden Light came down into my brain

And the grey rooms of mind sun-touched became,

A bright reply to wisdom’s occult plane,

The calm illumination and a flame.”

(Ibid. p. 134)

 

Brain is the seat of intelligence, the thousand-petalled lotus of the Tantra. This symbol of a psycho-physical truth. “Mind,” says Sri Aurobindo, “in its essence is a consciousness which measures, limits, cuts out forms of things from indivisible whole.” (Life Divine, American ed. p. 151) This limitation thus forming the basis of human ignorance, disappears and it happily answers to the call of the Infinite Supermind whose portion the mind is. Mind now becomes a calm light where before it was only half-shadow and half-knowledge.

 

Descending further:

 

“Thy golden Light came down into my throat,

And all my speech is now a tune divine,

A paeon-song of thee my single note;

My words are drunk with the Immortal’s wine.”

(Sri Aurobindo Centenary Vol 5. p. 134)

 

The throat centre, according again to Tantra, is the centre of speech, the Anahata. It is also the centre of self-expression. Commonly human speech is an obscure vehicle voicing trivial emotions, feelings or thought which now had become in the poet pregnant with immortal power, for word was the first creator, according to the Bible and Om the great primordial vibration of the creation.

 

The light descends further:

 

“... … into my heart

Smiting my life with Thy eternity;

And it has grown a temple where thou art

And, all its passions point towards only thee.”

(Ibid)

 

The heart is the seat of emotions, the seat of higher rife, the stronghold of energy, or according to Sri Aurobindo. “Most people live in the vital. That means, that they live in their desires sensations, emotional feelings, vital imaginations and see and experience and judge everything from that point of view. It is the vital which moves them.” (Sri Aurobindo Centenary Vol. 24. p. 297)

 

Suddenly all the narrowness and triviality were smitten by “eternity,” the endless power, of light and of joy. And the heart became a temple of the Absolute.

 

Coming to the end of the journey,

 

“Thy golden Light came down into my feet

My earth is now thy play-field and thy seat.”

(Sri Aurobindo Centenary Vol. 5. p. 134)

 

Feet is the seat of inconscience, the citadel of matter, the antipode of the spirit. Here the great light came down and the opposite poles of existence were bridged. And the result she sings:

 

“My breath runs in a subtle rhythmic stream,

It fills my members with a might divine:

I have drunk the Infinite like a giant’s wine.

Time is my drama or my pageant dream.

Now are my illumined cells joy’s flaming scheme

And changed my thrilled and branching nerves to fine

Channels of rapture opal and hyaline

For the influx of the unknown and the Supreme.

 

I am no more a vassal of the flesh,

A slave to Nature and her leaden rule;

I am caught no more in the sense’s narrow mesh

My soul unhorizoned widens to measureless sight,

My body is God’s happy living tool,

My spirit a vast sun of deathless light.’

(Ibid. p. 161)

 

This marks the high watermark of subjective poetry, the poetry of spiritual experience.

 

This had a meagre, hesitating and clouded beginning in the metaphysical poets, growing in more transparent in Blake, Thompson, A E and Yeats and finding its full diapason of expression in Sri Aurobindo. Surely there are others who have touched on this particular subject, but this is only an outline, hence we have limited ourselves only to the outstanding poets of this genre. Otherwise we would have to include a great many writers like Coleridge, Rilke, Gibran and Tagore.
