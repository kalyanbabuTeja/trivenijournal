‘TRIVENI’ HAS SHED LIGHT ON MY PATH.

                                 BLESSED BE HER NAME’

 

‘THE TRIPLE STREAM’

 

K. RAMAKOTISWARA RAU

 

On Quitting Office

 

In a determined effort to reform the Congress organisation, the ruling party in India empowered the Prime Minister to accept resignations of senior Ministers of the Central Cabinet and of Chief Ministers of some of the States. This was meant to be the first step in the process of winning for the Congress the affection and the confidence of large sections of Indian citizens who had expressed doubts about the ability of the Congress Governments at the Centre and in the States to administer the affairs of the nation with some degree of altruism. The reverses sustained by the Congress in important by-elections brought matters to a head. While these defeats did not materially alter the party position in the Central Assembly, they lowered the prestige of the Congress and fostered the impression that the Party was fast losing its hold over country. The opposition parties could not successfully challenge primacy of the Congress nor provide an alternative Government. But they could urge that the Congress had ceased to be the puissant body which won freedom for the country, and that opposition to its policies and programmes was on the increase. For the first time after the dawn of Independence, a no-confidence motion against the Nehru Government was discussed in Parliament and the weaknesses and the lapses of that Government were prominently mentioned. The resignations of Ministers were not, however, the outcome of this heated debate in Parliament. They were due to the plan sponsored by Sri Kamaraj, the central feature of which was the strengthening of the organisational branch of the Congress by drafting into it leading Congressmen from the Parliamentary branch.

 

At the start, the plan had a striking effect. It was unusual for the leading lights of the ruling party in any democratic country to quit office voluntarily with a view to serve the party outside Parliament. But there were indications of dissent in which some of the ex-ministers, notably Sri S. K. Patil, joined. These related to the manner in which the resignations were accepted by the Prime Minister, and it was vaguely hinted that the opportunity was availed of to get rid of particular individuals who had forfeited the Prime Minister’s good opinion. This was an unwarranted accusation against the Prime Minister and it gained no credence in circles noted for their impartiality. A more serious and disturbing feature was the wrangle for power between rival Congress groups in most of the States which had to select new Chief Ministers. In the beginning it was hoped that the choice would be unanimous but even the friendly intervention of the Congress High Command and its special ‘observers’ could not stifle the voice of the dissidents. In most States there is a rift between the Legislative Party and the Pradesh Congress Committee, and within each of these there are groups pulling in different directions and seeking to seize power in both spheres. Even where no Chief Ministers had to be chosen, in Kerala and Andhra Pradesh particularly, these group loyalties have fostered a spirit of chronic hostility, and there are constant confabulations between the High Command at Delhi and the leaders of the rival groups in the various States. All this has caused great mental distress to the Prime Minister and to all those who wish the Congress well and would love to see it restored to its position of the first few years following Independence.

 

It is likely that most of the problems now exercising the minds of leading Congressmen will be solved before the next session of the Congress in Orissa. The choice of Sri Kamaraj as President is calculated to pave the way to greater harmony within the Congress and to a more effective implementation of its programmes in the administrative sphere. The nation as a whole is concerned more with the latter aspect. The leaders of the opposition parties including the Swatantra and the P. S. P. have questioned the wisdom of planning far beyond our means, and of the attitude of non-alignment in the international sphere. There is pronounced hostility in some quarters to legislative measures like the Seventeenth Amendment. A mere strengthening of the Congress can only mean the emergence of an all-powerful single party which can overcome all opposition and wield power for an indefinite period. If Parliamentary democracy is to be a success, it ought to be possible for at least one other party, pledged to the democratic process, to win nation-wide recognition as a possible successor to the Congress. The Socialist pattern, which in actual practice may lead to State Socialism and stifle individual initiative, is not the final word in social and economic growth, and human nature needs wider opportunities for the expression of whatever is in consonance with its higher Being. The element of compulsion and regimentation in diverse fields of national endeavour may rob the nation of the richness and variety which indicate the flowering of the best in the nation. From time to time, and notably at the time of a general election, the citizen must be in a position to make a free choice between the rival programmes of two or more political parties. This will provide the needed corrective to the growth of authoritarianism, which will spell the ruin of democracy.

 

Thus, while the average citizen welcomes the present move to make the Congress more efficient as a party organisation, he would also welcome the rapid growth of other democratic parties with well-defined aims and programmes, so that the political education of the public may not be one-sided, and the leading party may not be tempted to perpetuate its power, brushing aside all opposition as irrelevant or unpatriotic. This tendency has been growing among Congressmen in recent years, and is responsible for some of the evils which have crept into the organisation. Well-organised political parties with a nation-wide following and clear-cut rival programmes are the truest safeguard of democracy.

 

The Sahitya Akademi

 

The Sahitya Akademi at Delhi and similar bodies in some of the States have striven to focus attention on the progress of literature in the various languages of India. Leaving the task of research largely to the Universities, they have encouraged the production of books of high literary quality by offering prizes year after year. In addition, the Central Akademi, in collaboration with the UNESCO, has sponsored a programme of translation of world classics into all the Indian languages. Translations of the outstanding creative work in each language into all other Indian languages, and anthologies of verse and short story have formed an important part of the Akademi’s work. As far as possible, the translations have been made direct from one language into another, and where this has not been possible, the aid of a common language like English or Hindi has been taken. Classics like Plato’s Republic and Machiavelli’s The Prince had to be rendered from the standard English version, because a knowledge of Greek or Italian is not common among our scholars. And yet, the translations of these two treatises on Politics into Telugu are admirable and convey something of the atmosphere of ancient Greece and medieval Italy.

 

Private literary organisations and individual patrons of literature continue to foster the growth of literature in their limited spheres. They are not subject to the rules of procedure governing semi-official institutions like the Akademi. In addition, there are writers’ societies formed on a co-operative basis, helping their members to publish works of merit. Some of the professional Publishing Companies in different parts of India are occasionally impelled to help struggling authors by publishing their books even at the risk of sustaining an initial loss.

 

But all this refers to the business side of literature. It is true today, even as it has been throughout the ages, that the loftiest of poets and seers write or sing in answer to an overpowering inner impulse to express themselves. It is their way of communing with the Divine, and at the moment of creation extraneous considerations of profit or publication are of no account. But all creative work is not of this supreme quality, and any recognition of literary or artistic endeavour is welcome, especially in the case of young authors who have yet to achieve fame. It is in such cases that the Akademi and other organisations will fulfill a need. To seek out talent is one of their prime concerns.

 

Brahmarshi Venkataratnam Naidu

 

The 26th of October marks the centenary of the birth of Sir R. Venkataratnam who, for many decades, held a position in the cultural and religious life of Andhra similar to that of Ram Mohan Roy in Bengal. Somanatila Rao, a poet in Telugu, once compared Viresalingam to Iswarachandra Vidyasagar and Venkataratnam to Ram Mohan. The comparison was widely appreciated and it is remembered whenever a reference is made to the life and work of these two leaders of thought in Andhra. They owed allegiance to the Brahmo Samaj and dedicated themselves to the task of countering the evils which encrusted Hindu society through generations of stagnation. Their reforming zeal communicated itself to the bright and idealistic young men who gathered round them.

 

Among these were some of the great writers and patriots of the land.

 

‘Naidu Garu’, as he was affectionately called, was a scholar of eminence in English and Urdu. He was a devoted student of Shakespeare, and Wordsworth, of Browning and Tennyson. Emerson, Carlyle and Ruskin were among his favourite authors. Literature, to him, was like a life-giving stream, purifying, ennobling, and drawing man into an intimate relationship with the Oversoul. His scholarship was thus a step to sainthood. He was a man of God living perpetually in the Great Presence and allowing that Presence to direct every thought, word and deed of his. All work was worship and an offering to the Divine. In the many sermons he delivered from the altar of the Brahmo Samaj, he poured forth a flood of love and enriched the lives of those who came under his beneficent influence. At Hyderabad, Masulipatam or Kakinada where he taught in important educational institutions, he created a tradition of purity, unselfish service, and abiding love of humanity. The class-room where he taught took on the character of a shrine and the lesson became a prayer and a benediction.

 

The spirit of the Upanishads pervaded his entire being, and throughout the series of volumes entitled “Messages and Ministrations” the vision of the ancient Indian sages acquires a new significance in the light of modern experience of Reality. That a person of his stature and nobility of aim and endeavour should have moulded the careers of innumerable young men and women is a matter for grateful recognition and rejoicing in the India of today.

 

After retiring from the Principalship of the Pithapur Raja’s college, Naidu Garu served for a period as the Vice-Chancellor of Madras University and won the affection and esteem of educationists all over India. He was also a member of the Madras Legislative Council. But it was not office or power that attracted him. His heart was always with the poor and the down-trodden, the widows and the orphans. Several of these were taken care of by him and treated as members of his family. A widower and without children of his own, he bestowed affection on many to whom he was mother as well as father.

 

The Centenary Committee will celebrate the occasion with the veneration due to his memory. Many whom he taught and loved “with an exceeding love”–Mutnuri Krishna Rao, Pattabhi Sitaramayya, Dr. V. Ramakrishna Rao and P. Ramaswami–are no more. But there is one who is fortunately with us still, Krishna Sastri, the poet and dreamer, who has agreed to write a full-length biography of his Guru. The public will look forward with eagerness to what promises to be one the greatest biographies in Telugu, full of devotion and understanding, and in a prose style revealing Sri Sastri’s mastery of the ‘other harmony’.
