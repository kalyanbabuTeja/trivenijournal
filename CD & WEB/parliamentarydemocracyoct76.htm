PARLIAMENTARY DEMOCRACY IN RETROSPECT: CONSTITUTIONAL ASPECTS

 

M. C. CHAGLA

 

            When we drafted our Constitution, we had the choice of adopting the American Presidential system or the British Parliamentary form of Government. We selected the latter for various reasons. Before Independence, we had been working our legislatures both at the Centre and in the States more or less on the British model and so we were accustomed to the practice and conventions of that system. We also felt, and rightly, that the Parliamentary form was the best to give a concrete expression to a Democratic Government. Finally, an elected President as the Head of the Executive was too reminiscent of the kings and emperors, who had ruled us in the past.

 

            Parliamentary form of Government is the most difficult system to work. It has succeeded in very few countries and the trend today definitely is towards a strong Executive, which can control the turbulence and tumult of political life and the tremendous challenges of the modern world.

 

Convention and Standard

 

            The success of the system depends not so much on the written word of the Constitution as to the conventions and the standards of public and political probity.

 

            The Executive power of the Union is vested in the President, and the council of Ministers with the Prime Minister at its head is to aid and advise him in the exercise of his functions. And yet by the convention, which is as well settled and unalterable as the law itself, the President is bound to accept the advice of his Ministers. It will be remembered that Dr Rajendra Prasad challenged this doctrine and questioned his obligation under the Constitution to accept the advice of his Ministers. He wanted to veto the Hindu Reform Bill to which he was opposed as strongly as Nehru was in support of it. A head-on collision between the President and the Prime Minister was averted by the tact and inherent democratic sense of Dr Rajendra Prasad, who bowed to the inevitable and accepted his position as the constitutional Head of Government just as the Royalty in England. In England, too, theoretically there are many royal prerogatives which have all fallen into dissimilitude and which, it is inconceivable, could ever be used again.

 

Role of Opposition

 

            The Constitution has not recognised the Opposition at all and yet without one the functioning of the Parliament would become impossible. An alert and vigilant and critical Opposition is the very essence of Parliamentary Government. The democratic way of life requires that there should be dissent, that the voice; of dissent should be loud and effective and even if incapable of altering the course of events due to the majority having its own way in Parliament, it should be able to influence public opinion which could assert itself at the next election.

 

            In England, the Opposition always provides an alternative Government. It thus makes it difficult for it to be irresponsible or demogogic. For the policies it formulates in Parliament may come home to roost when it assumes power, and is called upon to discharge the promises it had made when sitting on the Opposition benches. Our Parliament has suffered from a lack of such an Opposition. It is fragmented into several parties and rarely speaks with a single united voice.

 

Appointment of Prime Minister

 

            The Prime Minister has to be appointed by the President but here again convention requires that he must select the leader of the largest party in the Lok Sabha. It is more than a convention because the Ministry must command the majority at least in the Lok Sabha, otherwise it would not be in a position to carry through its legislative measures, and more important the administration will not get the finances voted with which to carry on. The Rajya Sabha has no voice in the voting of suppliers as it is the representatives of the people who have the right to impose taxation and not the representatives of the States.

 

            The Prime Minister selects his own colleagues and the Constitution does not lay down either the maximum or the minimum. This has resulted in the unhealthy practice of defection which has besmirched the fair name of our Parliament. The prime Minister or a Chief. Minister if threatened by the loss of his majority can win over a group of dissidents by offering them a Ministership and a Ministership has often been the badge of disloyalty rather than the reward for merit or efficiency.

 

            It has been said that a large majority is often a greater handicap to the leader than a smaller majority. Because in the case of the former, party members are apt to be careless and rebellious knowing that there is no danger of the Government being voted out of office. In the case of the latter when every vote counts, the members have to be on their toes and cannot take anything for granted.

 

Duration of Lok Sabha

 

            The duration of the Lok Sabha is five years unless earlier dissolved. The Constitution vests the power of dissolution with the President but here again convention has laid down that he must act on the advice of the Prime Minister. This right of the Prime Minister is a strong weapon to keep his party under control for no member likes to contemplate the possibility of an early election.

 

            The Constitution provides for an Election Commission for adult suffrage and for the age of voters being not less than 21. The procedure for elections and other details have to be provided by Parliament by law passed for the purpose. We are proud of the way nearly 250 million people go to the polls in every Parliamentary election to elect about 500 members. Foreign observers have paid eloquent tribute to the fair and free manner in which our elections are conducted and the efficient manner in which they are organised over a vast country.

 

Elections

 

            Our real problem about our elections is the expense involved in fighting a large constituency with tens of thousands of voters. In U. K. election is a simple affair. Here it involves a huge expenditure with all its attendant evils of corruption and bribery. Our Constitution makes no provision for limiting the expense or how it can be brought about. It is for Parliament to do so. And unless Parliament does it the whole structure of Parliamentary democracy might collapse ironically enough under the sheer weight of the number of voters and the inability or incapacity of an honest candidate to cope with the problem. What is the solution?

 

            Indirect elections which mean less expenditure and more contact between the voter and the candidate? Or a strict curtailment of the permissible expenses by the candidate and even by his party because the distinction between the two can always be blurred.

 

            There are some people and their number is growing larger who would prefer the Presidential system in India. But so long as we are governed by our present Constitution, Parliamentary system has come to stay. It is for Parliament itself to take a frank and honest look at itself and set its own house in order so that the most populous democracy in the world can function as the finest Parliamentary democracy as well.

 

Respect for Conventions

 

            But looking back upon the last 25 years during which our Parliament has been functioning, we can justly take pride in the fact that notwithstanding the strains and stresses through which we have passed during these years, Parliament has continued to discharge its functions and carry out its duties. The Government of the day has always shown respect for Parliament and even when the Opposition is small and truncated, it has, as far as possible, respected the conventions even when they may be more appropriate to a large and powerful body of critics.

 

Privileges of Parliament

 

            Parliament is rightly jealous of its privileges. The powers, privileges and immunities of Parliament are the same as those of the House of Commons until Parliament chooses to change them. That is the reason why May’s Parliamentary Practice, a well-known text-book on English Parliament, is perhaps the most read, best known book in the library of our Parliament. It has been a matter of constant debate whether we should codify our privileges, etc., rather than be dependent on May’s Parliamentary Practice. It is a well-known experience that when you define something, it usually results in limitation and restriction on the ambit of power. Therefore, in my opinion, the present practice which has on the whole worked well should be allowed to continue.

 

            But jealous as Parliament is about its privileges, it has never claimed them to the detriment of the interest of the citizen or the freedom of the Press which it has always respected.

 

Committees-Watch-dogs of Public Interest

 

            One must also say a word about the important committees of Parliament on which are represented members of all parties and which have acted as watch-dogs of public interest. Personally I should like this committee system to be expanded so that back-benchers should have some useful work to do and be in a position to elicit information from Ministers and Government officials on the day-to-day working of the administration. It is difficult for Parliament as a whole to study and go into all the details. The American Committee System has much to recommend it and should be studied to see to what extent it can be applied here. We can, therefore, proudly claim that while in many other countries, parliamentary system has broken down and dictatorships have been set up, we remain steadfastly loyal to the institution of Parliament, which proves our devotion and dedication to the principle of democracy.
