ROBERT SOUTHEY AND HINDU MYTHOLOGY

 

TANGUTUR PRABHAKAR

 

In comparison with that of his contemporaries Southey’s poetry cannot be said to be popular. Many living authors and know Thalaba, Kehama, Madoc, and Roderick but by name, by universal consent, their author was placed at the head of the literature of England. He is one of those unfortunate authors whose real worth has hardly been recognized in England. But the praise bestowed on him by the great men of his own day, such as Landor, Coleridge and Scott, was really high. Scott declined the laureateship in favour of Southey whom he called ‘my elder brother in the muse’. Coleridge refers to southey in his Biographia Literaria (Chap. 111) and declares that Southey’s works have been characterized each more strikingly than the preceding ‘by a greater splendour, a deeper pathos, profounder reflections, and a more sustained dignity of language and metre’. A poet who enjoyed such high contemporary esteem does merit our reconsideration and reassessment.

 

Southey had always a fascination for mythological lore. At one time he had been greatly impressed by Picart’s Religious Ceremonies and before he left school, he had formed the intention of exhibiting ‘the most remarkable forms of mythology which have at any time obtained among mankind, by making each the groundwork of a narrative poem.’ This juvenile ambition was to in his fabulous inventions such as Madoc, Thalaba and Kehama. While Madoc deals with a subject drawn from the Welsh and Mexican legends, Thalaba and Kehama are based on Mahommedan and Hindu mythologies respectively. We must admire Southey for his extraordinary industry in studying all the available works and collecting the necessary materials for his long narrative poems. The copious notes which he has appended to his poems bear testimony to his indefatigable industry, his extensive research and erudite scholarship.

 

Following the inspiration of his age Southey borrowed for his poems materials from distant lands. The researches of Sri William Jones, and the members of the Asiatic Society in Calcutta, opened the literary treasures of India to the writers of Europe during the last decades of the 18th century. The scholarly activity, relating to India, at this time, had a fruitful impact on the literary minds of England. It was an age of exploration into the poetical traditions of remote ages and distant lands. India afforded a vast magazine of mythological lore not yet thoroughly explored. In the contemporary writings on Indian literature, philosophy and religion the English poets of this time found a new world of thought with which they could enrich their imaginative writings. Southey fell under the spell of the Oriental influence. India made a strong appeal to him and here he found materials for a poem perfectly to his liking. Of all the Orientalists whom Southey consulted, Sir William Jones had been the first to introduce Hindu mythology into English poetry. Jones demonstrated how it could be used in English poetry by writing nine hymns which he addressed to the Hindu gods and goddesses. He proved beyond controversy that gods and goddesses of India, Italy, and Greece, had a common source. Inspired by the example of Jones, Southey made a conscious effort to acquaint himself with the contemporary writings on India. His Curse of Kehama is an example of the impact of the scholarly activity relating to India on an English poet. It is perhaps the only long narrative poem in English literature wholly based on materials drawn, from Hindu mythology. It is unquestionably the best of Southey’s epic poems, for he says in the preface: ‘No poem could have been more deliberately planned or more carefully composed.’ In the Words of Jack Simmons, ‘it stands with Gabir at the head of English Oriental poems of the Romantic period, a very much more distinguished work than Lalla Rookh or The Giaour.’ (Southey, 1945, p. 212) ‘The gorgeous shows of Indian courts and Indian nature,’ writes Dr. Barnett, referring to Kehama in the Dictionary of National Biography, ‘are admirably reproduced in intricate and sonorous stanzas’. The glory of Southey’s genius is its originality. The raw materials of his poem are there; but the imagination that moulded them into beautiful and magnificent shapes is all his own. The Hindu belief which is the foundation of the poem is explained in the preface which reads thus:

 

‘In the religion of the Hindus….there is one remarkable peculiarity. Prayers, penances and sacrifices are supposed to possess an inherent and actual value, in no degree depending upon the disposition or motive of the person who performs them. They are drafts upon Heaven for which the gods cannot refuse payment. The worst men bent upon the worst designs, have in this manner obtained power which has made them formidable to the supreme Deities themselves, and rendered an Avatar, or Incarnation of Vishnu the Preserver, necessary. This belief is the foundation of the following poem….’

 

The story is thus based on the Doctrine of Avatar which means an incarnation of the Deity in the person generally of a human being. Hindus believe that such incarnations of the Deity have been innumerable; but they reckon ten principal Avatars of Vishnu, the Preserving Deity. When disorder is produced and vice is rampant, special interposition of Providence in the affairs of mankind becomes necessary to destroy the wicked and to protect the good. Southey borrowed this article of Hindu faith probably from The Bhagavad Gita which had been first translated into English by Charles Wilkins in 1785. The Avatars were meant for the humiliation of pride and punishment of vice in Asuras or Demons. The nature of the story of each Avatar is roughly the same and has served as the basis for Southey’s poem. In all these the common theme is a fight between Vishnu known as Jove in Western mythology and the giants or titans who are evil powers. The tremendous austerities voluntarily undergone are supposed to give the devotee power even over the elements of nature. The demons who have been elevated to power by means of dissembled piety and bodily austerities grow insolent, tyrannical and cruel and the intervention of God thus becomes necessary.

 

A brief summary of the story in Southey’s poem is as follows: Kehama, a mighty Rajah, has, by a course of austere penances and extraordinary sacrifices, extorted from Siva enormous powers. After acquiring full power over the earth, he continues a series of mysterious sacrifices, the consumation of which would be the conquest of Swarga or Heaven. The devatas anticipate with growing alarm the period of their final subjection to this inexorable aspirant. Arvalan his only son, when trying to violate the virgin Kailyal, is detected by her father Ladurlad who kills him with a stake. Ladurlad is brought before Kehama for punishment and is subjected to a curse. The effect of the curse would be that the elements will lose their power over him and render his life indestructible; but it would be one of torture, devoured by an inward fire. The curse is very curious and does credit to Southey’s power of invention:

 

I charm thy life

From the weapons of strife,

From stone and from wood,

From fire and from flood,

From the serpent’s tooth,

And the beasts of blood: 

From sickness I charm thee,

And Time shall not harm thee;

But Earth which is mine,

Its fruits shall deny thee;

And water shall hear me,

And know thee and fly thee;

And the Winds shall not touch thee

When they pass by thee,

And the Dews shall not wet thee,

When they fall nigh thee:

And thou shalt seek Death

To release thee, in vain;

Thou shalt live in thy pain,

While Kehama shall reign,

With a fire in thy heart;

And a fire in thy brain;

And sleep shall obey me,

And visit thee never,

And the Curse shall be on thee

For ever and ever. (II, 14.)

 

Thus cursed, yet protected, Ladurlad becomes the means of frustating the intentions of Kehama. The miraculous nature of the curse affords scope of no common kind to poetic imagination. The man whose life is secure from weapons may defy armies; he who is safe from fire and flood may plunge into the consuming element at his pleasure; he may explore the depths of the ocean itself and perform exploits beneath the waves since they know him and fly him. The power thus bestowed enables Ladurlad to save his daughter from death on many occasions. The ghost of Avalan incessantly haunts her and what it is unable to perform, it seeks to execute by the help of a horrible witch named Lorrinite. Ereenia, an immortal Gandharva, becomes the protector of Kailyal and counteracts the spells of her evil persecutors. The conflict between these powers is carried on with various success; but eventually Arvalan and the witch are defeated. Meanwhile Kehama completes his hundredth Aswamedha Yaga, which gives him the sovereignty of Swarga and subsequently of Pathala, the Hindu Hell. Here he obtains the cup of Amrita, a divine liquor which will confer on him immortality. He then offers his hand and throne to Kailyal, but is rejected. He presumes to be equal to Siva, the supreme Deity himself and drinks the liquor. At this point, however, he has reached his allowed limit. Siva, the God of Gods, opens his eye of anger on the accursed Rajah. The liquor confers immortality on Kehama, but it is one of torment, and he is reduced to one of the supporters of the throne of Yama, the god of Pathala. Kailyal also drinks from the cup. But in virtue of her chastity, she becomes purified from all the grossness of mortal flesh and is transported to Swarga where she enjoys an eternal happiness with Ereenia.

 

Soon after its publication in 1811, the poem was reviewed in many contemporary literary periodicals. It was condemned by many critics for its wild and extravagant nature. Unfortunately the Hindu mythology was not well known to the people of England at that time. So they could not appreciate the esoteric values of the poem. Every critic discovered that the poem displays to a greater degree than Southey’s former works, the highest poetical genius with which he was unquestionably gifted. Southey takes for the theatre of his action no less a field than the entire universe: and in this he has followed Milton. We are transported at the will of the bard from the surface of the earth to the bower of bliss in Heaven and from heaven down to the region of flames in Hell. We dive with him below the waves of the sea and breathe in mid-air. We are startled at his self-sailing ship and self-balancing car. The poem contains a number of exquisitely beautiful passages. A reader will feel that he has never seen more perfect instances of images startlingly alive through the diction. The scenery is splendid and the diction is copious and eloquent. The author’s force of imagination and power of expression are manifest throughout the poem. It opens with a highly wrought, poetical, and startling description of a midnight funeral–the funeral of Arvalan, the son of Kehama;

 

Midnight, and yet no eye

Through all the Imperial City closed in sleep!

Behold her streets a-blaze

With light that seems to kindle the red sky,

Her myriads swarming through the crowded ways!

Master and slave, old age and infancy,

All, all abroad to gaze;

House-top and balcony

Clustered with women, who throw back their veils

With unimpeded and insatiate sight

To view the funeral pomp which passes by,

As if the mournful rite

Were but to them a scene of joyance and delight.

 

The ornaments, the landscape, the animals, the similes and the sentiments are all Indian. The ornaments of a Hindu lady are recalled in the following description of the unadorned beauty of the heroine Kailyal:

 

No idle ornaments deface

Her natural grace;

Musk-spot, nor sandal-streak, nor scarlet stain,

Ear-drop nor chain, nor arm nor ankle-ring,

Nor trinketry on front, or neck; or breast,

Marring the perfect form: she seem’d a thing

Of Heaven’s prime uncorrupted work; a child

Of early nature undefiled,

A daughter of the years of innocence.

–(XIII, 13)

 

Southey’s poetic art is prodigiously displayed in the following description of the Hindu Avatars:

 

For often would Ereenia tell

Of what in elder days befell

When other tyrants in their might,

Usurp’d dominion o’er the earth;

And Vishnu took a human birth,

Deliverer of the Sons of men,

And slew the huge Hiranyaksha,

And piece-meal rent, with lion force,

Hiranyakasipu’s accursed corse,

And humbled Baly in his pride;

And when the Giant Ravanan

Had borne triumphant from his side

Sita, the earth-born God’s beloved bride,

Then from his island-kingdom laugh’d to scorn

The insulted husband, and his power defied;

How to revenge the wrong in wrath he tried,

Bridging the sea before his dreadful way,

And met the hundred-headed foe,

And dealt him the unerring blow;

By Brahma’s hand the righteous lance was given,

And by that arm immortal driven,

It laid the mighty tyrant low;

And earth and Ocean, and high Heaven,

Rejoiced to see his overthrow.

Oh! doubt not thou, Yedillian cried,

Such fate Kehama will betide;

For there are Gods who look below,

Siva the Avenger, is not blind,

Nor Vishnu careless for mankind.

–(X, 13)

 

The poet’s description of Padalon or Hindu Hell which was highly admired by Scott, stands comparison with Milton’s Pandemonium:

 

The strong foundations of this inmost Earth

Rest upon Padalon

 

Eight gates hath Padalon; eight heavenly Powers

Have them in charge, each always at his post,

Lest from their penal caves the accursed host,

Maugre the might of Baly and the God,

Should break, and carry ruin all abroad.

Those gates stand open, night and day,

And souls of mortal men

For ever throng the way.

Some from the dolorous den,

Children of sin and wrath, return no more:

They, fit companions of the spirits accursed,

Are doom’d, like them in baths of fire immerst,

Or weltering upon beds of molten ore,

Or stretch’d upon the brazen floor,

Are fasten’d down with adamantine chains;

While on their substance inconsumable,

Leeches of fire for ever hang and pull,

And Worms of fire for ever gnaw their food,

That still renew’d

Freshens for ever their perpetual pains.

–( XXII, 1, 2)

 

But, oh! What sounds and sights of woe,

What sights and sounds of fear,

Assail the mortal travellers here!

 

Thick walls of adamant divide

The dungeons; and from yonder circling flood,

Off-streams of fire through secret channels glide,

And wind among them, and in each provide

An everlasting food

Of rightful torments for the accursed brood.

Loud around,

In mingled sound, the echoing lash, the clash

Of chains, the powerful hammer’s iron stroke,

With execrations, groans, and shrieks and cries

Combined, in one wild dissonance, arise;

And through the din there broke,

Like thunder heard through all the warring winds,

The dreadful name.

–(XXIII, 4, 7.)

 

The vehicle called the Ship of Heaven in which Ereenia carries Kailyal to Swarga has been hailed by many critics as a happy novelty. Southey borrowed the idea of a flying car from Jones’s translation of Sakuntala. Shelley and Keats probably influenced by Southey employed similar poetic devices in their poems Queen Mab and Endymion respectively. Southey with a judicious boldness has adapted his vehicle more to the nature of the element:

 

Then in the ship of Heaven, Ereenia laid

The waking, wondering Maid;

The ship of Heaven, instinct with thought, display’d

Its living sail, and glides along the sky.

On either side in wavy tide,

The clouds of morn along its path divide;

The winds who swept in wild career on high,

Before its presence check their charmed force;

The winds that loitering lagg’d along their course,

Around the living Bark enamour’d play,

Swell underneath the sail, and sing before its way.

(VII, I)

 

Southey’s collection of material from various sources is so rich and elaborate that he incorporates into his poem even the minute mythological details. The conflict for pre-eminence between Brahma and Vishnu, a popular legend with the Hindus, is described briefly in a few lines:

 

……..for when in days of yore,

For proud pre-eminence of power

Brahma and Vishnu, wild and rage, contended,

And Siva in his might,

Their dread contention ended;

Before their sight

In form a fiery column did he tower,

Whose head above the highest height extended,

Whose base below the deepest depth descended.

Downward, its path to sound

Vishnu a thousand years explored

The fathomless profound,

And yet no base he found:

Upward, to reach its head,

Ten myriad years the aspiring Brahma soar’d,

And still as up he fled,

Above him still the Immeasurable spread.

The rivals own’d their lord,

And trembled and adored.

(XIX, 5)

 

The story of Baly whose pride Vishnu, in his fifth Incarnation as a Brahmin dwarf checked, is described with beautiful and exquisite workmanship:

 

………he whose name

In ancient story and in poet’s praise,

Liveth and Bourisheth for endless glory,

Because his might

Put down the wrong, and aye upheld the right.

Till for ambition, as old sages tell,

At length the universal Monarch fell,

For he too, having made the world his own,

Then in his pride, had driven

The Devatas from Heaven,

And seized triumphantly the Swarga throne.

The Incarnate came before the Mighty One,

In dwarfish stature, and in mien obscure;

The sacred cord he bore,

And ask’d, for Brahmin’s sake, a little boon,

Three steps of Baly’s ample reign, no more.

Poor was the boon required, and poor was he

Who begged….a little wretch it seem’d to be:

But Baly ne’er refused a suppliant’s prayer.

He on the Dwarf cast down,

A glance of pity in contemptuous mood,

And bade him take the boon,

And measure where he would.

Lo! Son of giant birth,

I take my grant! the Incarnate Power replies.

With his first step he measured o’er the Earth,

The second spann’d the skies.

Three paces thou hast granted,

Twice have I set my footstep, Vishnu cries,

Where shall the third be planted?

Then Baly knew the God, and at his feet,

In homage due, he laid his humbled head.

Mighty art thou, O Lord of Earth and Heaven,

Mighty art thou! he said,

Be merciful, and let me be forgiven.

He ask’d for mercy of the Merciful,

And mercy for his virtue’s sake was shown.

For though he was cast down Padalon,

Yet there, by Yamen’s throne,

Doth Baly sit in majesty and might,

To judge the dead, and sentence them aright.

(XV, 4, 5, 6)

 

The scope for the range of Southey’s fancy is almost unbounded in the description of the ‘gem-lighted’ submarine city of Mahabalipuram. It was Landor’s favourite picture.

 

Their golden summits in the noon-day light,

Shone o’er the dark green deep that roll’d between;

For domes, and pinnacles, and spires were seen

Peering above the sea, a mournful sight!

Well might the sad beholder ween from thence,

What works of wonder the devouring wave

Had swallowed there, when monuments so brave

Bore record of their old magnificence.

And on the sandy shore, beside the verge

Of ocean, here and there, a rock-hewn fane

Registered in its strength the surf and surge

That on their deep foundations bent in vain.

In solitude the Ancient temple stood,

Once resonant with instrument and song,

And solemn dance of festive multitude;

Now as the weary ages pass along,

Hearing no voice save of the ocean flood,

Which roars for ever on the restless shores;

Or visiting their solitary caves,

The lonely sound of winds that moan around

Accordant to the melancholy waves.

–(XV, 8)

 

The story is obviously an allegory embodying a moral truth. It portrays a struggle between the powers of Good and the powers of Evil and the triumph of the former over the latter. Kehama, his son Arvalan and the wicked sorceress Lorrinite symbolise evil. Kehama, with his enormous power is a modern Ravana or Hiranyakasipa. He is the personification of cruelty, ambition, arrogance, tyranny and lust. He resembles Milton’s Satan in that he challenges the very powers of God. As Satan is reduced to a hissing serpent, so Kehama, as a result of his pride and ambition, is doomed to eternal suffering under the throne of Yama. His son Arvalan embodies lust and revenge and rightly deserves a punishment of torture in the Hell. The heroine Kailyal, her father Laudurlad and Ereenia form the other group representing the sterner virtues of fortitude, faith and disinterestedness. Kailyal ranks with Cordelia, Desdemona, Una and a host of other ideal female characters. She was called by Shelley ‘the divine Kailyal.’ She is an embodiment of filial affection, religious faith and feminine chastity. In purity of character she resembles Sita in The Ramayana. Ravana’s attempts to persuade to marry him were met with utter contempt. So Kehama’s persecutions fail to induce Kailyal to marry him. Kehama’s sovereignty over the three worlds and his super-natural power have little effect on Kailyal. She is always lovely and magnanimous. Her purity, simplicity and devotion sanctify her in our fancy. In her sufferings she could derive but little assistance from the gods. It is her moral character which baffles the ambitious purposes of Kehama. His wickedness and persecutions serve only to increase her patience and piety. The poet whose heart is always true to moral feeling, has taken care to emphasize the divine qualities of this injured lady whose virtue, like that of Spenser’s Una, is described as subjugating brute ferocity:

 

A charm was on the Leopard when he came

Within the circle of that mystic glade;

Submiss he crouch’d before the heavenly maid,

And offered to her touch his speckled side;

 

On comes the Elephant, to slake

His thirst at noon in yon pellucid springs.

 

But when that form of beauty meets his sight,

The trunk its undulating motion stops,

From his forgetful hold the plane-branch drops,

Reverent he kneels, and lifts his rational eyes

To her as if in prayer;

 

Lo! as the voice melodious floats around

The Antelope draws near,

The tigress leaves her toothless cubs to hear;

The snake comes gliding from the secret brake

Himself in fascination forced along

By that enchanting song;

(XIII, 10, 11, 12)

 

Ladurlad defies the almighty Rajah with his undaunted courage unshakable faith. The fortitude of the two sufferers becomes more and more consolidated and is finely represented. Faith moves mountains and it is faith in these suffering wretches that finally proves to be the most potent virtue:

 

O force of faith! O strength of virtuous will!

Behold him in his endless martyrdom,

Triumphant still!

The Curse still burning in his heart and brain,

And yet doth he remain

Patient the while, and tranquil, and content!

(XIII, 9)

 

Faith was their comfort, Faith their stay;

They trusted woe would pass away,

And tyranny would sink subdued,

And Evil yield to Good.

(X, 14)

 

Ereenia, with his graceful form, wings and movements, stands for justice and saves the persecuted father and daughter many a time. He embodies the spirit of service and disinterestedness. He is a true Gandharva or heavenly angel.

 

The poem with its profound moral spirit,–a quality very common in Hindu epic literature–did not fail to attract the attention of the younger poets of the period. To Shelley, for example, the poem has been a source of inspiration and its influence is discernible in all his important poems.
