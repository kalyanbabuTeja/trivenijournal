DEMOCRACY AT WORK IN INDIA

 

N. V. GADGIL

 

“Parliamentary institutions have not had a very good time in Asia”, wrote the Manchester Guardian in 1954. “All that is happening of the kind, which is working in an exemplary way....Pericles said that Athens was the school of Hellas. Mr. Nehru without boasting may say that Delhi is the school of Asia”. Much water has flown down the Jamuna since this was written. Nearly nine years have passed and two general elections have been held since then. Do the events that have taken place since then justify this confident language? It is the purpose of this article to find out whether Delhi has really become the school of Asia.

 

The democracy that our Constitution contemplates is nothing if not parliamentary. The system of government that we have adopted, at the Centre as well as in the States, has as its basis the British Parliamentary system of government. British Parliamentarianism in India is a translation and it is evident that in even the best translations the essential meaning is apt to be lost. It is commonplace knowledge that democracy is a government by discussion and parliamentary democracy presupposes freedom of speech and the spirit of tolerance. It follows that such a system works only with certain political assumptions and constitutional habits. The theory and practice of the British system shows that it rests on four foundations, viz., the existence of well organised national political parties; (ii) the principle of majority and the willingness of the minority to accept the decision of majority at least for the time being; (iii) free and fearless Press reflecting the true public opinion in the country; and (iv) a large independent floating vote uncommitted and owing no permanent allegiance to any one of the parties.

 

No Real Party System: The three general elections held so far undoubtedly fought by national political parties. It cannot, however be said that India has developed a real party system. Except perhaps the Congress and the Communist, none of the parties in India are well-organised parties having roots in the people. They have neither the resources nor the strength of a British Political Party. Even the Congress has lost its spirit of mission and the sense of direction. The average person in power in the Congress today is a careerist aspiring for still better patronage. Democracy within the party is on the decline. Hero-worship is the order of the day. The Congress has become a huge whispering gallery of persons mechanically echoing the current opinions of the High Command. Criticism is equated with disloyalty and indiscipline. Inevitably it is being uprooted from the masses.

 

The P. S. P. is a party by courtesy and an organisation by sufferance. It has always been a party with confused ideology and woolly programme, few followers and no leadership. Ashoka Mehta once said “Ours is not a party; ours is a family. If there is one family which requires family planning, it is this family. The P. S. P. never had a past and it is without any future. It is fast losing its hold and in a few years it will be no more.

 

The Jan Sangh is an abbreviation of the R. S. S. It is a party with an organisation but with very little else. The Communist party is undoubtedly a well-organised party with an ideology of its own. But it does not and cannot, by the very tenets of its ideology, believe in parliamentary democracy. Like the Hindus of ancient India the Communists have four Vedas represented by the writings of Marx, Stalin, Lenin and Mao. None of them have accepted the parliamentary democracy. Indeed the very title of a chapter in Lenin’s collected works is abolition of parliamentarism. The Indian Communists are no better. In a speech made on 4th of September 1952 in the Rajya Sabha Mr. Sundarayya made it clear that his party has accepted our Constitution itself only as a matter of tactics.

 

The Socialist party is only in a slightly better position. Dr. Lohia has stated in unmistakable terms that they have more  faith in extra-constitutional and non-parliamentary methods such as strikes, morchas and Satyagraha. It is a party which is both irresponsible and inconsistent. It has disobeyed the ruling of the Chair in the Parliament as well as in the State Assemblies. Its idea of militancy is that its members should be carried out of the Legislative Chamber on the shoulders of the Marshal. If the Socialist party seriously proposes to persist in this as a matter of policy, they will have to select before long a wrestler of the calibre of a Kaiser-i-Hind as their next candidate for the Parliament. Dr. Lohia started with theories of equi-distance, equal irrelevance and equal indifference and has now ended with joining in an unholy alliance at least with the Jan Sangh. Like the animals in the ‘Animal Farm’ of George Orwell, the Socialist party has now come to inhabit a world in which both the Congress and the Communist are equi-distant, but occasionally one is more distant than the other.

 

Opposition and the Congress: In the ultimate analysis, the system that we have adopted cannot succeed with such political habits. If political parties throw their ideologies to the wind to secure a few seats in the Parliament or if they resort to mass Satyagraha and general strikes, morchas and hunger strikes to achieve political ends, the Parliament will cease to be the centre of political gravity and the minority will get the decisions of the majority changed by more effective extra-constitutional methods for which there is another word in the dictionary–coup-de-tat. A responsible opposition cannot, however, exist without a responsive ruling party. The record of the Congress in this respect is far from being clean. The Congress has, more often than not, conceded the legitimate and just demands only after firing and bloodshed. Power has corrupted the Congress and the tendency, too often, is to disregard and discard the opposition. The inclination always is to equate the Government with the Congress. Hardly a debate in the Parliament has changed the nature of policy or the course of the events. In England the Norvic Debate displaced Chamberlain. The O’Brien affair substantially altered a pristine legislation. The Middle-Eastern policy of the Labour Government was changed by a debate in the House. Many a controversial bill has been withdrawn in deference to the insistence of the opposition. No such instance has occurred in this country. There is not a single occasion when a legislative measure has been so withdrawn in the Lok Sabha. It follows that unless the Congress party responds to the reasonable demands of the opposition in Parliament, the minority will be driven to join issues it in the streets.

 

Role of the Press: The Indian Press has a great tradition behind it. It is also growing fast. The fifth report of the Registrar of Newspapers shows that there are 8,026 newspapers and periodicals in existence today as against 7,651 in 1959. But it is also true that it is becoming a monopoly Press. In this economics of newspapers, freedom of Press and speech becomes meaningless. The Indian Press has been described as the “Jute Press”. It is also a Juke-box Press. The man who puts the coin calls the tune. The public opinion that it claims to reflect is frequently a public opinion manufactured by itself to suit the needs of certain vested interests. Such a Press can hardly be said to be a true guardian of democracy in this country.

 

Declining Standards of Judiciary: The other sentinel of democracy is the judiciary. It is common knowledge that standards of judiciary are fast declining. The Law Commission has revealed that appointments to the highest courts on the basis of regional, communal and political considerations are not unknown. After all a Judge’s mind is not a legal slot-machine. If he owes his appointment to such considerations, they are bound to be reflected in the decisions that he gives. Add to this the dangerous tendency discernible in the speeches of the highest in the land to ignore and ridicule the judiciary. In such a state of affairs, the citizen is likely to lose his faith in the judiciary and he will cease to look up to the courts for the protection of democratic values.

 

Political Consciousness: Political parties and leadership in this country have not lived up to our expectations. But there is one silver lining. The Indian people have matured remarkably. The three general elections show that the average illiterate Indian has become politically conscious. He does not regard parliamentary democracy as un-Indian nor does he pessimistically assume that the Parliament is only a facade meant to conceal an authoritarian dictatorship. The real question for India, therefore, is not whether democracy will work successfully, for she has no alternative. What we must ask is how quickly the leadership and the intelligentsia will create those political conditions and constitutional habits without which it is bound to fail.
