A GARLAND TO M. S. SUBBULAKSHMI

(A TRIBUTE ON HER SIXTIETH BIRTHDAY)

 

PROF. G. V. L. N. SARMA

 

Mrs. M. S. Subbalakshmi has been sixty springs. She has scattered around her the fragrance of all roses sixty Indian springs in celestial showers of melody. Through innumerable concerts, recorded music of haunting beauty, generous acts of charity, which even princes could not emulate, and through loyalty to lofty Indian traditions, she won not only the hearts of her countrymen but also discriminating music lovers in Europe and America. Her pious and inspiring life is indeed a glorious adoration to the Deity; her art was a transmutation of it, and, today she stands assured of fame among the supreme artists of the world.

 

            Subbalakshmi is the illustrious daughter of the celebrated veena maestro, Madurai Shanmugavadivu. Her creative genius became manifest at an early age. Kunja, as Subbulakshmi was lovingly called at home, attracted the attention of her mother with her golden voice when she was just eight years old. The mother heard, or overheard, Kunja singing a Marathi song Ananda ja which the child picked up casually. A few days later Shanugavadivu gave a veena concert in the Raja Setupati High School in Madurai. Kunja was left playing outside. Suddenly the sponsors of the music programme took her inside to the dais and asked her to sing Ananda ja. The audience was pleasantly surprised by the sweet soft notes of the child. At the age of ten Kunja’s first song was cut on the gramophone disc. By the time she was seventeen she was a bright jewel of Karnataka Sangita. At that age she gave a concert at the Madras Music Academy, which was reputed for inviting seasoned vocalists only for polyphonic expression. Subbulakshmi’s first concert at the Music Academy was the first mark of public recognition of her musical genius.

 

            Subbulakshmi achieved country-wide acclamation after her concert at the All-India Music anti Dance Festival in Bombay in 1944. Pandit Omkarnath, the famous adept at Hindustani Music, was spellbound when he heard her rendering of Sankarabharana tune and ran to the dais to congratulate the divinely-inspired musician after her concert. Hers was the golden full-throated harmonious Sruti-suddha blending of bhava and raga, swara and sahitya.

 

            There have been wonderful musicians from mediaeval times, from Tansen to Pattammal, whose singing, perhaps, ‘hath charms to soothe a savage breast or to soften rocks.’ Subbulakshmi sur­passes them all in one respect. Music for her is a Sadhana and an aradhana. She does not sing to the sensual ear; she pipes spirit ditties. Each of her bhajans is a shower of ananda. To listen to her music is to meditate, to transform one’s inner weather. It appeals most to people who have reverence for life. Mahatma Gandhi cherished her bhajans and listened to those soul-thrilling raptures whenever he had an opportunity. On Oct. 2, 1947, he wanted to invite her to Delhi to listen to her devotional songs. Subbulakshmi could not leave Madras. So she got some bhajans recorded on tape, which she sent for being played before Gandhiji. On hearing the bhajans a pleasant idea struck him. He wanted to hear his famous Mira song ‘Hari Tum Haro’ from her lips. When Srimati Sucheta KripaJani communicated from Delhi Gandhiji’s wish to Subbulakhmi, the latter modestly sent word to Sucheta that she did not know the bhajan and that it should be recited by some other musician at Delhi. Gandhiji said that none other than Subbulakshmi could render the uplifting supplication to Hari in that immortal Mira bhajan. In deference to his wishes, she sang the song on the night of Oct. 1, and it was broadcasted from Madras the following morning. Gandhiji was overjoyed when he saw Mira’s agony and ecstasy glowing afresh in Subbulakshmi’s haunting music. Who is not moved by the rich tone and the crescendo of emotion when Subbulakshmi repeats the hallowed word Hari in the bhajan? A few months later, on the fateful evening of January 30, when Subbulakshmi heard the news of Gandhiji’s assassination, followed by her song, ‘Hari Turn Haro,’ she fainted in tears through intense emotion. For several years she could not sing that song.

 

            Subbulakshmi gave her concerts in many Indian cities and towns. Besides, she touched every pious home in towns and villages, where her concerts were not held, through her recorded music, particularly Sri Venkateswara Suprabhatam, Vishnu Sahasranamam, the songs of the divine Thyagarajaswami, and the hymns of Sri Guru Nanak Dev.

 

            It is gratifying that she took her Ganges of melody to the West too. Lord Harewood, who heard her music in Madras with great pleasure, invited her to participate in the International Music Festival at Edinburgh in 1943, when he became its Director. Her splendid performance at the Festival was praised by Western music critics, particularly the London Press. Later that year, she held a series of concerts in many European cities, and became one of the best exponents of Indian culture. Three years later, Subbu­lakshmi gave concerts in American cities. It was a new cultural conquest of a new audience. The music critic of the New York Times wrote: “Subbulakshmi and her ensemble are a revelation to Western ears. Their return can be awaited only with eagerness.”

 

            Subbulakshmi won many coveted laurels. Our Government conferred the titles of ‘Padma Bhushan’ and ‘Padma Vibhushan’ upon her. The Madras Music Academy honoured itself by honouring her with the title ‘Sangita Kalanidhi.’ Sri Thyagarajaswami Music Committee of Tirupati awarded her the title ‘Saptagiri Sangita Vidwanmani.’ Everybody knows that Subbulakshmi is the laureate of music in Lord Venkateswara’s Temple. A couple of years ago she was awarded the Magsaysay Award, which highlights her philanthropy. As soon as she received the award (Rs. 80,000), she donated it to three institutions which have been languishing for financial help – Bharatiya Vidya Bhavan, Voluntary Health Services in Madras and Vedic School in Kumbakonam.

 

            Subbulakshmi generously used her divine gift in philanthropy. At Sri Rajaji’s behest, she gave benefit recitals to Kasturba Fund in 1943.            She gave many concerts to raise funds for schools, hospitals, temple renovation and memorials to national leaders. For a time she entered the film field to raise funds. In 1941 she acted in the Tamil film Savitri for the purpose of raising funds for the magazine Kalki. In 1944 she was cast in the role of Mira in Tamil film which was later retaken in Hindi. Sarojini Naidu, known as the nightingale of India, declared, after seeing the Hindi film, that India should be proud of the supreme artist. While she left the theatre where she saw the film, Sir T. Vijayaraghavachariar quipped, “Well, Mrs Naidu, will you now surrender your title (nightingale of India)?” Sarojini quickly answered, “I did it long ago.”

 

            What strikes one in Subbulakshmi, apart from her divine music, are – her respect for her husband, Sri T. Sadasivam, whom she married in 1924, and her humility. Sri Sadasivam was not only her spouse, but also her Guru and Daivam. It was Sri Sadasivam who advised her to make her music God-oriented.

 

            What Ernest Newman, the great music critic, said of Beethoven, the great German composer, applies equally well to Subbulakshmi, the queen of music: “Again and again she lifts us to a height from which we revaluate not only all music, but all life, all emotion and all thought.”
