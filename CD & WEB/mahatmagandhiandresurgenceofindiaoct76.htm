MAHATMA GANDHI AND RESURGENCE OF INDIA

 

DR G. SRIRAMA MURTY

 

            Pandit Jawaharlal Nehru has aptly compared the coming of Gandhi to that of ‘a whirl-wind that upset many things, but most of all the working of people’s minds.’1 He was indeed a force to reckon with. There is no department of national life which does not bear his impress and signeur. For nearly half a century, the entire country has been under the spell of Gandhi. During the elections his name has been a magic shibboleth for opening the heart-gates of the voting populace. Nehru and Sastry spoke the language of Gandhi and tried to tread close to his footprints. But gradually, there came a change in the temper of the times. Though lip sympathy has been shown to him, still, though still his birth anniversary has been observed with ritualistic fervour, every one knows today Gandhi is but an empty name that had lost its charm. The age of scientific socialism that came after him has no use for him.

 

            The decline in the interest of Gandhian ideology was followed by an all round decline in national life. The disruptive forces of casteism and communalism, of corruption and nepotism that were held on leash by Gandhian leadership of ascetism and self-denial lifted up their ugly heads again and made the country a bear garden. Violence had become the order of the day and the authority of the Government was flouted with impunity by all and sundry. Schools and colleges were closed; and nation’s youth were on roads, veritable sons of Baliol, Naturally amidst this clamour and confusion, men’s minds turned to Gandhi once again in the hope of finding a penacea there. Some of the recent critical appraisals of India wondered whether Gandhi is really the Father of the Nation, whether he had contributed substantially to the resurgence of India.

 

            In a challenging book on contemporary Indian situation, Hindu Ethos and the Challenge of Change, its author, Subhayu Dasgupta, had written a few words regarding the role of Gandhi in shaping new India which we may take as fairly representative of the contem­porary attitude to Gandhi. He writes:

 

            “I refrained from including Gandhi into the same category with Tagore, Nehru or Jayaprakash Narayan because Gandhi in many ways was to me, much more a man of tradition than of renaissance, much more a man of the past than of the future”.2

 

            He laughs at Gandhi’s trusteeship idea and wonders why the Government has been spending colossal amounts on Khadi and village industries. The sooner India sets over this fond sentiment­alism the better for its future. We can understand his virulent attack on Gandhian economics but we fail to understand when he says that Gandhi does not even favourably compare with Viveka­nanda whose mantle we know had fallen on him.

 

            Gandhi has been mostly misunderstood because many of his critics believe that he is no more than a bringer of freedom to India, who had enjoyed unprecedented popularity with masses because of his simple living and absolute sincerity of word and deed. What they miss to note is that to the Mahatma national liberation is but a step forward in the direction of greater liberation or Moksha of Man. His ideal is a nation of liberated souls, though it sounds utopian. He himself said that he was essentially a man of religion strayed into politics. It follows from this that his religious outlook defined his position as a politician, economist and social thinker. Since religion and culture go together and any particular culture is but an expression of its faith, it emerges that if we want to understand Gandhi’s contribution to modern India, it must be sought in the field of cultural renaissance of India. All other aspects of Indian life subsume in the cultural stream of India.

 

            The cultural foundations of India are to be found in the Vedas, the Upanishads, the Ramayana, the Mahabharata and the Bhagavad Gita. Gandhi’s basic thought is moulded by these books and the influential saints like Tulsidas, Kabirdas and Mirabai who mirrored the living religious thought of Hindu scriptures in their lives. Gandhi seems to rely more on the living tradition of Hindu saints than on the abstract, difficult to understand philosophies of the learned Acharyas like Sankara, Ramanuja and Madhva. An exponent of the prestine Hindu thought, free from the later day accretions of rigid casteism, vulgar superstitions, cant and hypocrisy, he established Truth as living God and recognised nothing else beside it. When a correspondent accused him of having confused truth and non-violence with the Hindu creed, he rebutted the charge by saying,

 

            “The crime is deliberate. It is the good fortune or misfortune of Hinduism that it has no official creed. If I were asked to define the Hindu creed, I should simply say: search after truth through non-violent means. A man may not believe even in God and still call himself a Hindu. Hinduism is a relentless pursuit after Truth”.3

 

            This is the master light of all his seeing. All his social, economic and political thought, his relentless opposition to the oppression of the downtrodden, his ceaseless fight against com­munalism, colonialism and exploitation of every description spring from this focal point. He arrived at this point not merely by following the Hindu thought and tradition but by sifting it with the rational approach he imbibed from the West. He was deeply influenced by writers like Ruskin, Thoreau and Tolstoy. Buddhism, Jainism, Islam and Christianity too coloured his life, thought and outlook on life.

 

            His Hinduism, then, is not a legacy of the middle ages which petrified the soul of India, necessitated a renaissance and precipi­tated reformation in the nineteenth century. The tradition to which he clung ardently is no soul-freezing caste-ridden religious­ tradition perpetrated by the purblind pontiffs of hallowed religious institutions. On the other hand, it is a revived spiritual tradition re-interpreted in the light of the needs of the changing society. His place in the cultural renaissance of India is similar to that of Vivekananda. As Dr Kai Nicholson observes “the thought of the Swamy found expression through the deeds of Gandhi”. 4 Gandhi like Vivekananda believed that it is through spirituality alone that we can become a nation.

 

            This brings us to another important contribution of Gandhi to the movement of national renaissance. By his re-discovery of the ancient Hindu tradition and by re-interpreting some of the fundamental tenets of Hindu thought such as Karma, Dharma, Avatar and caste he blazed a new trail of thought that might be called a new philosophy of history. His interpretation of Gita is a classic by itself. In Gita he found a sanction for the disinterested action. By preaching and practising the gospel of anasakti yoga, he not only prepared the nation to rock the foundations of the British Empire in India but also tried to rebuild the Hindu society on the firm foundations of duty, charity and control. By accepting a teleological view of history and by advocating purposive living as human ideal, he had been able to give a spiritual meaning and content to the historical process and human destiny. An inveterate believer in the inevitable progress of man and perfectabilhy of human nature, he redeemed the traditional Hindu view of life of its latent pessimism and created a new outlook on life. By placing a heavy accent on the role of the individual in society and at the same time highlighting the responsibility of the community in giving opportunities to the individual to live his destiny, he paved the way of conflictless society. His untiring advocacy of communal harmony in the place of class conflict and his posing “Sarvodaya” as an alternative to scientific socialism opened a new vision of history. He recognized the importance of economic forces in shaping the history of mankind but refused to accept Marxist philosophy as valid. “While he appeared to agree with the Marxian analysis that man’s commerce with matter, his economic life, his modes of production and distribution of economic goods influence, politics and ethics and generally the social life of community, he refused to believe that there was a necessary co-rela­tion between the construction of a new social order and the distinc­tion of the old one or that the ‘economic’ was the only factor which mattered”.5 The new view of history is best summarized by B. G. Gokhale as “a two-dimensional unfolding of the inter­action between man and the supreme spirit.”

 

            Inspired by the Hindu concept of circular time, Gandhi rejects the idea of linear and continuous progress advocated by the western historians. It is unfair to call him a reactionary who wants to set the clock back on that score. Once we understand his spiritual interpretation of history based on Hindu religious thought, the criticism seems to be wide of mark. A man of tradi­tion he is also a man of renaissance because in his hands tradition itself acquired a new significance and donned the dress of modern­ity. Perhaps he is more a man of renaissance than a man of tradition since his definite contribution is towards renaissance. We close this section with an observation made by A. M. Mundadan in his paper Hindu and Christian view of life regarding the contribu­tion of Gandhi and others to the renaissance of India. He writes:

 

            “As Samantha states, there are indications that a more positive view of history is being emerged in modern India. To substantiate this observation he calls attention to three major themes in modern Hindu thought represented by such religio-philosophical thinkers of modern Hinduism as Tagore, Gandhi, Aurobindo and Radhakrishnan, who are not merely the products of modern Hindu renaissance but also men who, in some measure, made a lasting contribution to its character and movements”.6

 

            The gravamen of Gandhi criticism, however, is directed against his political economy. This determined opposition to industry and excessive stress on self-sufficient village community and the idea of trusteeship, though all apiece with his central doctrine of truth and non-violence, appear to be all moonshine, especially in the context of ever-growing technocracy of the ever-shrinking world of the supersonic age. It is, however, well to remember two things in this connection. One, his economics of Swadeshi and opposition to machinery are but logical corollaries of his first principles–­truth and non-violence; two, he is grossly misrepresented regarding his views on the use of machinery. Gandhi often makes contra­dictory and untenable statements on the spur of the moment. But these statements do not trouble us the moment we link them with his first principles. We should give some credit to his pragmatic approach in evaluating his theories. His basic approach to machines is clearly defined by the following passage and all other statements must be interpreted in the light of this:

 

            “Pure Swadeshi is not at all opposed to machinery...Opposition to mills or machinery is not the point. What suits our country most is the point. I am not opposed to the movement of manufac­turing machines in the country, nor to making improvements in machinery. I am only concerned with what the machines are meant for. I may ask, in the words of Ruskin, whether these machines will be such as would blow off a million men in a minute or they will be such as would turn waste lands into arable and fertile land”. 7

 

            Gandhi’s sagacity comes home to us when we consider strikes like the one organized by employees of the Life Insurance Corporation of India as a protest against the introduction of computers. The developments all over the world since his death, especially those in Japan, seem to justify his attitude to small-scale industries. Gunner Myrdal’s appraisal of Gandhi as a Radical Liberal is more to the point in this context than any other estimate of his economic programme:

 

            “His hostility to modern industrial technology and to machines and, more generally, his pro-rural and anticity bias were sometimes expressed in terms which are hardly compatible with enlightened liberalism. But the recognition in recent years of the very predominant importance of agriculture in economic development and also of the under-utilized labour resources have, to an extent, proved these ideas of his to be less irrational than they seemed to be at a time when develop­ment was narrowly defined as industrialization and when it was believed that industrialization would rapidly create new employment that would make possible the ‘skimming off’ the ‘labour surplus’ in agriculture. Even at the culmination of that era-approximately in the years when the Second Five ­Year Plan was prepared–the planners found a compromise with Gandhian ideology, to reserve a large area of production of consumption goods for traditional labour-intensive technology. In regard to central state planning generally, when Indian policy has seemed to deviate most conspicuously from Gandhi’s teaching, there may now be forebodings of an evolution of thinking that comes close to Gandhi’s own. The plans produced in predominantly financial terms have turned out to be fictitious and misleading. Consequently India is beginning to realize the necessity to perceive development as a process encompassing the entire social system. This was Gandhi’s view, though he never elaborates it”. 8

 

            Thus we see even in the sphere of political economy Gandhi’s ideas are of certain relevance and use in new India.

 

            Gandhi belongs to the future as well. In future, he will be followed even in the societies when Hinduism is of no consequence. Already we have seen how Martin Luther King has used Gandhian techniques in North America to checkmate the ruthless exploita­tion of the Negro race by the power-drunk whites of America. Many African and Asian nations owe their independence to the beneficial posthumous influence of the Mahatma. So long as freedom and equality remain the cherished goals of humanity, so long as fear, greed and jealousy curb the free expression of man’s spirit, so long as man plays wolf to man, Gandhi will survive. A saviour of mankind, he will live for ages and earn the gratitude of the dumb millions of untold generations to come.
