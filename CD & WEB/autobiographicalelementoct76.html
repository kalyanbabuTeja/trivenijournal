AUTOBIOGRAPHICAL ELEMENT

IN R. K. NARAYAN’S EARLY NOVELS

 

K. NIRUPA RANI

 

    Grouping together Mulk Raj Anand and R. K. Narayan and Raja Rao, Professor K. R. Srinivasa Iyengar regards them as “a remarkable triad”, while William Walsh labels them as the “Big Three.” Of the “Supreme Trio” Narayan perhaps is the most widely-read-and-appreciated novelist.          In presenting a realistic picture of the traditional Hindu middle class life, in a comic mould, Narayan is unique. Hailing from a South-Indian middle-class family, he interprets life in a subjective manner. And some of the details related in his autobiography, My Days, bear testimony to this fact. Yet “the personal and the particular” becomes “the general and the universal” in his art. To achieve this effect Narayan locates the action of his novels in an imaginary Tamil town, Malgudi, which is replete with the classical atmosphere of typical South-Indian community. Narayan’s Malgudi is like Hardy’s Wessex or Faulkner’s Yoknaphatwapha. His novels can be called Malgudi-Novels in as much as Richmal Compton’s can be categorized as William-Novels.

 

    Narayan is a profound lover of humanity; he portrays with sympathy life in all its flaws and frivolities from a comic point of view, laughs and makes his readers laugh at the silly human follies, and foibles, for indeed man is a toy in the hands of fate. His view of life is one of practical wisdom; and he treats human sentiment­alism, selfishness, manners and meanness with sympathy and compassion. Springing from the experience of his early life, his early novels–Swami and Friends, The Bachelor of Arts and The English Teacher–are truly autobiographical. Yet, they are subjectively-objective, like the autobiographical novels of Mulk Raj Anand (Seven Summers, Morning Face, and Confession of a Lover). ‘Childhood’ is the theme of Swami and Friends; ‘youth’ is the theme of The Bachelor of Arts; and ‘manhood’ is that of The English Teacher. Though Swami and Chandran and Krishnan are the chief characters, Child and Youth and Householder are respectively the real heroes of the three novels. They are an objective portrayal of the first three phases of life which is Narayan’s own. As Professor Srinivasa Iyengar says, the characters appear to have taken a sort of transmigration from body to body, from novel to novel.

 

    Narayan’s first novel, Swami and Friends, is a portrayal of his own childhood recaptured with a thorough knowledge of child-psychology–reluctance to school and discipline. The activities of Swaminathan and his friends in Malgudi appear to be a dramatization of the activities of Narayan and his friends during his early life in Madras described in his My Days. Swami is a little boy of an orthodox middle-class family who has already changed two schools. He is shown not only through the dimension of child psychology but in his full range of being even at the beginning of the novel:

 

    It was Monday morning. Swaminathan was reluctant to open his eyes. He considered Monday especially unpleasant in the calendar. After the delirious freedom of Saturday and Sunday, it was difficult to get into Monday mood of work and discipline. He shuddered at the very thought of school...

 

        The four boys whom Swami has honoured with his friendship are: Somu the monitor who is more or less the uncle of the class, Mani the mighty good-for-nothing, Sankar the most brilliant boy of the class and Samuel the Pea on account of his size. To others his attitude is one of haughty indifference. Narayan gives a comic account of the friends and their preparation for examination, their behaviour in the classroom and at home and at a mass meeting, their concern with the driving away of the British, and their cricket. It is like a novel treatise about all schoolboys in India. Portraying dextrously the friendship of Swamis Somu, Mani, Rajan, Sankar and Samuel, Narayan gives an inkling of the family-background of each one of the boys reflected in his conduct, words and deeds. Rajan, a Police Superintendent’s son, has dashing nature. He can be considered a representative of upper middle class family where old people overpamper children. Mani belongs to an unsophisticated family: his mind always works on evil plans and he tries to achieve his goal by either force or bribing. Though the novel deals with their quarrels and friendship, Swami is made the central character with a comic touch. Any child–a Swami, a Mani, a Rajan, or a Samuel–experiences that early part of its life with innocent enthusiasm ignorant of the after-effects of its childish deeds. Thus the novel is about everybody’s childhood. Graham Greene says: ‘It is a classical schoolboy story of a child, written with the same understanding sense of beauty and sadness.’ The novel thus deals with the inevitable sad music of growth from dreary childhood into a contemplative adolescence.

 

        From the schoolboy Swami to the college-youth Chandran in The Bachelor of Arts is a natural transition. In this novel Narayan appears reminiscing his own youth and adolescence experience. The novel deals with the critical phase of life, adolescence, which every­body has to pass through in his last year at the college and in his first year in the world. Chandran is a student of history like Narayan himself. In his essay “Green Sari,” Narayan relates how he fell in love with a girl and how he was disappointed when the horoscopes failed to match. In his My Days, Narayan describes again that, after graduation in 1934, ‘he passed through a phase of impossible love-sickness, falling in love at first sight with all and sundry, all one-sided,’ that specially in Coimbatore he espied ‘a girl drawing water from the street-tap near his sister’s house,’ and that ‘after a number of hitches, particularly astrological’ the marriage took place. Narayan dramatizes this experience. Like Swami, Chandran also hails from an orthodox middle-class family. We find some of Rajan’s character in Chandran in the beginning. But Chandran’s behaviour during his disillusionment is like Swami’s. The first part of the novel deals with Chandran’s ambition, snobbery and vanity. He lacks in gamesmanship which is necessary for success in life. The second part presents the turning point in Chandran’s life. He faces the problem of finding a job and a better-half for himself. Chandran falls in love with Malathi whom he has seen from a distance and wishes to marry her. But his orthodox parents disapprove of it as their horoscopes fail to match. Even today so many marriages do not take place because: of the orthodox belief of the elders in the agreement of horoscopes. The third part describes the love-sick odyssey of the hero, Chandran’s ascetic wanderings over South India in search of solace. Disillusioned he returns to his parents in Malgudi. In the fourth part he marries Susila, the girl chosen by his parents, and finally finds a job for himself. Even Swami’s friends seem to have undergone transmigration as Veeraswami and Mohan. Mani’s aggressive nature is seen in Veeraswami, and Mohan is an apostle of pragmatism and callous realism. The Bachelor of Arts depicts in general terms how youth continue their lives taking their strides and shocks and tremors of the past, and how the care-free graduates through rough experiences change and fit themselves in stable professions in life, like Narayan himself.

 

    The English Teacher is not only a natural continuation of the earlier novels but perhaps it is Narayan’s greatest novel marking the turning point in his art. The novel portrays the third stage of life-the householder. Krishnan the English Teacher is a continu­ation of Chandran, the Bachelor of Arts. In his My Days, Narayan reveals that his wife Rajam had gone on a holiday to her parents’ home in Coimbatore. He was full of misgivings during her absence. But some weeks after her return, she died of typhoid. He felt he had lost his anchorage and dismal emptiness stretched before him. One day he was introduced by his cousin to his friend Raghunatha Rao, who invited him to ‘some psychic experiments’. ‘At one of the sittings, Rajam communicated to him through Mr. Rao’s automatic writing.’ She assured him that she was always at his side. Narayan practised psychic contacts and learnt to have total view of one personality. Narayan himself admits that The English Teacher is an adaptation of his own experience:

 

    I have described this part of my experience of her (Rajam’s) sickness and death in The English Teacher so fully that I do not perhaps go over it again. More than any other book, The English Teacher is autobiographical in content, very little part of it being fiction. The English Teacher of the novel, Krishnan, is a fictional character in the fictional city Malgudi, but he goes through the same experience I had gone through and he calls his wife Susila and the child Leela instead of Hema. The toll that typhoid took and all the desolation that followed, with a child to look after and the psychic adjustments, are based on my own experiences. This book falls in two parts­–one is domestic life and the other is spiritual. Many readers have gone through the first half with interest and the second half with bewilderment...

 

            That is because in the first half Narayan has painted a picture of the typical contemplative manhood of a householder of middle­class society, experiencing the first shocks of life. The second part gives a picture of a distressed young widower who becomes ‘both father and mother’ to his innocent three-year-old daughter and brings her up.

 

            Thus Narayan’s three early novels portray the atmosphere of the orthodox middle-class life and society. It is a world of children, boys, householders, wives, husbands, mothers, grand-mothers, students, teachers, parents, sons and daughters–all true to life. They are all remarkable for their subtleties and convic­tions with which middle-class family relationships are saturated, which Narayan paints vividly and humorously.

 

            Raja Rao also deals with childhood and youth and manhood, but in a diffused and disjointed way which is natural and necessary to the narrative technique and religio-comic tone of his novels. But they do not form the main themes of his novels. It is Mulk Raj Anand who first had dealt with childhood and youth and manhood enchantingly in a subjectively-objective manner. His range is vast, and he dramatizes the three stages of life at all levels in general in a tragi-comic manner. Narayan has chosen only one slice of humanity, the middle-class society, confined to a single town Malgudi: his range is narrow thus and of ‘resolved limitations, just a few inches wide’, like Jane Austin’s. Anand’s picture of the three phases of life is typically North-Indian in colour: Narayan’s portrayal is supremely South-Indian in nature. While Anand’s range is epically vast, Narayan’s (though limited) is supremely intensive and profound, serio-comic and sympathetic, perfect and grand.
