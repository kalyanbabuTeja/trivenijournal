THE OTHER SIDE OF THE PICTURE

 

Meadows Taylor and John Masters: A Comparison

 

DR (Mrs) ILA RAO

 

            It is remarkable how a literary genius can find material in the most unusual incidents and connect them into a topic of interest. What would most probably have been deemed to oblivion and obscurity in the dusty pages of the journals that dealt with the problems of British administration in India is brought to life in The Confessions of a Thug by John Meadows Taylor. It is interesting to note how he moulded all the information he had gained as an administrative officer into a novel.

 

            There may be many who may not even be familiar with the name Thug in India today but in the 18th and 19th centuries the organization of Thugs had made travel practically impossible in the country. Curiously enough both Muslim and Hindu Thugs were united in a common cause, in the worship of Kali or Bhavani. It was an absolutely close-knit fraternity which was bound by common rules, regulations, superstitions and customs. There is no knowledge about the actual origin of this association. In 1816, however, a paper was published by Doctor Sherwood in the Literary Journal of Madras in which he had made a very detailed study of the ceremonies and practices of the Thugs of Southern India.

 

            In 1830 Colonel Sleeman, who was the political agent for the Central Provinces, brought to light certain gruesome crimes committed by the Thugs of that area. On the basis of the papers he published and the appeals he made for the eradication of Thugs, the attention of the public was aroused and mass scale preparations were made to apprehend and wipe out all Thugs in the country. It was in the form of a crusade, and Colonel Meadows Taylor was one of the officers entrusted with the work of apprehending and trying Thugs in the Central Provinces­.

 

            Philip Meadows Taylor was the son of a merchant and was born in Liverpool in the year 1803. His father’s failure in busi­ness landed him in financial difficulties, so he undertook the job of working as a clerk for a merchant. When he was fifteen years old he was sent to Bombay to work in the same capacity for another merchant. Certain unhappy incidents made him give up civil life and take to a career in the army, so he enlisted as a soldier of the Nizam. In his regiment he had to make the first investigation about the Thugs. Later, he acted as the Regent for the minor son of the Raja of Sholapore. Later on when he was made Governor of one of the Berar districts he managed to keep perfect peace during his regime. He had a natural inclination for writing and a flair for narrative. Most of the information and personal experiences acquired during his long stay in India find their way into his novels. Besides The Confessions of a Thug he has also written a number of historical novels.

 

            The Confessions of a Thug is based on the notes he had prepared during his service in India and also his personal encounter with a notorious Thug. In 1834 when he was recovering from a bad attack of Jungle Fever he wrote this book as a mere form of relaxation. He had a good deal of first hand information on the subject of Thugs and his mind was absolutely full with the extensive notes he had made on individual cases. In his preface to the first edition which was published in 1839 he tries to explain his reasons for writing the book. He makes it quite clear that his motive was not to gratify a morbid taste in people for tales of horror and crime, but to expose fully the practices of the Thugs. While accepting the author’s explanation one still has the feeling that he would have also been prompted to a certain extent by the idea of the “Whiteman’s burden” which most English administrators of India had at that time. In his preface to the second edition the author explains why the book had been received favourably by the English public.

 

            The hero of the confessions is not an imaginary character. He is created from the author’s personal encounter with a notorious Thug. In the year 1832 the author came to know a well-known Thug who was working as an informer for the Nizam’s territories. He had narrated to the author the story of his life and his exploits as a Thug and how he had been instrumental in the murder of seven hundred and nineteen travellers. The very idea was gruesome and terrifying but Meadows Taylor was very curious to know as to how a human being could so degrade himself, bring himself down to the level of an insensible wild beast, and mercilessly kill so many innocent men and women. The book is a very interesting and fascinating study of the criminal instinct not just in one but a group of individuals. The main incidents described in the book are dependent upon actual facts, but a certain amount of fiction has also been used to connect the events and make the adventures of the Thug as interesting as the nature of his horrible profession would permit.

 

            The book is written in the form of a narrative in the first person. Amir Ali the Thug narrates his adventures to the author, and only now and then the author makes his personality felt by the occasional comments, remarks and explanations. In the first chapter the Thug tries to justify his profession. He calls Thuggee a game or a sport akin to the hunting of a wild animal. For the Thug the prey is man against whom he has sworn a “relentless unerring destruction.” The author is positively horrified and revolted by this statement, but is prompted by curiosity to know the reason why a human being develops such hatred towards his own fellowmen. There must be some psychology behind such hatred, so he asks the Thug to relate the story of his life, how he became a Thug and his exploits as a member of that gang.

 

            The confession starts with the faint recollections that Amir Ali had about his early childhood. He had been born in a well-to-do family and he had a little sister who had been very attached to him. He could remember a journey that he and his family made from their village to another and how the party had halted at a village for taking rest and food. It is here that they met a stranger who had taken a great fancy to Amir Ali, and when in the evening the party started on their journey, the stranger with another unknown man accompanied them on the pretext of friendship and company. But when the party reached a lonely part of the forest these two strangers suddenly attacked them and killed every member of that group by strangling them with a handkerchief. Amir Ali could only remember the dead body of his mother and he fell over it weeping. At that stage one of the Thugs put his handkerchief round the neck of Amir Ali with the intention of killing him, but the stranger who had probably taken a genuine liking for him saved him and took him to his house and adopted him as his son as he had no children of his own. The name of this stranger was Ishmail and he was the leader of a band of Thugs. He however took Amir Ali and put him in the village with his wife who looked after him affectionately and brought him up. His education also was not neglected, the village Maulvi took him in hand and taught him his letters and how to read the Koran. After attaining manhood Amir Ali was then faced with the task of choosing his career. The old teacher advised him to become a Moolla. At this stage of his narration the Thug repents for not having taken the advice of his teacher, then he takes comfort in the fact that life is dominated by the decrees of fate and since it was fated that he should become a Thug he could not decide as to which course to follow in life. Unable to take a decision on his own he approached his foster-father Ishmail one night after he had returned from one of his expeditions and asked him for guidance. Ishmail naturally spoke in very glorious terms about his own profession and he also tried to explain the philosophical significance behind the organization of Thugs, and how the belief in the ‘Divine authority’ unites even people from different religions, the Moslem and the Hindu, to live as brothers. This account given by his foster-father so impressed Amir Ali that he had no difficulty in making up his mind to enter the organisation.

 

            To take the life of a living creature whether human or beast takes a great deal of determination and courage, The hesitation has to be overcome but once that is done the individual becomes something like an addict, much like a beast, a man-eater for instance; that once having killed becomes a habitual killer. This is more or less the psychology behind the inhuman attitude of the Thugs. A few days later Amir Ali had the opportunity of killing a tiger and for the first time he had a feeling of elation in taking the life of a living creature. So on Dasara Day Amir Ali was initiated into the profession, and a pickaxe was given into his hands as the symbol of his profession. He was then again reminded about the divine nature of his profession and how destruction is a part of life which is essential and how Thugs have been given the power to destroy by Kali herself. As a sign of his having joined the fraternity “goor” or “brown sugar” was distributed all round and he too was made to eat some. After the ceremony he had an occasion to see the Thugs at work. An old man and his handsome young son were strangled and Amir Ali was overcome with pity and sorrow for the death of the fine, handsome boy. One of the members of the gang justifies this killing by telling Amir Ali the long story of injustice and cruelty that the old man had inflicted on him and had made him more or less an outcaste in society until he joined the organisation. Thus the killing of the old man and his son was motivated by a sense of revenge.

 

            After that incident Amir Ali had the opportunity to kill his first victim and thereafter it became very easy for him to plan and execute the murders and in course of time he became an adept. All the descriptions are very gruesome and horror-inspiring.

 

            After some years, due to various accidents and other happen­ings, the band was considerably diminished and Amir Ali was find­ing it difficult to continue his work, so he and a few others joined the Pindaris, a group of robbers led by a man called Chettoo. Though they managed to get a good deal of money and plunder it did not have the excitement of Thuggee. Besides, they could never get on with a gang that did not have the unity that the Thugs had. Finally they gave the Pindaris the slip and stole what they could from them and got away. For sometime Amir Ali and his father lived with the vast wealth, they had accumulated, but the restless spirit prompted him to start again on his exploits. This time he reached a village which seemed very familiar and he met a young couple going to another town. The wife was extra­ordinarily beautiful and bore a striking resemblance to him. She had round her neck an old coin on a thread which was supposed to have been given to her by a brother lost in childhood when her parents had been killed by Thugs. Amir Ali always had a fascination for charms and amulets and he decided to murder this couple just for the sake of that old coin. He posed as a friend and travelled with them and after reaching the outskirts of the village he killed them. He got the chain from the neck of the girl and while doing so he looked at her face and was impressed by her extraordinary beauty. It is this face that haunted him throughout his life, for it was later on that he came to know from an old member of his gang that this was his own sister and that the so-called charm was the very coin that he himself had given her as a child. There was the rule among Thugs that women should not be strangled. This was the second woman that Amir Ali had killed. It appeared as if the curse of Bhavani had fallen on him, for this time when he returned home, the Rajah of his village arrested him and his father. His father was tortured and put to death and he was branded and set free. His possessions were all seized and his wife coming to know that he was a Thug died of grief. His only child, a daughter, was taken care of by the Moollah of his village. However before being arrested again he managed to get money and secretly gave it to the Moollah for bringing up his child. The rest of his life he spent in prison. The last task he did was to act as an informer and be instrumental in the arrest of an old Thug who had been responsible for the murder of his mother and father and who had deliberately kept the truth from him and had not prevented him from killing his own sister. This last act of revenge over, the Thug was sentenced to spend the rest of his life in captivity.

 

            The book is a mine of information about many surprising and unfamiliar customs. There is a complicated hierarchy in the organisation of Thugs, the principal person being of course the leader. There are people who are allotted particular jobs–the grave-diggers, stranglers and the organisers. On comparing the Thugs with similar secret organizations like the Pindaris and the dacoits, the author comes to the conclusion, that the Thugs though detestable were a little more humane than others as they saw to it that their victims never suffered for more than a minute before death. Besides there were some strict rules and regulations–women should not be molested; none should be tortured; and there was a strong bond of fellowship that knit the gang together, whereas other underground organizations did not have this code of honour. This does not mean that Meadows Taylor had any admiration or praise for the Thugs. On the other hand he had all contempt for them, and considered them to be inhuman in their attitude.

 

            The style of Meadows Taylor is quite interesting. Being at the same time faintly reminiscent of the 18th century picaresque novels it has also an individuality of its own. He attempts as far as possible to incorporate Urdu and Hindi words and idioms into his style in a very appropriate manner, so as to make his narrative as realistic as possible. Again he draws the line of distinction between the author and the narratory or the hero of the novel. It is only the hero of the novel who utilizes so many vernacular words. The author, when he speaks as himself, on the other hand, never digresses from the language of the conventional Englishman. The difference between the two speeches is very cleverly represented. One must not forget the fact that Meadows Taylor was so familiar with the local dialects that it was not at all difficult for him to transfer the tone, the rhythm and idiom of vernacular speech to the style he had used. This makes his style very realistic and effective.

 

            It is interesting how Meadows Taylor could just take the facts and figures about the activities of Thugs and mould them into a novel. Generally such type of picaresque novels are very dull and monotonous; and the nature of the topic is such that the events and incidents become very stereotyped. Meadows Taylor has tried to overcome this defect as best as he could; he has therefore introduced an element of suspense and mystery which is clarified towards the end of the novel.

 

            More than a century after the Confessions of a Thug, John Masters used the same topic as the theme of one of his novels­ – The Deceivers. This book which was published in the year 1952 can be compared to the Confessions. The novel starts with the marriage of William Savage to Mary Wilson, and their journey to the Mandya District where Savage was the Collector. The day after they reach their headquarters they come to know that a young woman was going to be a Suttee. Her husband had been missing for sometime and recently she had confirmed in her dreams that he was dead. Chandra Sen was the patel of the village and he was the one who gave them all the details of the missing man whose name was Gopal. According to him there was a striking resemblance between William Savage and Gopal except of course the complexion. This fact makes William Savage think that the only way of saving the woman was to impersonate Gopal and appear before her and thus avert the Suttee. While on this mission of saving Gopal’s wife, Savage meets a man who contrives to give him a chance to see a gang of Thugs at work. This sets him off on the trail of Thugs operating in that area. He naturally found it very convenient to keep his disguise to facilitate his work as he would be readily accepted into the gang as one of them. After great hazards and dangers he finally manages to unearth the various gangs of Thugs and also proves that Chandra Sen, the patel, is also one of them and thus brings them to justice.

 

            Meadows Taylor had based his Confessions of a Thug on his own experiences and had tried to give an artistic shape to every report that he had prepared about the Thugs. There is therefore a good deal of versimilitude in his account. Even his principal character is drawn from real life and is not just imaginary. The author has himself explained that he has cleverly manipulated fact and fiction to maintain and sustain the story.

 

            Though the theme and subject are the same the very technique of John Master’s novel is different. It is not written in the form of an account but as a narration in the third person. Besides he makes his aim and purpose very clear in his post-script to the novel.

 

            “My purpose in this book as in Night Runners of Bengal was to recreate the feel of a historical episode than write a minutely accurate report.”1 There is no doubt that he has taken all his material from General Sleeman’s report and Meadows Taylor’s novel, but he has given his story a different perspective altogether. Where Meadows Taylor has made use of all the information he had collected about the Thugs, John Masters makes use of only a few selected incidents and limited material. Meadows Taylor makes the Thug his main character, and he tries to arrive at a psychological explanation of the inhuman actions. Besides he tries to advance the theory that evil is a sort of a power and once in the clutches of evil it forms a vicious circle from which it is absolutely impossible to break away from. In every case that he has studied in the Confessions Meadows Taylor makes it quite clear that there is a history of frustration and a motive for revenge. He does not defend the action of the Thugs, but he tries to analyse the motive behind such inhuman behaviour.

 

            In The Deceivers, John Masters also describes the practice and beliefs of the Thugs, but his main idea, however, is to bring out the full implication of the personal struggle of William Savage which is drawn on the lines of General Sleeman’s report. It is however nowhere indicated that the methods Sleeman used were the same that Masters has described in his novel. There is no doubt that some of the incidents described by John Masters are more or less reminiscent of General Sleeman’s report.

 

            If in the Confessions the incidents were represented from the Thug’s point of view, in The Deceivers as William Savage is the hero, the incidents are narrated from his point of view. The story of The Deceivers is not the adventures of the Thugs; but the story of William Savage, the activities of the Thug is only an essential part of the plot of the story. The few incidents about the Thugs which find their way into the novel are highly dramatized to increase the interest of the story. It is also highly improbable that the hero should have taken on the garb of a Thug and encountered various dangers, and neglected his wife in order to bring to light the secret practices of the Thugs. A certain amount of unusual and sensational events have been used by John Masters to make the story gripping and interesting. Meadows Taylor’s novel is written in the style of a picturesque novel. As the author makes no attempt to dramatize any event there is a certain monotony and boredom in his book. Further there is no progress so far as the main character is concerned. Nowhere in the book does he attempt to show a self-realization or remorse in the attitude of the character. The Confessions is just a narration of his exploits and not a means to self-realization.

 

            Meadows Taylor’s book gives one side of the picture, that is, the activities of the Thugs; John Masters’ novel gives the other side–the struggle of the hero to suppress the Thugs.

 

            Besides the very perspective is also different. In the story of the Thug, Meadows Taylor has very rightly taken the Eastern attitude–namely the preponderance of fate in shaping events. Again and again through the various incidents the author stresses upon the element of chance and fate. There is no scope for the reference to a personal struggle or a personal involvement as the principal character is helpless in the clutches of a divine providence. This attitude may be a reflection of the author’s own belief in destiny and fate which he has referred to in his other novels also. In The Deceivers Master’s attitude in spite of the melodrama is entirely different. It is more the personal involvement of William Savage rather than the story of the Thugs.        Besides John Masters was interested in tracing out the genealogy of the Savage family and not the progress of the Thugs. He has there­fore conveniently and very aptly too used the title “Deceivers” which in an ordinary sense may refer to the Thugs themselves as a transliteration of the Indian word. There may however be a greater involvement in the title “Deceivers” for it might also refer to William Savage and other characters in the novel who in one sense or the other are deceiving others and deceiving

themselves. We are also very keenly aware of the “Whiteman’s burden” in this book, from the way in which the author manipulates fact and fiction. In The Deceivers John Masters can be commended for the wonderful way in which he has taken the bare facts of a report and turned it into an interesting story. Meadows Taylor can be praised for his truth to details and his unusual interest in a gruesome organization, the activities of which were in complete darkness. John Masters’ novel, however, has the makings of a popular novel. He has gone out of his way to make the story interesting so that he has had to use a good deal of highly unrealistic incidents. So far as the story is concerned he has been able to maintain the suspense and the interest, but he has failed to make the novel realistic. On the other hand Meadows Taylor has made little or no effort to fit all his material into the framework of a popular novel. Hence the work is tedious and at points very stereotyped. We can only commend him for his patience in the collection of facts and his endeavour to gather them all into a novel.
