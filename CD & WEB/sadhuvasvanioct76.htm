SADHU VASVANI

 

A mystic-saint of modern India

 

B. RADHAKRISHNA RAO

 

            A spiritually-illumined soul radiating a fragrance of purity, an inspired mystic harmonising in him all that was best and noblest in the Indian heritage and the Western tradition, an apostle of India’s hoary spiritual culture, a torch-bearer of an enlightened age of harmony and understanding and a great servant of the suffering humanity whose love and compassion overflowed to embrace birds and beasts, Sri T. L. Vasvani reverentially called ‘Dadaji’, meaning elder brother, was an illustrious son of Mother India who offered his all in the Yajna of restoring India once again to its former greatness–spiritual as well as material. Belonging to the select galaxy of the seers and sages of this holy land, Sadhu Vasvani, constantly inspired by the vision of India’s revival to fulfil her world mission, called upon the youth of the country to imbibe the message of Shakti preached by Swami Vivekananda and break the shackles of slavery binding the Mother.

 

            A god-intoxicated savant, he had unshakable faith in the limit­less power of the human soul and the ultimate triumph of the humanity centring round “new knowledge, new power and new capacity”. In a world beset with the unceasing conflict between the old and new, Dadaji formed a golden link between the ancient and modern. His sweet and all embracing compassion made him believe in the fellowship with all creation. “The creation of God,” he said, “is bound by golden chains to the feet of one God, the one divine father of us all.” Endowed with the myriad qualities of head and heart, his life was like a rainbow reflecting the varied hues of the earth below and heaven above.”

 

            As a messenger of equality and brotherhood, he strived to clear the fog of sectarian strifes and quarrels in the name of religion, and enshrine in the hearts of men the true religion of service, compassion and spiritual beauty. All scriptures, he asserted, are but the repository of spiritual wisdom propounding the unity that transcends the barriers of clime, creed and nationality. Communism too, he felt enshrined a truth to which it owed its dynamic character. “I belong to no sect,” he said, “I adore but one God. And my faith is to worship the one Master and to do good to all.”

 

            Dadaji, for whom the service of the suppressed and depressed was the highest worship of the supreme, till the last day of his earthly sojourn gave solace and soccour to the broken and bereaved ones. Every human being, every creature for that matter, was to him an image of divine beauty. Out of the abundance of the God Dadaji received abundantly and he shared his abundant gifts with the poor and needy. “The noblest temple,” says Dadaji, “is the heart of a poor man who gets his food and who blesses the name of God.”

 

            Indeed, his life was a shining saga of service and sacrifice radiating spiritual beauty and divine fragrance. Everyday sitting under the trees he loved, he gave unto the downtrodden, the poor and the miserable ones whatever he had – money, food, clothing and more than anything else, the healing touch of his Godly heart. About his unassuming humility and utter simplicity J. P. Vasvani says: “There was a time when he rubbed shoulders with the tallest in the land. In those days his name was coupled with those of Mahatma Gandhi and Rabindranath Tagore as one of the three leader of New India. But he chose to walk the humble way of life. He made his life an offering at the altar of suffering humanity.”

 

            Born on November 25, 1879, in Hyderabad Sind–the blessed land of spiritual sublimity associated with the Vedic seers and Muslim mystics and fakirs, contemplatives and men of renunciation – after a brilliant academic record he earned his master’s degree in English at the age of 22. After passing the M. A. examination he joined as Professor in the famous Metropolitan College of Calcutta. Calcutta, which was then the nerve-centre and throbbing-heart of the revolutionary activities in India, played a significant role in channelising his patriotic aspirations.

 

            As India’s representative, he thrilled the select audience at the World Congress of Religions by his inspiring and enlightening speech on India’s ancient heritage. He was then only 30 years of age. His subsequent lectures and speeches in different parts of Europe aroused deep interest in Indian thought and religion and linked many with him in India’s mission of help and healing. He set on fire the hearts of many with the vision to make the wisdom of India’s seers and sages world-dynamic.

 

            The passing away of his mother when he was 40 years of age severed his only link with the mundane world and kindled his smouldering fire of renunciation and service: he resigned his lucrative job and dedicated himself to the service of the nation and people. He renounced everything to become “an humble servant of India and the Rishis.” He entered the arena of freedom struggle without any fanfare or publicity.          While most of the leaders guiding the destiny of the people were working with the whole and sole aim of driving away British from India, Dadaji had his vision rivetted on preparing a cadre of young men of strong moral fibre and burning patriotism for nation-building activities. British, he said, should leave India sooner or later. But that was only a small part of the struggle.

 

            It was at this time that the charismatic personality of Mahatma Gandhi had appeared on the Indian scene with the launching of the unique Satyagraha movement. As one of the supporters of Mahatma Gandhi he was actively associated with the “Young India” started by Gandhiji to propagate the message of Satyagraha. The very first article on the front page of the inaugural issue was an article by Dadaji.

 

            Dadaji was a profound scholar and a writer of eminence blessed with the abundant gifts of muse. A prolific and spirited writer in English and Sindhi he wrote a spate of books–India: Arise, Awake, Young India, India’s Adventure, India in Chains, The Secret of Asia, My Motherland, Builders of Tomorrow, Apostles of Freedom–all of which breath an air of intense patriotism urging the youths to dedicate their lives to the service of the Motherland. These inspiring books which fired the sensitivity of the youths to fight the brute force of the imperialist rulers were proscribed by the British Government. Through his writings he renewed the inner life of India, and inspired by the truth of renunciation, brotherhood and love, he transformed the social life of countless people.

 

            Realising the importance of the vital field of education in nation-building, Dadaji turned his attention to education and other spheres with an emphasis on character-building. Inspired by the faith in the youths of India whom Dadaji felt can change the destiny of the country, he started “youth centres” all over India. He asserted again and again that India’s youth were the destined leaders of the nation. His message to young India was: “Be simple, be manly, be strong and spend your strength for the poor and broken ones.” As a worshipper of Shakti, he pleaded for the birth of a “new physical culture, a new spirit of adventure, a new love of danger and difficulty.”

 

            He laid stress on orienting the whole set up of education to further India’s mission of service and sacrifice. “Our schools and colleges” he said, “are prison cells. They keep out the sunshine of Indian ideals and culture. This isolation of modern India’s brain from the mighty souls that made Aryavarta a model nation in the long – this is the tragedy of our life today.”

 

            A humanitarian to the core, he moved across the length and breadth of the country to see and study the miserable conditions under which the peasant masses live. Pleading for a peasant renaissance, he remarked: “Give land to the poor and teach them scientific methods of intensive agriculture and co-operative organisation.”

 

            In 1933, Dadaji founded the “Meera movement in education” which has today its headquarters in Poona. Its basic objectives were imparting moral training to the youth and preparing them for nation-building activities. The movement which was originally started in Sindh was cruelly uprooted from its native soil in the holocaust of partition setting at naught the best efforts of the enlightened men of Sind. It was the unbounded faith of Dadaji in human goodness that enabled him to recreate on the Indian soil the network of institutions that had built-up over many years in Sind. The Meera movement is aimed at enriching students with the vital truths of modern life at the same time making them grasp Indian ideals and Indian culture, at once idealistic and spiritual. In 1962, he started St. Meera’s College for Women at Poona, the opening ceremony of which was per­formed by Dr. Radhakrishnan, the then President of India. The college is inspired by the faith that in the new unfolding of India’s destiny women have to play an important role. “The women soul” said Dadaji “shall lead us upwards, on.”

 

 

            Under the ever-living inspiration of Dadaji’s dynamic spirit was founded a number of institutions which continue to perform the humanitarian activities. They include two charitable dispensaries where hundreds of poor patients receive free medical aid; a welfare department which gives help in cash and kind to the poor and needy; St. Meera’s educational institutions where education is given free to poor students; ‘Jiva Daya’ department, dedicated to the service of birds and beasts; a publication department which publishes books and journals interpreting the wisdom of East to West – Meera and the East-West series brought out by the depart­ment continue to disseminate the message of world peace, compas­sion to all creatures and the harmony of East and West.

 

            To a large number of people who used to flock to him to listen his words of compassion and love, his advice was: “Religion is not a creed or dogma: creeds are but broken reeds. Not decorated temples but in the broken cottages of the poor is the great God wiping the tears of the widow and orphan and singing his new Gita for the new age.”

 

            He was an eloquent speaker and gifted orator. The music of his rich voice was an inspiration to the listener to tread on the path of blessedness.

 

            To him, like St. Francis of Assissi, birds and beasts were brothers and sisters. He greeted them and poured upon them the love of his heart. “Kill not animals,” he said “kill only the animal within thee, thy little ego.”

 

            His earthly pilgrimage ended on 16th January, 1966. Dr. Rajendra Prasad, the first President of Free India, paying rich tributes to Dadaji’s many-splendoured life, remarked: “The life of Vasvaniji has been a saga of unassuming service, spiritual illumination and a source of inspiration to us all.”
