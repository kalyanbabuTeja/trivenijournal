“KHOKA” GOES TO SCHOOL

(Adapted from Swarnkamal Bhattacharya’s ‘Hate Khadi’ in Bengali)

 

DR. N. R. DEOBHANKAR

 

The house was agog since early morning. Nilima was thoroughly excited, and allowed poor Bhujju, the domestic servant, not a moment’s peace. Instructions were raining upon him like a hail-storm: “Do this…Hold that…Come here….Go to so and so…” It was no ordinary occasion, but a red-letter day in the life of this little family. Bablu, the only son and heir, was going to school for the first time and Nilima, the mother, was swelling with pride!

 

Bablu was no longer the same Bablu now. Nilima was yearning to inaugurate his formal name “Surjit”, and to practise it in her varied tasks, but somehow her lips could not get it out yet. The call was still “Bablu,” or sometimes “Khoka!” or “Khokan!”, meaning Baby or little one.

 

Bablu’s mind was torn between joy and apprehension. He had sense enough to guess that whatever ‘school’ may be, it was certainly not Mama-badi, the Maternal Uncle’s house and proverbial sanctuary for holiday. Recently, when some truancy was discovered by the father, he had experienced what chastisement meant. He had also the unpleasant memory of how Banamali Babu, his private tutor, gnashed his teeth to relieve his wrath which, but for his promise to abjure corporal punishment, he would have preferred to express otherwise. With all this, however, the idea of going to school exercised a strange fascination over his seven-year impressionable mind.

 

Nilima had finished her kitchen tasks much earlier today and had folded and arranged the child’s clothes much ahead of time. A little Kohl was kept ready for the eyes and Bhujju had collected bale leaves, mango blossom and fresh sprouts of grass, as ordered.

 

Now and again Nilima’s thoughts would wander dreamily for a few moments. How this tiny baby had, under the very eyes of the watchful parents, grown so much so suddenly! This stark truth stared her in the face as it were, for the first time this morning. Nilima’s baby was not Bablu now, but “Shriman Surajit Roy!” An unknown path leading to a bright future was opening out before him. Today was the first auspicious step on that glorious pilgrimage.

 

“Do you hear?” Nilima said, addressing her husband.

 

“Yes…..” replied Viswajit, without looking up from his paper.

 

“This is the third time you have said Yes” objected Nilima, irritated at his apparent casualness.

 

“Well, what is it?”

 

“Today is Khoka’s first day at school. Take him there yourself.”

 

“Can’t Bhujju do that? I have a lot of work on hand.”

 

“Bhujju has to go to the Post Office for a money order, then to Bablu’s school to carry his tiffin, then again to bring him home. A servant is not a machine!”

 

Then let the boy go with Paltu and Mani of the neighbouring house.”

 

Nilima was really upset this time. “As if Paltu and Mani are making their first entry in the school! And is there any comparison between him and them?” Viswajit saw he was in for it now, and agreed in the end. But when a husband agrees only half-heartedly he cannot escape some wigging. “Nobody on earth ever neglects one’s own child this way” snapped Nilima. “That’s just like you” laughed Viswajit. “A little thing makes you impatient. If you are going to bring up the child in this manner, all is over! Children must be taught courage and self-reliance from this age.” “Enough of your lecture, if you please. You can save your breath,” cut in Nilima. “Everywhere you must come out with a sermon! Who does not feel nervous on the first day? You of course have reached where you are in a single jump!”

 

Just then Bablu, over whom raged all this argument, turned up. “What do you say, Khoka?” inquired the father with enthusiasm. “Can’t you go to school by yourself?” Bablu nodded his head in prompt assent. “You naughty child!” cried Nilima, holding him close. “You must never be so rash!”

 

“I can really go alone, mother” persisted the boy. “Didn’t I see where the school stands, when I went for a walk with Kalu Dada next doors the other day? Our school is just next to the thana. Then comes the Revenue Office, then the Post Office, still further is Madhu Kundu’s place. To reach our locality we’ve to go round it. I can easily find my way, mother.”

 

Bablu mapped out the route as if he had it by heart. The parents were pleased as they listened. And yet Nilima was vaguely uneasy, though she could not say what she feared. Being a petty town, there were no trams and buses. The risk of cars and lorries too was insignificant. The husband had established a good practice as a lawyer in a short time. Even if their child missed his way there was no chance of his getting lost. Yet Nilima’s fears would not be allayed.

 

The father encouraged the child further. “I shall go with you myself this once” he agreed “but you must go alone thereafter. There’s nothing to be afraid of, is there?”

 

This annoyed again Nilima “Don’t instigate the child.  I don’t like this recklessness.”

 

“But I really know the way, mother,” assured Bablu with pride. “Paltu-Da also goes and returns by himself.” “Let them go who want, but not you. Remember,” she warned him. Her firmness silenced the boy for the time. But he made a plan and kept it to himself. Going to school alone was a trifle. But a couple of miles off there was a village green at Rahasyapur, on the District Board road, where a great fair was held last Sankrant. Within a few days Bablu would show his mother the real mettle of her son by by walking up to there and back by himself!

 

Viswajit bathed hurriedly and sat down to breakfast. In the meanwhile, Nilima was engrossed in dressing up Bablu. She tied the gold-bordered dhoti round him–it was a present at last year’s Pooja festival–slipped a silk Kurta over him, touched his face lightly with snow powder and put a tiny dot of snndalwood paste on his brow. In that make-up the child was neither Bablu nor Surjit, but the central figure on a wedding platform, as fondly envisaged by Nilima!

 

Bablu submitted to all this dolling up in good humour, but firmly resisted when the mother tried to darken his eyes with Kohl. He felt it derogatory to be made to look like an infant!

 

While a playful struggle between mother and son was in progress over this, Viswajit arrived on the scene. “Oh ho! Quite a Prince!” he exclaimed in banter. “Your son is setting out to conquer the world, I presume!” he mocked.

 

Bablu hid his face behind the mother in painful embarrassment. Nilima pretended to be angry with the husband at his untimely levity. “When asked to do something useful” said she “you stand dumb, and when you are not wanted at all you start your oratory!” Lifting up Bablu’s blushing face she consoled him, “What is there to be ashamed of my child? Look up nicely. Don’t be a simpleton. You are not going to a country school like your father. Those days are gone. Look up.”

 

Bablu touched his forehead before the holy Kumbha, put the green sprouts on his head, lay prostrate in reverence to his mother, held his father’s hand and crossing the front door gained the road. Nilima stood staring at his retreating figure. So at last Khoka too had begun his separate life!

 

Nilima felt that her motherhood was fulfilled only today, not seven years ago. It was now her turn, after her mother-in-law, to sit gazing at the path for the son’s return. The only difference was that while the one used to count months before she could see the son returning from the distant city, the other would be counting hours and minutes before her darling would be back from school.

 

It was the mother-in-law’s wish that her son should become a teacher in the village high school, and not be parted from her. But that was not to be. She suspected this was due to the daughter-in-law’s influence. She used to go about complaining to relations that the son had now become a stranger. And yet her own two daughters had joined their respective husbands in distant parts, where they were employed. She did not object to this, and was quite satisfied, even a bit proud of their prosperity. Why then must Nilima be blamed? Must others’ daughters be always made scapegoats? What was Nilima’s fault? It was the peculiar temperament of the mother-in-law’s own son. He had delegated to the wife the duty of writing letters to the mother, and of making routine inquiries about her welfare. That is how in every letter she had to mention: “Your son is busy day and night, and has no time to write separately. He is well,” That was the way of the son! As if writing a few lines to the mother with his own hand was a superhuman task! Even sending money to her regularly every month was Nilima’s job. Her husband left all his responsibility to her and was free of worry.

 

The web of Nilima’s thoughts was torn when Bhujju, the servant, asked what time he would have to carry Khoka Babu’s tiffin. “At twelve,” she replied. “You had better finish your bath and meal early.”

 

It was nearly 1-30, and Nilima was growing impatient about the servant’s return after delivering the tiffin. Only four hours had passed, but what a long interval it seemed! She needed some word about her child. He had never been parted from her for so long any day any where before. Perhaps he was moping in a corner, bored with strange companions. Perhaps he was home-sick and thinking all the time of mother!

 

Fortunately there was no cruel beating in schools these days, as used to be common in the olden times. Nilima had heard that caning was banned by law now. There may be occasional pulling of ear or a mild slap, but surely not even that on the first day. Yet the mother’s heart was vaguely uneasy with groundless doubts.

 

Hearing the front door opened, Nilima eagerly asked: “Is that you, Bhujju?”

 

“Yes, Maji.”

 

“Come here.” He did so and stood before the mistress.

 

“Did you feed Khoka with your own hand?”

 

“Yes, Maji.”

 

“Did he drink up all the milk…..rejected nothing?”

 

“Nothing, Maji.”

 

“Not a drop?” Not quite understanding this, Bhujju was silent.

 

Didn’t he want to come away with you?”

 

“No.”

 

“Not about anything?”

 

“No.”

 

“Very Well, You may go.”

 

The child must surely have been longing for home, thought Nilima. Bhujju had little sense. How should he catch what the child felt?

 

Soon after she called him again: “Bhujju!”

 

“Yes Maji” he replied, standing dutifully.

 

“Where did you meet Khoka? Inside the class or outside?”

 

“Outside, Maji.”

 

“What was he doing then?”

 

“He was playing.”

 

“Playing?”

 

“Yes, Maji….chasing children on the lawn and shouting and jumping with the whole lot.”

 

Nilima was speechless. Khoka didn’t even ask about her!….and was quite happy! How strange! The tiger-cub had his first taste of blood today. The cramping walls of the home were now to him like the alphabet he had grasped. It was the wider world that he was after, like the first lesson of his new primer.

 

After a while Nilima herself went to Bhujju’s quarter and stood at the threshold. “Bhujju!” she called out.

 

“Yes, Maji.”

 

“Don’t you miss your mother here?”

 

Confused, he replied “Yes….no just a little….at times.”

 

“You write to her, don’t you?”

 

Bhujju hung his head in shame. He was illiterate.

 

Wouldn’t I write for you, you wretch, if you asked?”

 

Later in the evening, Nilima sat near the window opening on the road. It was quarter to five. Khoka was not expected to be so late. Another half hour passed and there was Bablu at the bend of the road, marching ahead of Bhujju, proud, like a conquering hero, returning home. Nilima rushed out and stood at the front door. There was not a sign of distress on the child’s face! It was, on the contrary, sparkling with joy!

 

“Wait, wait” he cried, evading the mother’s out-stretchal arms. “Let me first put my books and things away, and he ran towards the study.

 

“Bhujju,” asked Nilima, “What kept you so late there?” “Don’t ask, Maji. Do you think the young master listens to me? Every few steps he stopped on the road! Then he wouldn’t budge without having a look at a white Saheb staying at the Dak Bungalow. Coming to the Post Office he must see at once how telegrams are sent.”

 

“Why didn’t you object?” Nilima asked.

 

“If I do so” wailed he “I get bullied. When we reached the water-works Khoka Babu got stuck again. He paid no heed when I promised to show the machinary tomorrow. You don’t know how daring he is, Maji! He wanted to climb the Durgabadi bridge, and swing by the railing!”

 

Nilima was thoroughly cross, “You are no good for anything, Bhujju!” she rebuked the innocent servant. “If you can’t manage a little child, seek another job where they will pay you to be idle!”

 

Poor Bhujju was stunned! He had thought the mistress would be pleased to hear the exploits of her fearless Son. But things went all wrong! Bhujju sadly withdrew.

 

“Do You know, mother” began Bablu with great pride “Our school team has won a big silver cup this time!”

 

What to Bablu was a sensational event fell flat on his mother , .

 

“Finish this first” she Urged, placing refreshment before him.

 

“I am not hungry yet, mother.”

 

“Of course you are! Have I to start learning from you now about your hunger?”

 

Bablu had to begin eating. Events and scenes of the new world were jostling in his mind.

 

“Did you ask about home lesson?” asked Nilima.

 

“Horne lesson!….Does anybody give home lesson on the first day? You don’t know anything, mother!”

 

Nilima kept silent for a minute, then said: “May be I know nothing. But is that any reason why a mother should be answered that way?”

 

Adjourning to the bed-room, Nilima drew the boy to her lap.

 

“Tell me, Khoka, did you feel very home sick at school?”

 

“Oh no!”

 

I know you cried! I know! While having tiffin you were longing to return home with Bhujju. Have I not guessed rightly?” Without waiting for the son’s reply she said, “You silly one! Where is the fear? Am I going to hold you tight for ever? You’ve become a big boy now!”

 

Bablu listened in silence. “Khokan!” she called again.

 

“Yes, mother.”

 

Hereafter you’ll be busy with your books all the time, isn’t that so? What a lot of friends you will have!”

 

Bablu made no reply.

 

“You naughty boy! Have you nothing to say? Your whole time at home will be for your books hereafter.”

 

As it would not do to say nothing in response, Bablu replied:

 

“No, mother”

 

“In the beginning it will be your books and papers, and then, it will be your bride! Can’t I see it coming as clear as day?”

 

“Go on!” protested Bablu, still in a fog.

 

“You needn’t pretend all this innocence! As if I can’t see through your heart!”

 

Bablu was overcome with shyness, not exactly knowing why, and smiled gently. Nilima sat gazing intently at the son. She knew full well that a river can never end as a lake. Well, what if it doesn’t? At least for today she wanted to cling to a sweet dream of holding, however briefly, a strong-limbed handsome youth of the future, in complete dependence upon her!

 

Not following the mother’s sudden flights of mood, Bablu sat looking at her with an expectant gaze. “Khokan!” she said “whatever you do, you must write to me once every week….and..…mind you….with your own hand! Don’t forget. I won’t have you shoving the task on to the wife and get out of it! Will you remember?”
