SASTRI'S POLITICAL BIOGRAPHY*

 

K. CHANDRASEKHARAN

 

Very few, if any at all, will feel disillusioned about Sri P. Kodanda Rao’s gifts and competence otherwise to write the biography of his master, the late Rt. Hon. V. S. Srinivasa Sastri. His close association with him for more than quarter of a century and the intimate knowledge he has had of the many inner workings of his leader’s mind, especially in the none-too-comfortable political atmosphere of the times between 1920 and 1946, entitle him to our listening with respect to his words. For he writes as though he speaks with little effort. The result is not superficiality of either matter or manner but solid material to enliven us about his great master whom, according to an undisclosed urge in him the world has not judged aright.

 

Diffident as he is of his literary powers, the reader at no place feels the lack of either adequate language or accurate presentation of a fine portrait. It is all one long, well-constructed, unfolding of a political career. Maybe, here and there, Kodanda Rao tries to restrain himself from an attempt at making Sastri look better than what he appeared to him; still he can be acquitted of any trace of throwing mud at the political antagonists of Sastri in his narration of some of the strange misundersrandings which Sastri was subjected to by interested persons. Judged by the best standards of a biography, Kodanda Rao’s work falls no whit behind any of the well-known standard biographies in English.

 

Kodanda Rao has not for one moment concerned himself whether to give something of a modern biography or something after the Victorian fashion. All that he has done or has striven to do is to give a faithful account of a life that was rich in both the qualities of greatness and of achievement in no mean measure. There are many delightful spots for the connoisseur of anecdotes to dwell upon in this life. At the outset we are introduced to the three important figures in the Indian political scene, at any rate as accepted by all, to have played very significant parts despite the proportions of their stature differing in perspective to a later generation. Gokhale, Gandhi and Sastri met not only together at times but also separately within the very brief period after Gandhi’s return to India and Gokhale’s death. Sastri’s self-confessions regarding his dress and appearance, to the perturbation of his own master, draw our undiluted sense of the humour of the situation. Let us turn to Kodanda Rao’s account: “Gokhale’s delicate hint about dress and appearance was however lost on Sastri. Lord Sydenham, when he was Governor of Bombay, suspected that the Society was a cover for seditionists and played with the idea of suppressing it. To stave off the catastrophe and to let the Governor know the members and their work by personal contact, Gokhale invited him and his wife to tea at the Society. That morning Gokhale discussed with Sastri the arrangements for the tea and invited him to sit with him at the table of the chief guests. Sastri noticed that Gokhale had something more on his mind but hesitated to say it. Gokhale essayed to speak but hung back with evident embarrassment. Finally, he clinched the brewing crisis by suggesting that Sastri might take a shave before meeting their Excellencies. Having released the words, Gokhale retreated to his room, somewhat abashed at the liberty he had taken with Sastri. Sastri was terribly upset and blamed himself for not anticipating Gokhale’s suggestion, and for having caused him such great embarrassment. In distress and in tears, he returned to his room, only to find a brand new safety razor on his table–a gift from Gokhale!” This is a very amusing episode told with such sense of proportion that even the trifling incident looks quite a sizable insight into the Gokhale-Sastri relationship.

 

Kodanda Rao’s chief merit lies in his making us believe in an unvarnished tale. During the year 1931, when events were shaping towards the Second Round Table Conference in London, before the actual meeting of the conference, Sastri expounded the policy of the Indian Liberals in a broadcast. Kodanda Rao’s comment is disarming in the context. “Sastri confessed that the Liberals did not foresee the magnitude of the people’s response to the Mahatma’s propaganda; they had not made the political education of the masses their duty, and the discovery came to them as a shock. The Government officials were even less in touch public opinion and did not realise the gravity of the situation.” 

 

It is not easy to summarise the many details in this volume so far as the political events connected with Sastri are concerned. The reader will have a very gripping time when he actually turns the pages, consuming bit by bit of every anecdote with which Sastri’s name was intimately connected. The Imperial Conference in London to which Sastri was the delegate of the India Government in 1921, the Geneva League of Nations, of which also he was a member representing the India Government, the South African struggle which was a legacy from his master ending in the Capetown Settlement, the Kenya disappointment which almost brought upon Sastri a heart-break, the Washington Conference for Disarmament in 1923, for which he was sent by the India Government, his tour of countries at the invitation of the Premiers of the Dominions of Canada, Australia and New Zealand, the many contacts he had with the mighty in power in the Western countries, the many scenes of his triumph, as a speaker without a second in ability of presentation of India’s case, as well as Britain’s great role in the entire world for a higher sense of political values–all these and more are scattered about the pages of an out and out entrancingly written biography.

 

Though politics is the main preoccupation of the present writer, he has not eschewed some of the relevant interesting details possessing intrinsic human interest. For instance, one can hardly leave an incident as the one where Mrs. Sastri, unable to accompany Sastri to South Africa as the Agent General’s wife, had to resign herself to the satisfaction of leaving her husband’s care to an unswerving lieutenant of Sastri. Let us to Kodanda Rao himself: “Mrs. Sastri naturally presumed that she would accompany her husband and started making preparations. But Sastri felt she would not be able to adjust herself to the life he had to live in South Africa and be happy: she would be more a handicap than help to him. But he had not the heart to tell her and disappoint her. He postponed the evil day, as it were. Finally Mr. V. Venkatasubbaiya, who was the head of the Madras of the Servants of India Society and was on intimate terms with the Sastris, was persuaded to introduce the subject and get the matter settled to avoid last-minute disappointment. He came to Bangalore, called the couple together and put leading questions to Sastri. Mrs. Sastri was surprised and Sastri embarrassed. When she was told of the life she would have to live in South Africa as the wife of the Agent General, Mrs. Sastri herself decided that she should not go. With a heavy heart, the brave yet sad lady turned to me and her moist eyes pleaded eloquently with me to look after her beloved husband in his exile. It was a touching scene. Her’s was a great sacrifice.” Anybody else would have seized upon the sentimentality of the situation and made an overdrawn picture of the whole thing. But it requires superior instinct and artistry to indicate merely the occasion’s inevitable emotional appeal and pass on, as Kodanda Rao has done.

 

The chapter on ‘Sastri and Nehru’ will be read with interest by all persons who have a desire to know why to the last Sri Jawaharlal Nehru maintained an attitude of criticism of Sastri, as also proved in the pages of his justly famed Autobiography. The attempted intervention of Gandhiji to see to a better understanding by Nehru of Sastri by the suggestion of a gift of his book to Sastri by himself was aborted even at the outset by Sastri. Let us learn it from Sastri himself. His letter to Mahadev Desai, the personal secretary to Gandhiji, explained his disinclination: “I endorse and applaud your sentiment. There is nothing in the world equal to an understanding of those from whom you differ. It is beautifully put. Your good offices and those of the Mahatma are always available in this sphere. May I say I am grateful for the effort which, as you say in your letter, has already made?

 

“But for the purpose it is not necessary that Mr. Nehru should be induced to give me a copy of his book. If he were to give a copy to each person that stands in his circle of acquaintance at the distance at which I stand, he should give away thousands. What he would not do in the natural course, he need not be made to do. He would’nt hesitate to act on Mahatma’s suggestion. But in the Corner of his mind the thought might occur: ‘Why am I asked to do this?’

 

“I won’t labour it any more. My request still is that you forbear to write.”

 

The episode brings out more than one fine lesson to us. In the first place a sensitive mind, as Sastri’s, would rather risk allowing a misunderstanding to continue than permit an out of the way attempt to reconcile two persons to something like an understanding. Again, the part played by the Mahatma shows how much more human he had been to have felt that a prejudice lurking in the heart of one good friend should not be neglected to the extent of causing pain for long in that of the other, after a common friend like himself had come to know of it. More than all, the way Sastri had written the letter to Mahadev Desai with poise and delicacy proves a worthy example to follow for many, who fall easy victims to irritation or imbalance, the moment something not of their own choice or liking has taken place.

 

Many of Sastri’s admirers too are not aware, we can assure the public, of the personal contacts he had developed with many foreign friends or the extent of that intimacy or understanding which found itself in some of the letters now disclosed to us. For instance, few alone of his many close friends were aware, while he was living, of the very affectionate relationship that existed between him and the artistes-couple, Moshe and Eva, in England. “You gave me” he writes, “you and Eva, the most affectionate, charming, noble home I have had or am likely to have. Remember where I write from. I am very happy here. Still Eva and you gave me a vast deal which I miss and miss sorely.” The letter was sent from his Mylapore home, but what a home he always would cherish more is revealed in that letter. He loved an intellectual company but at the same time he was free from inhibitions on grounds of convention and tradition, if they tried to constrict his free flow of conversation or companionship.

 

A mind as his could not be sustained long on anything but the highest in literature. If he was an unswerving follower of English literature, it never stood in the way of his appreciating our own, whether in Sanskrit or Tamil. He was even a bit partial to Sanskrit, when it came to a question of his final evaluation of permanent literature. His musings on the Ramayana which were delivered in the form of lectures towards his last years and afterwards published in book-form, give the proof direct to his unshakeable belief in, “the Ramayana...to be the most without a rival in the world’s literature. Whether we judge by the grandeur of the theme, by the variety of characters portrayed, by the tone of its idealism or by the appeal that it makes to the devout heart, it ranks amongst the noblest monuments of the poetic genius.” However much one may think that the appreciation of the Ramayana by itself is not a conclusive test of one’s capacity to love great literature, still it is an unfailing test of anyone’s deepening spirit to have felt, as Sastri had, about the epic.

 

There may be readers among friends who may not relish the chapter dealing with ‘Sidelights’ of his character and inclinations of which some details are provided by Sri Kodanda Rao, as occasional departures from convention in the matter of food and other habits. To some readers these may lack justification to find a place in a book of this kind treating of his political life alone, or even for that matter in any account of his life, which was one of unsullied reputation and lofty principles. But they have certainly a place here for the very reason a biography is not a panegyric but an assessment of a great hero by another.

 

On the whole, the book satisfies the desideratum that ‘the aim of biography’ in the words of Sir Sidney Lee, ‘is the truthful transmission of personality.’ Sri Kodanda Rao has, in the light of his own accurate diaries as well as many other documentary materials, traced the history of the political events in which Sastri had himself made history no doubt by his labours and statesmanship. Sri Kodanda Rao may have occasionally let fall a slip in a statement of a fact or failed to tell quite as exactly as some of Sastri’s other friends whose particular knowledge on a point of actual happening may be a bit more correct than his. But it will not, it is hoped, take away the intrinsic merit and value of a clear, correct and conspicuous judgment on a life of such noble and exemplary conduct, approached with a sense of veneration, yet with a self-imposed restriction, to express nothing untrue or unconscionable according to the writer’s obvious purpose of giving the world an enduringly faithful picture.

 

* The Rt. Hon’ble Srinivasa Sastri: A Political Biography by P. Kodanda Rao. Asia Publishing House, Bombay 1. Rs. 28.
