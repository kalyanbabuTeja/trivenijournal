REVIEWS

 

Tribute to a Titan V. Krishnaswami Aiyar by K. Chandrasekharan (Printed at Triveni Press, Masulipatam. A. P. Pages 40. Price One Rupee).

 

Public memory being proverbially short, leaders even of this generation tend to be forgotten soon after their death. We have the famous example of Subhas Bose, who was the darling of the youth in his time, but whose name is hardly known to the students of today. Then what about the statesmen and national leaders who died more than half-a-century ago? There is little material available to the general reader on their life and work. Biography seems to be the weakest link in contemporary Indian writing and Mr. Chandrasekharan who has to his credit a full-length biography of his illustrious father in Tamil, has done well in preparing a brief outline in English for the benefit of the common reader, on the Occasion of his birth centenary earlier this year. It gives a brief but comprehensive and factual account of the great man’s life and work. He had a wide range of and his achievements were many-sided.

 

Sir P. S. Sivaswami Aiyar’s tribute, quoted in this booklet, aptly sums up Krishnaswami Aiyar’s legal personality: “Taking all things together, in subtlety he might not have been superior to Bhashyam Iyengar; in quickness of perception, he might not have been superior to Sir S. Subramania Aiyar; in learning he might not have been superior to Mr. Sundara Aiyar and in eloquence he might not have been superior to Mr. Norton; but taking all in all, there was no one who combined all the essentials of an advocate as Mr. Krishnaswami Aiyar.” Corning as they did from one who was a stickler for accuracy, these words can hardly improved upon as a contemporary assessment. This was but one of the many facets of the diamond.

 

In the political field his ties were closest with Gokhale, whom he admired and resembled in many ways. A moderate by conviction, he was perhaps an extremist by temperament and could get excited on the need for not getting excited. Anecdotes are related by some of his younger contemporaries describing how he often lost his temper with his friends and co-workers on the merit of keeping a cool head and not losing one’s temper. This is not very surprising as there are other instances of great men still happily alive who find it harder to control their tempers than to maintain a balance of mind. It is but a curious reflection on the tremendous vitality of the man in each case.

 

As a political leader, Krishnaswami Aiyar was resourceful and imaginative, of course, within the framework of constitutional methods. He was a man of vision who was able to look far ahead of his times. The author mentions how the Sankaracharya of Kamakoti Peetham “referred to Krishnaswami Aiyar’s munificence and projections of mind in three distinct directions, namely, the cure of the body, the welfare of the mind and the economic security of the individual. The Ayurvedic College and Dispensary catered to the needs of the body, the Sanskrit College to the requirements of the soul and the (Indian) Bank to the wherewithal for keeping both body and soul in tact.”

 

Some of these references and quotations culled by the author from a possible mine of information at his disposal only serve to whet the reader’s appetite. He would indeed be doing a distinct service to the English-reading public in this country (many of whom might not know Tamil) if he were to bring out a more detailed work which could take its due place in Indian biography.

 

Red Hibiscus (Bengal life in fiction) by Padmini Sen Gupta (Asia Publishing House. Pp. 165. Price Rs. 6).

 

Perhaps, one of the most typical of Bengal’s flowers is the Red Hibiscus which the author has aptly chosen for the title of her novel of life in Bengal, which can represent the rest of India itself in a general way. This flower is evidently very common in town and village, beloved of women and used in the worship of Kali, but the colour is not without its sombre associations of impending danger and ultimate success, in popular sentiment.

 

All the elements thus evoked are present or at least sought to be presented in some degree in the story set in a small town in Bengal at the beginning of the Second World War. Sita Das comes of a respectable upper middle class family with a record of patriotic service. A young unsophisticated student at the fag-end of her college career, she falls, head over heels, in love with Nirmal Chatterjee, a handsome officer in the I. A. F., who only proves a philanderer with stronger attractions elsewhere. In her disillusionment she had to reconcile herself, not too willingly, to an arranged marriage with Santosh Bose, a serious-minded college professor, fresh from Cambridge. She could find nothing basically wrong with the well-meaning husband, but she comes to feel that she had been missing the thrill of life in the placid home with a staid husband who seemed to take her for granted and a peevish mother-in-law who would not. Her first baby is still-born and this experience pulls her down further in her dejection. But this serves as a turning-point too. The husband becomes more ardent and imaginative and the mother-in-law more sympathetic. The ordeal she underwent in the Hindu-Muslim riots brings the family closer together still and the dawn of national independence finds them happier than ever before, basking in mutual love and understanding.

 

There are quite a few other characters in the novel–Dr. Bimal, the gentle and affectionate brother of Sita, her vivacious sister-in-law, their sprightly kids, Koumudini, the gossipy well-meaning cook, and the noble widowed mother. The struggles of Rasmi, the servant woman, and her family are a kind of side string to the main theme, though they are not properly woven into the fabric of the novel as a whole. The story is well told, with a sense of subdued realism, but it does not somehow quite succeed in gripping the reader’s attention. The conflict suggested in more than one place is not worked out into a good drama and the realities of life are not probed deeply enough to make a lasting impression. But the novel is readable and the narrative has an easy flow, though there is little in it that haunts the memory or fires the imagination.

–D. ANJANEYULU

 

Sankara’s Hymns to Siva (Sivanandalahari) by Dr. T. M. P. Mahadevan. Published by Ganesh & Co. (P) Ltd., Madras-17. Cr. 8-vo. Pp. 132 + XVI. Price Rs. 4.

 

Dr. Mahadevan’s latest is a book on Sankara’s Sivanandalahari. Adi Sankara is known only as a Jnana Yogi, but in his Sivanandalahari, we get a new personality of Sankara. Here he is a devotee, pure and simple. He pours forth his heart in the hundred beautiful verses and in the process we get a brilliant analysis of the concept of Bhakti. The hymns are full of what is familiarly known as the nayaka-nayaki bhava (or what Dr. Mahadevan calls ‘the bridal mysticism’, in his inimitable manner) an allegory associated with the Alwars and Nayanmars and to some extent with the sufis. The combination of deep piety with high literature is nothing new to us, but Dr. Mahadevan has heightened the value of the book by giving short but brilliant commentaries on the philosophical and literary quality of each of the hundred verses. One example is his commentary on verse No. 69. It is quite original and remarkable.

 

Dr. Mahadevan has given the Devanagari script of the verses, transliteration, translation and commentary. There are also parallel citations from similar works like the Narada Bhakti Sutras, the Gita, the works of the Tamil savants etc.

 

It will be of great service to the cause of literature and Philosophy if the present volume is followed up by a similar book on Soundaryalahari.

 

Kalidasa Samiksha. Published by Sri Venkateswara University, Tirupati. Demy 8-vo size. Pp. 122. Price Rs. 4.

 

Lovers of literature who are familiar with the large volume of criticism around Shakespeare will be delighted to see the present book on Kalidasa brought out by the Venkateswara University. The book contains the proceedings of a seminar on Kalidasa held under the auspices of the Sanskrit department of the University in January 1962. Illuminating Presidential speeches, one in Sanskrit by the erudite Kasi Krishnacharya, and thought-provoking lectures on the various aspects of Kalidasa by scholars, make the book eminently readable.

 

Prof. Rama Rao discusses the date of Kalidasa. His conclusion, assigning Kalidasa to the later half of the 5th century and the beginning of the 6th century, is in consonance with the view of scholars that Kalidasa must have been a contemporary of Aryabhatta. The use of the term ‘Jamitra,’ by Kalidasa in Kumarasambhava and also by Aryabhatta, a term believed to have been borrowed from the Greek astronomers, is the basis for this theory. Dr. Subbarao’s  paper on the un-paninian usages of Kalidasa is scholarly and novel. There are scholars who believe that these peculiarities in usages show that Kalidasa belonged to a period when Sanskrit was probably a spoken language and when Panini’s grammar had not taken firm roots in Sanskrit literature. There are also papers on the Grihya ceremonials in Kalidasa’s works (by Dr. Varadachari), the etymologies offered by KalidasA in his own words (by Sri Krishna Sarma) and Kalidasa’s choice of metres (by Sri Anjaneya Sarma). Sri Gopalaswamy’s paper on the Upanishadic thoughts in Kalidasa is an original contribution. Sri Subrahmanya Sastry’s ‘Psychology of Love according to Kalidasa’ is highly interesting and brings to our mind parallels from Shakespeare, Keats and Kamban. The book ends with a small but brilliant summary by Prof. Lakshmikantam on the poetic genius of Kalidasa. It is a remarkable appreciation and correct assessment of the secret of Kalidasa’s art.

 

It is to be hoped that the present brochure will give a fillip to more such efforts so that we can also have a literature of criticism and appreciation on Kalidasa as interesting and impressive as the literature on Shakespeare.

–T. C. A. RAMANUJAM

 

Studies in Bengali Poetry by Humayun Kabir (Bharatiya Vidya Bhavan, Chaupatty, Bombay-7. Pages 124. Price Two Rupees).

 

Time was, not very long ago, when it used to be said that what Bengal thought today the rest of the country would think tomorrow. It was particularly true of the political and intellectual spheres. After the famine and partition and other adversities of fortune this no longer holds good in politics. In the realm of pure thought, however, and of literature, Bengal still gives ample evidence of being more sensitive to new world trends than the other linguistic regions. The booklet under review, which is one of the latest in the Bhavan’s Book University series, is a reprint of Prof. Kabir’s articles originally written over a quarter of a century ago. They were first published in the Bengali periodical ‘Chaturanga’ and in their English version they appeared some ten years later in the ‘India’ monthly. They seek to understand the literary situation in terms of the social phenomena, political forces and economic factors, which make it possible. With the true instinct of a philosopher who goes to the fundamentals, the author traces the role of the Bhakti movement in giving rise to Vaishnavite lyricism of the impact of Muslim invasion on the shape of the language in relaxing the grip of Sanskrit forms, and of the British conquest in the growth of a new social and intellectual outlook based in the dignity of the human individual and the supremacy of reason. Among the great names, who are like landmarks in the growth of modern Bengali poetry, enough space is devoted to Michael Madhusudan Dutt, Bankim Chandra Chatterji and Rabindranath Tagore. In the post-Tagore period, only Nazrul Islam is discussed at some length. The account needs to be brought up-todate. Though some of the conclusions may be controversial, it is a model for originality of treatment, novelty in outlook and lucidity of exposition. There is a felicity of expression that invests the study with a compelling charm.

–D. ANJANEYULU

 

TELUGU

 

C. P. Brown (1798–1884) Raw Material for Biography by Dr. K. Virabhadra Rao. (Andhra Pradesh Sahitya Akademi, Hyderabad. Pp. 279. Rs. 5)

 

Dr. C. R. Reddi by G. Venkatasubbaiah (Desikavita Mandali, Vijayawada-2, A. P. Pages 290. Price Rs. 8)

 

Though there is no obvious comparison between C. P. Brown and C. R. Reddi in their life and work, it is beyond any doubt that both of them deserve to be remembered with gratitude by lovers of the Telugu language and literature. Any attempt to perpetuate their memory is therefore welcome, especially in the form in which it is sought to be done here. One rescued many of the classics of Telugu poetry from decay and oblivion and preserved for us reliable versions of their texts. The other gave us a new way of looking at some of the popular classics. One was a pioneer in grammar, lexicography and even the art of printing. The other was a pioneer in literary criticism in its modern sense. One gave a new status and sanction to the use of spoken Telugu in written works. The other felt that the spoken dialect changing from district to district, it was best to stick to the bookish form in the written word. Both had a classical background (though of different kinds) and a modern outlook and stand above the common run of scholars by the spark of originality that lit their work.

 

But, it is out of the question that C. P. Brown’s achievement was by far the more substantial and lasting of the two. That was certainly due, in a large measure, to his spirit of dedication and capacity for concentration and his many-sided interests in the field of scholarship. It is not always that one finds a stranger to an Indian language taking to it with such minded devotion as Brown did in the case of Telugu. He learnt it, to start with, as part of his official duties as a junior official the Ceded Districts and Northern Circars. But he began to love it in course of time and his whole life and fortune were spent in the digging up of its treasures of literature. That a modern Telugu scholar should devote more than ten years, off and on, to a study of the life and work of this great savant of the last century is encouraging indeed. It is also evident that quite a lot of hard work has gone into the collection of material for this biography.

 

But it is just as well to remember that the collection of data is but a part of the biographer’s burden. And material in itself, rich and useful as it is, does not constitute a biography, any more than tons of brick and mortar do a shapely building. A good biographer must have a point of view that decides the pattern of his work, he must have a sense of form and of style and thirdly he must know what to put in and what to keep out. The material presented here, partly in English and partly in Telugu, was possibly collected in the course of the author’s research on a thesis which had since been published in book-form under the title “The Influence of English on Telugu literature.” The initial chapter in English entitled ‘C. P. Brown and his literary pursuits’ seems to have appeared in ‘Swatantra’ weekly as an article some years ago and is presumably incorporated here as it is. As for the regular text, it looks as if there are more words within quotation marks than without (which incidentally used to be a matter for congratulation in our college days) and as many in the foot-notes to every page as above the demarkation line. And then there are almost as many passages in English as in Telugu! It looks as if the real choice was between producing a book of 100 pages in English or of the same length in Telugu, but by some admirable legerdemain of the art of book-making he has just pulled off a substantial book of over 250 pages which is part English and part Telugu!

 

If the best part of the book is only to give what Brown wrote in his articles and notes and what his numerous correspondents wrote to him, one is at a loss to imagine what the present author is here for. There is here more incorporation than interpretation, more documentation than discussion. The material gathered is unexceptionable, but the biography promised is yet to be.

 

The present biography of Dr. C. R. Reddi, perhaps the first of its kind in Telugu or English, serves to illustrate the point that the varied and picturesque life of a man of genius need not make an excellent biography in itself, if the writer does not know how to do it. It also highlights the fact that biography is yet a new branch of literature in most of the Indian languages. We have fulsome eulogies, yes, and resounding panegyrics, but good balanced biographies, not yet.

 

In his incisive foreword to this book, Mr. Narla has asked the author (who seems to admire Dr. Reddi on this side idolatry) to try to ask himself why a man of such brilliant endowments of the mind, had failed to make a mark in life in keeping with his distinction as a speaker, writer, poet and critic. Narla puts his finger on the right spot when he traces the failure to certain inherent contradictions in Reddi’s own character and mental make-up. A Plato, with the propensities of a Puck, would he not be an absurdity indeed in any walk of life?

 

The author, however, goes on in his own self-complacent way to complain against a society and a province which did not find the proper opportunities for a man of Dr. Reddi’s gifts and attainments. A genius, it is said, not only snatches the opportunities where they arise, but creates new ones where there are none. Also the account is tolerable where Reddi’s work in Telugu literature is concerned. But where it relates to his work as an educationist and his speeches and writings in English and other subtleties on the floor of the House or outside it, the author finds himself frankly out of his depths.

–D. ANJANEYULU
