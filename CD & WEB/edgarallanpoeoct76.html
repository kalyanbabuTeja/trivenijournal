EDGAR ALLAN POE AND HIS THEORY OF THE

SHORT STORY

 

DR M. C. SAXENA

 

            As a special literary type the short story is of a recent origin. It began primarily in America with Edgar Allan Poe. It is a peculiarly American form. In England there was hardly any encouragement to a writer of short fiction at the turn of the 19th century. Book publishers desired novels, preferably in many volumes, because these were in great demand. Moreover, the novels suitable for the sensibilities of novel reading ladies, whose appetite for reading novels dealing with more or less innocently amorous relationships among the higher classes was voracious, were in great demand. The periodicals also preferred long fiction which they could serialize.

 

            The authors who turned to writing short fiction were also novelists who thought short story-writing to be an exercise in the novelist’s craft. The 19th century English novel, says W. B. Harris “from Scot onwards depended essentially on the creation of a little world, whether on the Scottish boarder, in the labyrinth of London, or in a quiet cathedral town, and a tracing of the life of its key characters through a varied sequence of incidents and moods”.1

 

            Such a fictional world cannot be created in a short story. A novel can have a panoramic view, but in a short story the panoramic is likely to dull the edge of interest the plot may have. If one looks at the short stories of Dickens, Collins, Mrs. Gaskell, Trollope and Thackery–all of them were primarily novelists–one can discover a “diffuseness that displays itself in lack of economy and un­certainty of tone”,2 while the outstanding characteristic of a short story is ‘unity of effect.’

 

            It is an accepted assumption of Poe scholarship that Poe’s primary aim as a writer of tales was to create a ‘unity of effect.’ It should be remembered that Poe was a journalist, and his critical opinions are scattered all over his reviews and other critical writings. Although he never had time to formulate a systematic theory of the ‘unity of effect’ his opinions are sufficiently well articulated. Poe was the first conscious artist who tried to set standards for the short story. His statement that ‘unity of effect’ is necessary in a tale constituted an innovation.

 

            The first extent reference to the concept of ‘unity of effect’ occurs in Poe’s review of Tales of Peerage and Peasantry “edited” by Lady Dacre. He praises the ‘unity of effect’ achieved by Lady Dacre:

 

            An everyday writer would have ended a story of continued sorrow and suffering, with a bright gleam of unalloyed happiness and sunshine–thus destroying, at a single blow, that indispensable unity which has been rightly called the unity of effect, and throwing down, as it were, in a paragraph what perhaps, an entire volume has been trying to establish.3

 

            The next reference to the theory of ‘unity of effect’ is in his review of the poems of Mrs. Sigourney. Mrs. Sigourney was in the habit of prefixing epigrams to her poems. This gave Poe an excuse to dwell upon the principle of ‘unity of effect:’

 

            In poems of magnitude the mind of the reader is not, at all times, enabled to include in one comprehensive survey the proportions and proper adjustments of the whole. He is pleased–if at all–with particular passages; and the sum of his pleasure is compounded by the sums of the pleasurable sensations inspired by these individual passages during the progress of perusal. But in pieces of less extent–like the poems of Mrs. Sigourney–the pleasure is unique, in the proper acceptation of the term–the understanding is employed, without difficulty in the contemplation of the picture as a whole and thus its effect will depend, in a very great degree, upon the perfection of its finish, upon the nice adaptation of its constituent parts, and especially upon what is rightly termed by Schlegel, “the unity or totality of interest.”... Mrs. Sigourney is utterly at variance with this unity. By the initial motto–often a very one–we are either put in possession of the subject of the poem; or some hint, historic fact, or suggestion is thereby afforded, not included in the body of the article, which, without the suggestion, would be utterly incomprehensible. In the latter case, while peruing the poem, the reader must revert; in mind at least, to the motto for the necessary explanation. In the former, the poem is being a mere paraphrase of the motto, the interest is divided between the motto and the paraphrase. In either instance the totality of effect is annihilated. 4

 

            The concept remained essentially the same, although Poe shifted emphasis from structural unity to the unity of emotional effect, as the situation demanded. Almost six years later, Poe again repeated this passage – in his review of Longfellow’s Ballads and Other Poems – and the changes he made from this passage are rather insignificant.

 

            Just as Poe condemned the practice of Mrs. Sigourney, Cole­ridge said that nothing that is ‘super added’ only for ornament or decoration can really please in a poem. For Coleridge a legitimate poem is one in which the parts

 

            ...mutually support and explain each other; all in their proportion harmonising with, and supporting the known influence of material arrangement. The philosophical critics of all the ages coincide with the ultimate judgement of all countries, in equally denying the praises of a just poem, on the one hand, to a series of striking lines or distichs each of which absorbing the whole attention of the readers to itself, disjoins it from its context, and makes it a separate whole, instead of an harmonising part; and on the other hand, to an unsustained composition, from which the reader collects rapidly the general results, un attracted by the compo­nent parts. The reader should be carried forward, not merely or chiefly by the mechanical impulse of curiosity, or by a restless desire to arrive at the final solution; but by the pleasurable activity of the mind excited by the attraction of the journey itself. 5

 

            Coleridge used the ‘unity of effect’ in the service of organic unity, while Poe invoked it to demand autonomy for the poem or the tale itself. Schlegel too insisted upon organic unity –­ for him the formal unity of the neo-classicists was of no value. He likened a work of art to life, in which unity is apprehended not by the eye and the ear alone, but by intuition which could produce a joint impression on the mind.

 

            Poe offered Dickens’ tale “The Pawnbroker’s Shop” as an illustration of the concept ‘unity of effect’ and made the claim that each sentence in that tale gave a fuller view of the picture Dickens was trying to paint.

 

            The concept ‘unity of effect’ received its final crystallization in his review of Hawthorne’s Twice Told Tales and “The Philosophy of Composition.” The theory of ‘unity of effect’ was the governing principle of Poe’s theory of short story and it received a philosophical reorientation in Eureka.

 

            As a reviewer for the Southern Literary Messenger Poe had to deal more with novels than with short stories for the simple reason that it was the more popular genre. Although he was prepared to praise individual works highly he did not like the form. Elaborat­ing his theory of ‘unity of effect’ Poe developed his theory of single sitting, and applied it equally to poetry and to fiction:

 

            Were I bidden to say how the highest genius could be more advantageously employed for the best display of its own powers, I should answer without hesitation–in the composition of a rhymed poem, not exceeding in length what might be pursued in an hour. Within this limit alone can the highest order of true poetry exist. We need only here say, upon this topic, that in almost all classes of composition, the unity of effect or impression is a point of the greatest importance. It is clear, however, that this unity cannot be thoroughly preserved in productions whose perusal cannot be completed in one sitting. (VII, 29-30)

 

            According to Poe the effect of the totality or unity can be created in a sufficiently brief piece. This leads him to call a long poem a paradox: “All high excitements are necessarily transient. Thus a long poem is a paradox. And without unity of impression, the deepest effects cannot be brought about. Epics were the offsprings of an imperfect sense of art, and their reign is no more.” (VII, 30) However, too much brevity also mars the effect:

 

            A poem too brief may produce a vivid, but never an intense or enduring impression. Without a certain continuity of effort – without a certain duration or repetition of purpose ­–the soul is never deeply moved... Extreme brevity will degenerate into epigramatism; but the sin of extreme length is even more unpardonable. (VII, 30)

 

            As a novel cannot be read at a single sitting the immense force derivable from totality is lost. If a work of art is read at more than one sitting, worldly interests “modify, annul or counteract” the impressions the author tries to create; even a simple cessation of reading will, of itself, Poe says, “be sufficient to destroy the unity.” (VII, 31)

 

            The principle of brevity is directed toward the proper manage­ment of the response of the audience or reader toward the unity of impression. Brevity, for Poe, was in direct ratio to the intensity of the desired effect.

 

            A qualitative subordinate of the theory of ‘unity of effect’ is the principle of originality. It is there for the proper management of the audience psychology to the unity of impression. We are reminded at once of Poe’s remark that a to originate is to carefully, patiently, and understandingly to combine.” (VII, 102) The originality of a piece of writing is to be judged not by the subject-­matter or theme, but by the manner in which the writer handles the elements he chooses or which are available to him. Poe speci­fically warns against using material that is absolutely novel. Originality, thus understood, he says, “tasks and starts the intellect, and so brings into undue action the faculties to which in the lighter literature, we least appeal.” (VII, 23) “The true originality,” he writes, “true in respect of its purposes–is that which, in bringing out the half formed, the reluctant, or the unexpressed fancies of mankind,...thus combines with the pleasurable effect of apparent novelty, a real egotistic delight...the reader feels and intensely enjoys it as really novel, as absolutely original with the writer and himself.” (VII, 23) For Poe originality is based not on the novelty of the subject but upon novelty of presentation and this novelty derives from a careful and deliberate attempt to produce an effect.

 

            In “How to write a Blackwood Article” Poe talks about the ‘intensities.’ These ‘intensities’ and ‘unity of effect’ cannot be attained in a novel for the simple reason that a novel cannot be read at a single sitting. In the interval that is naturally caused between the first and the second sitting many extraneous interests impinge on the mind of the reader. It is only the short tale which “affords unquestionably the fairest field for the exercise of the loftiest talent, which cannot be afforded by the wide domains of mere prose.” (VII, 29)

 

            A brief tale can be admired for its ‘unity of effect’ while a novel can only be admired for the brilliance of its detached passages without reference to the whole work. Poe was impassioned with the talk of ‘sustained effort’:

 

            Commonsense, in time to come, may possibly insist upon measuring a work of art rather by the object it fulfils, by the impression it makes, than by the time it took to fulfil the object, or by the extent of ‘sustained effort’ which becomes necessary to produce the impression. (VII, 27)

 

            Only a favoured few, if any, Poe believed, were possessed of the sculptural taste needed to take in a novel’s ‘totality of beauty.’

 

            Emerson R. Marks considers Poe’s antipathy to novel as one of his serious shortcomings. He says, “Poe was curiously blind to the aesthetic value of a complex whole that resides in a writer’s control of a great mass of material”.6 Marks, however, forgets that Poe was a journalist who was thinking in terms of mass audience. He was in search of an art form appropriate for a monthly magazine. One can agree with Robert D. Jacobs that “Poe’s concern about the reading time of a prose narrative represents journalistic expediency as well as practical psychology”. 7

 

            Poe not only tried to establish the short narrative, but between the short poem and the tale he preferred the later as it was capable of a variety of treatment.

 

            The writer of a short tale, in short, may bring to his theme a vast variety of modes and inflections of thought or expres­sion (the ratiocinative, for example, the sarcastic or the humorous) which are not only antagonistic to the nature of the poem, but absolutely forbidden by one of its most peculiar and indispensable adjuncts; we allude, of course, to rhythm. (VII, 32)

 

            The subjects that are suitable to the tales of effect, e. g., terror or passion cannot be treated in a poem which is suitable only for beauty: “the author who aims at the purely beautiful in a prose tale is labouring at a great disadvantage.” (VII, 32)

 

            Poe was not only a literary theorist but a practitioner of the genre, short story. It was essential that he should reduce his theory to practice. After emphasising the ‘unity of effect’ and elaborating the theory of single sitting he continues:

 

            A skilful literary artist has constructed a tale. If wise, he has fashioned his thoughts to accommodate his incidents; but having conceived, with deliberate care, a certain unique or single effect to be wrought out, he then invents such events as may best aid him in establishing this preconceived effect. If his very initial sentence tends not to the upbringing of this effect, then he has, failed in his first step. In the whole composition there should be no word written, of which the tendency, direct or indirect is not to the one pre-established design. And by such means, with such case and skill, a picture is at length painted which leaves in the mind of him who contemplates it with a kindred art, a sense of the fullest satisfaction. The idea of the tale has been presented unblemished, because undisturbed; and this is an end unattainable by            a novel. (VII, 31-32)

 

            A careful perusal of the above statement will clear any doubt about priorities. ‘Incidents’ are created to bring about the desired effect. The structure, according to Poe, is simply a means to an end – an end that a creative artist should always keep in mind is the ‘effect.’ As a necessary adjunct to ‘incident’ comes ‘tone.’ After the creative artist has chosen a desired effect, he should consi­der “whether it can be wrought out by incident or tone – whether by ordinary incidents and peculiar tone, or the converse, by peculiarity both of incident or tone...” (V, 32)

 

            Thus according to Poe, incident or tone is essential to any work of art desiring ‘unity of effect’ and a predominance of one over the other gives coherence to a work of art. In any work of art in which ‘incidents’ predominate, the unity of effect is achieved through unity of plot.

 

            Poe was always particular about plot. He was conscious of the fact that many good novels did not have any plot at all. In the review, of Cooper’s Wyandotte he wrote:

 

            Plot, however, is at best an artificial effect requiring, like music, not only a natural bias, but long cultivation of taste for its full appreciation; some of the finest narratives in the world “Gil Bias” and “Robinson Crusoe” for example have been written without its employment. (VI, 2)

 

            He, however, adds a word of caution. The absence of plot cannot be regarded as defect, yet “its judicious use in all cases aiding and in no case injuring other effects, must be regarded as of a very high order of merit.” (VI, 2) Nevertheless “A mere succession of incidents, even the most spirited, will no more constitute a plot than a multiplication of zeros, even the most infinite, will result in the production of unit.” (VI, 210) In the review of Bulwer’s Night and Morning he developed his theory of plot:

 

The word plot; as commonly accepted, conveys but an in­definite meaning. Most persons think of it as a simple complexity; and into this error even so fine a critic as Augustus William Schlegel has obviously fallen, when he confounds its idea with that of the mere intrigue in which the Spanish drama of Cerventes and Calderon abound. But the greatest involution of incident will not result in plot; which properly defined, is that in which no part can be displaced without ruin to the whole. It may be described as a building so dependently con­structed, that to change the position of a single brick is to overthrow the entire fabric. 8

 

            This definition of plot is important because it is an improvement upon Aristotle. To Aristotle, the plot, being an imitation of an action, must imitate one action and that a whole, the structural union of the parts being such, that if anyone of them is misplaced or replaced, the whole would be disjointed and disturbed. A thing whose presence or absence makes no visible difference is not an organic part of the whole. Poe exaggerates this requirement by emphasising that any displacement or removal of a part would ruin the whole. In the “American Drama” he said that a plot “is perfect only in as much as we shall find ourselves unable to detach from it or disarrange any single incident involved, without destruction to the mass.” (VI, 210-11) The artist who worked with a preconceived design, was like an architect who first drew the blueprint and then started the actual construction. This conception of plot found philosophic expression in Eureka where Poe calls the universe as the plot of God.

 

            Poe appreciates Robert R. Bird’s Shepperd Lee because “its incidents are well conceived, and related with force, brevity and a species of directness....” (VII, 88-80) On the other hand, he finds fault with Wyandotte: “As for plot, there has been no attempt of the kind. The tale is a mere succession of events, scarcely anyone of which has any necessary dependence upon any other.” (VII, I)

 

            Tone also plays a significant part in establishing the ‘unity of effect’. According to Poe, Bird violated tone in Shepperd Lee. Norman Forester suggests that Poe regards tone” as the word itself suggests–as the equivalent in prose for the music in verse, an element in which the theme floats and is carried to the culminating effects”.9

 

            Poe declares that the opening sentence should strike the key­note of the story. Unity of effect being; the object, the creative artist has failed if “his very initial sentence tends not to the upbringing of this effect.” (VII, 31) The very initial sentence should prepare the reader for the final sentence. What is indicated at the beginning should be fulfilled at the end. This is the key to the structural coherence of the composition. In “The Philosophy of Composition” Poe asserts: “It is with the denoue­ment constantly in view that we can give a plot its indispensable air of consequence, or causation, by making the incidents and especially the tone at all points, tend to the development of the intention.” (VI, 31) Haying the denouement constantly in view the author should write the story backwards. Godwin is famed to have written Caleb Williams backwards. Godwin’s preface to Fleetwood asserted the advantage of carrying back the invention from the conclusion to the beginning: “An entire unity of plot would be the result; and the unity of spirit and interest in the tale truly considered, gives it a powerful hold on the reader which can scarcely be generated with equal success in any other way”.10 These words reinforce the way suggested by Poe.

 

            The unfolding of plot brings about effect. Poe’s ideas of effect have been taken by many critics as some sort of contrived riddle. Certainly, in some cases Poe seems to think that stories are designed to fool the unsuspecting reader. But Poe was usually not playing games with his readers. He desired a bond of sympathy with them.

 

            Poe was a highly conscious artist who was confident of achieving in practice the concepts which he put forward in his critical writings. He said: “...the theory and practice are so much one, that the former implies or includes the latter. A theory is as good as such, in proportion to its reducibility to practise. If the practice fails, it is because the theory is imperfect.” (VII, 40) Critics generally agree with Poe and maintain that his theory and practice are inseparable.
