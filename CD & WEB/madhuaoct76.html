MADHUA

 

JAYASHANKAR PRASAD

 

(Translated from the original in Hindi by RAVI S. VARMA)

 

            “You talk of drinking, Sir, I have not even touched it for the last seven days. Yes, it is the seventh day (of abstinence), my lord.”

 

            “Oh, you tell a lie, your clothes are still reeking of it.”

 

            “That ... ...that happened a few days ago; more than seven days I presume.......Ah! such a short span of time. I was pouring the bottle in the dark, it spilled on my clothes and I could not taste it. What more should I tell you ... Please believe me. Seven days – Yes, full seven days and not a single drop has trickled down my throat.”

 

            Thakur Sardar Singh burst into laughter. His son studied at Lucknow and sometimes he would also come to stay with him. He was fond of listening to stories and anecdotes and after a long search had found this drunkard. He would visit Thakur Saheb at night, at noon and sometimes in the morning and enter­tain him with his interesting stories.

 

            “I promise you a drink today” said the Thakur with a smile.

 

            “I need’nt tell a lie, Sir, today I shall drink all I get. For nothing have I spent these seven days on gram and dry food.”

 

            “Strange! Even after seven days’ starvation you want to squander money on a drink rather than enjoying good meals; well....”

 

            “Sir, a tick of pleasure is better than a long but miserable life. Dry days can easily be skipped under the lingering intoxication for a tick.”

 

            “Well; what have you been doing the whole day today?”

 

            “Listen, if you want to. The morning was foggy and wrapped the sun all round like my smoky blanket. Both of us lay hiding our faces.”

 

            “What made you hide your face?” asked the Thakur with a smile.

 

            “Not a single drop had trickled down my throat. Then how could I dare show my face! The sun at last came out of the fog at noon, but I was miserable. I got up reluctantly and can’t describe how painful did I feel washing my hands and face. I had a little money with me, so the teeth refused to chew gram and ground together. I went to the shop of “paratha wala,” ate slowly and warmed myself up. Then I strolled up and down the bank of the Gomati till it grew dark and started drizzling. This made me run faster and seek shelter in your mansion.”

 

            “Well, was the story of the shepherd you told me the other day in which Asaf-uddaula had fined the skirt of the shepherd’s daughter with pearls, in return for the popped corn, true?”

 

            “True; that poor girl began to eat them hungrily and then spat them out. She started crying. Big people sometimes play such merciless jokes.”

 

            Thakur Saheb laughed his head off and then restraining his emotions asked him, “What do you mean by bigness? A pau­per is a pauper. The foolish girl. Had she ever seen pearls she would not have started chewing them up. It appears that your stories are invariably very pathetic.”

 

            “Do you know the stories of the troubled life of the princes or the painful stories of the unrequited love of the luckless begums of the seragoli, and nothing else? If you can tell me a humorous and rollicking story, I can stand you a high class drink.”

 

            “My lord, those golden days of the Nawab’s rule, the sallies of the rich, the painful cries of the afflicted, the begums who died by inches in the seragoli of which I have heard from the elderly people haunt me. I shed silent tears on their sad plight. The rich become paupers. Many a proud head falls to the dust, but the world is mad, and to escape its madness I started drinking. Otherwise, Sir, why should have I contracted this habit?”

 

            Thakur Saheb was dozing, the charcoal in the brazier was still red hot and the drunkard shivered with cold. He stretched his hands out to warm. Thakur Saheb started in his sleep and said, “Well, you can go now. I feel sleepy. A rupee is lying there, take it and send Lallu.”

 

            The drunkard picked the coin and slinked out of the room. Lallu was Thakur’s steward. When he came to his small cabin near the gate he heard tender sobs of someone and stopped there to make out what it was.

 

            “Why do you cry, O pig? Kunwar Saheb gave you only two kicks, he did not kill you.” Lallu was shouting at someone. One or two hiccoughs and continuous sobs could be heard in reply. He scolded more harshly, “Madhua, go and sleep. Don’t play a coquette or I shall thrash you. Understand?”

 

            The drunkard listened quietly. The boy’s wails and sobs increased. Then he heard, “Clear away from here or must I thrash you?”

 

            The fear-stricken boy emerged from the cabin, his small and fair face besmeared with tears still trickling down his eyes. He lovingly wiped his face and brought him out of the gate. It was ten O’clock and the night was chilly. They walked silently; the little simple heart having received the drunkard’s sympathy became silent. They turned into a narrow lane when the boy again sobbed. He scolded him, “why do you cry now, child?”

 

            “I have not eaten anything for the whole day.”

 

            “You have eaten nothing! You live with such a rich man and yet did not get anything to eat for the whole day.”

 

            “I was just telling this to the steward. I am thrashed every­day but today I had nothing to eat. I carried the Kunwar’s overcoat for the whole day during the game, came back at seven and attended to other chores till nine. I did not find time to knead flour and bake my bread. I went to report this to the steward.” Recounting this tale of misery, the boy suddenly felt the bite of poverty and hunger, and broke into sobs and hiccoughs again.

 

            The drunkard dragged him into the lane, pushed the door of a dirty cell open and entered it along with the boy. Groping in the dark he lit a kerosene lamp and started searching something under his worn out blanket. He found a piece of a ‘paratha.’ “You can munch it, while I get something more to fill your belly,” said he, handing the piece to the boy. “Listen child, don’t cry now. If you cry I’ll beat you. I don’t like anyone crying. You fool, do you want to make me cry....?”

 

            He ran out of the lane with the rupee coin still in his palm...Half a bottle of country liquor for twelve annas and tea for two annas...and ‘pakori’ for two annas...no no...‘potato and pea’...Leave it aside, I should get meat for all the four annas. But this boy? I must fill his belly too. How much will he eat, and what...? I never thought of others’ food or drink. What should I get then? First let me have half a bottle. All these ideas crossed his mind and his eyes were caught by the dazzling electric light. He found himself at the sweetmeat shop. Forgetting to buy half a bottle of liquor nor he began to buy sweetmeats and ‘puris.’ He bought some savoury and spent full one rupee. He almost began to run to get home quickly. Reaching his cell he arranged all the leaf-pots before the boy. The sweet aroma moistened the boy’s throat; he smiled.

 

            Pouring water from the earthen jug, he said, “Naughty fellow, you smile now when the appetising aroma is entering your nostrils. Now eat to your fill and, mind you, don’t cry or I’ll thrash you......”

 

            Together they ate like friends who had met after a long spell of separation. Sleeping in that damp place the boy wrapped him­self in the drunkard’s old coat. The drunkard covered himself with the blanket and muttered. “After seven days I had thought of drinking to my fill today and sleeping soundly but who knows wherefrom this useless chap has cropped up.”

 

            Today for the first time the drunkard realized with a penitent and worried mind the poverty and squalor spread in his cell and that innocent boy sitting there resting his chin on his knees. “Who brought forth cruelty to torture such tender flowers?” He indignant­ly asked himself, “Shall I become a householder with this boy? I’ll luck. I had never thought of this. I was always under the sway of liquor, now why do I feel so much love and affection for this little one? What part is this little fool destined to play in my life? What shall I do now? I must look out for a job, or how else will we make both ends meet? No...I shall drive him away... Let him face the music of the world.”

 

            The boy stretched his body and got up. “Get up and eat something from the night’s leftovers, then go your way,” said the drunkard. “Well, what is your name?”

 

            “Madhua,” replied the little one with a gentle smile. “Shouldn’t I wash my face and hands before starting to eat? And then, where shall I go?”

 

            “Where should I ask him to go? Should I say, go to hell? He has already been in hell-fire till today. Then...?” He was puzzled and silently came out of the hut muttering, “See, O fool, I shall not return. You can live here happily alone...”

 

            Leaving his hut he went to the bank of the Gomati and realized that he had been trying to think a lot of things and had failed to reach any conclusion. He washed his face and hands. The sun was bright and he silently watched the stream. Warming up in the sun he tried to get rid of his worries, then someone called to him “Where had you been gentleman? I am seeing you after ages. I looked for you everywhere......”

 

            The voice appeared very familiar to the drunkard, but he could not place him immediately.

 

            “I am talking to you; don’t you listen? Will you take your grinding machine back or I should throw it away? I have rented a small room and do you think I don’t have anything of mine to keep there?” snarled he.

 

            “Oh you are Ramji, aren’t you? Brother, I had forgotten all about it. Let’s go now, I shall take it back even today,” said the drunkard and thought: “It’s good. I shall sell it and get something for a few days more.”

 

            Ramji had a hurried bath, went to his hut nearby and handed the drunkard his grinding machine avidly and with thanks.

 

            Today he was carrying his old machine after a long time. He reached his hut and saw the little boy sitting quietly. “Have you eaten something?” He charged him haughtily.

 

            “Yes, to my fill, and see there is something left for you also,” replied the boy and filled the hut with his innocent sweet smile. The drunkard had his breakfast silently. “Can this be anything other than an indication of luck? I shall start my work of grinding and get enough to make both ends meet. The same old trouble seems to have fallen on my shoulders. I could easily manage myself telling some stories here and there. Now it will be difficult to run the house without some job on hand.” Then he drank water and asked Madhua, “Where will you like to go now?”

 

            “Nowhere.”

 

            “Have I some treasure that I shall dig up and buy sweets for you?”

 

            “We shall do some work.”

           

            “What can you do.?”

 

            “Anything you like.”

 

            “Well, then you’ll go round with me. I have brought this machine for you and will teach you how to grind. It’s not certain where I shall live. Are you prepared to spend nights under a tree?”

 

            “I can live anywhere but will never serve under that cruel Thakur.” The drunkard fixed his eyes on him and noticed a gleam of firm determination in his little bright eyes.

 

            “Where has this trouble come from?” said he to himself. Now I too shall have to vow to abstain from liquor.”

 

 

            He collected all the necessary things, tied them in a bundle and asked the boy, “What will you carry, the bundle or the machine?”

 

            “Anything you like!”

 

            “Very good, but if your father should sieze me?”

 

            “None will seize you. My father died long ago.”

 

            The drunkard was touched. He picked up the machine and the boy, the bundle. They left the hut and set off on their unknown itinerary.
