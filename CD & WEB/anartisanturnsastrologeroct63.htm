AN ARTISAN TURNS ASTROLOGER

(A Short Story)

 

GORUR RAMASWAMI IYENGAR

Translated from Kannada by K. SAMPATIIGIRI RAO

 

[Sri Gorur Ramaswami Iyengar, M. L. C., is one of the most popular writers in modern Kannada. He has to his credit many sketches, novels and short stories.

 

He is Specially known for his realistic studies of village life which are characterised by gentle humour. He is also a well-known public worker interested in Khadi, Village Industries and other items of Gandhiji’s constructive programme.

 

The story which follows is an English rendering of one of his short stories, taken from the first volume of a collection of his short stories.]

–Editor, Triveni

 

The blacksmith of our village, Veerabhadrachari, is a hefty fellow. He was an adept at many trades–so we called him an all rounder. Of course he was an expert in fashioning iron. There was no hammer that was too heavy for him. He plied the hammer as if it were a feather and what would make others go out of breath and strain their last ounce of energy was child’s play for him. It was wonderful to watch the sparks flying from the bottom of the hammer as it came down with majestic sweep and power to flatten out the red hot metal on the anvil. In the fading light of the evening, boys of the village gathered at his smithy to observe the sparks that shot out like arrows in all directions. It was a daily exhibition of which the urchins enjoyed heartily. And, clad in his scanty clothing above the knee and bare-bodied, the blacksmith let the sparks fly all round his body, and appeared like a veritable Allauddin of the Wonderful Lamp to the lads of the Village. I have already spoken of Veerabhadrachari as an adept at many trades–a sarva-tantra swatantra–a rather rare title for versatile geniuses. The great philosopher Vedanta Desikar was honoured with this title: it is said that this far-famed savant and philosopher once fashioned a pair of sandals which fitted perfectly, and provided a model for the professional cobblers. He had a nimble mind and nimble hands and his life was a lesson in self-help, even as Mahatma Gandhi’s was in recent times. Veerabhadrachari in his modest way acted on the same principle–though he may not have been conscious of the fact that any high principle was involved in his way of living. Of course, he had not heard of Vedanta Desikar at all. But it had become his habit to do all his work himself without dependence on others. If anybody had praised him for his fine quality or for following a noble principle, he would not have understood it.

 

When Veerabhadrachari took up a chiselling adze and started chipping a log of wood, the big pieces which he chopped would fly like hawks in all directions. He was an expert in building a house, in planning its foundations and its plinth, in erecting mud walls which were as neat and precise as if they were made of brick, in fixing up doors and windows, in devising hinges and bolts, in making up scaffoldings for building the upper floors of tenements. His fame as a skilled designer and builder had spread all over the region. Single-handed, he could do the work of ten blacksmiths, as many carpenters and masons, and had constructed numerous houses in the vicinity. Wherever one went in the villages near by, one came across this evidence of his handiwork; and all the houses looked so firm and lasting.

 

Veerabhadrachari constructed the house of Narasimhaiya, the priest of our village. If it had been situated in a town, it would have cost anywhere from sixty to seventy thousand rupees. But it cost only as much as ten thousand rupees, though built in a village in the good old days. It was one of the best houses built by Veerabhadrachari both from the point of view of cost and quality of the materials used. Though Narasimhaiya was a priest in the village temple, he had to pay the customary due of a bundle of hay at the time of harvest to the village astrologer, a custom handed down from many generations. But, latterly, Narasimhaiya, being himself in touch with divinity as a priestly demurred to this payment to an astrologer who lived by divination. ‘After all the astrologer derives his right by devotion to the stars and planets: How is my prerogative, who am engaged in the worship of Anjaneya, any the less?” argued Narasimhaiya. So this year when the astrologer came to the threshing floor for receiving the customary gift: “Well, your planets and stars can do nothing to me. I have my Anjaneya to take care of me. You may go your way,” said Narasimhaiya to the astrologer. The astrologer, whose income had been gradually shrinking, was not surprised at this, and went away quietly.

 

But a few days later Narasimhaiya had to solicit the favour of the astrologer to fix up an auspicious day for the house-warming ceremony of his newly-built home. However much Anjaneya was in his keeping, it was not possible for Narasimhaiya to take away from the astrologer his knowledge of the movements of the stars and planets. Indeed, Narasimhaiya was not at all comfortable at having to approach the astrologer in regard to this matter. But his wife was persistent: “It has been our custom to consult the astrologer to fix up an auspicious date, and when he gives a date it has always proved fortunate to the persons concerned. Please do consult the astrologer,” she urged. Narasimhaiya was reluctant: “The astrologer’s talk is mere bluff, as the stars and planets have no better business than to go about looking for my house!” Still, he proceeded to the astrologer’s house and asked him to fix an auspicious date. The astrologer said “The location of the house is not favourable to the position of the planets at the time of your birth. If you go to dwell in that house, you will not have any luck. You must propitiate the heavenly bodies and it means expense–at least fifty rupees will be required for the necessary ceremonies.” Narasimhaiya knew that he had incurred the displeasure of the astrologer, he did not, therefore, pay heed to his warning or advice: “I have come to listen to your rigmarole. I am going to dwell in that house without any of your advice” said Narasimhaiya and came away.

 

As soon as he returned home, he called his wife and said to her “Didn’t I tell you already that asking that astrologer is no good? He is planning to deprive me of fifty rupees. Am I going to bow before all this threat? We will move into the new house the day after tomorrow”. “What an idea!” exclaimed the wife. “After spending so much over the construction of the house, are we just moving into it as if it were a cattle pen? It should never be done without proper religious ceremonies. It is a house which must continue to bring luck to us and to our children for generations. Our days are over; bur what about the good of the children? Do not do anything that may bring ill luck to them in the future,” protested his wife. All news spreads quickly in a village. The word soon went round that Narasimhaiya was occupying his new house without due religious ceremonies and people said: “The pride of wealth has gone to his head.” Narasimhaiya came to hear of all this talk. Some elderly ladies called on Narasimhaiya’s wife and took her to task for this irreligious attitude. So at last Narasimhaiya had to agree to the performance of the necessary rites including the propitiation of the deities before occupying the house. “But I am not going to have it all done by that astrologer. Instead, I shall call in Veerabhadrachari,” he said.

 

Narasimhaiya had a reason to come to this decision. Veerabhadrachari had been often heard to say, “I know some astrology too. A person who can build a house surely knows also something of the rites to be performed for occupying it!” Everyone knew how versatile Veerabhadrachari was; and Narasimhaiya fully shared the confidence that the other villagers had in Veerabhadrachari’s many-sided accomplishments. Further, here was a good chance of paying off his scores against the astrologer and discrediting him. He did not want to lose such a good opportunity. “This fellow thinks no end of himself; he imagines that the crowing of the cock in his yard brings about the sunrise!” That very day Narasimhaiya met Veerabhadrachari and asked him: “You had better fix a good day for the house-warming ceremony yourself.” After some hesitation, Veerabhadrachari agreed to the request.

 

Veerabhadrachari knew a little reading and writing. He immediately secured an almanac, and chose an auspicious day. He had been present at several such ceremonies; so he pieced together in his memory the rites he had observed and their sequence. He also consulted a few house-owners who had performed these ceremonies. So he made a list of things required, flowers, fruits, betel leaves, nuts, cocoanuts, new cloth, vessels, lamp stands; cooked rice, dyed red and yellow; new clothes for himself and his wife. Finally, when he came to know that the astrologer had prescribed special propitiatory ceremonies to ward off evil planetary influences, he also put in a demand for a hundred rupees towards the expenses of these special ceremonies. This put Narasimhaiya into a state of great embarrassment. But he could not draw back at this stage. If he called off the arrangements and abandoned Veerabhadrachari now, all the dinner being prepared, with so much trouble and expense would go waste: he would cut a sorry figure before the people of the village, and, what was worse, he would be giving a chance to the astrologer to crow louder than ever. He would prevent at all cost the astrologer growing in prestige. So he meekly submitted to all the demands made by Veerabhadrachari, though he was smouldering with inward resentment all the time. The ceremony was duly gone through and Veerabhadrachari, decked in a new silk-bordered cloth, and his wife wearing the new sari presented to her, took leave of him and went back to their house. The other perquisites presented on the occasion, different kinds of grain, cocoanuts, fruits, flowers etc., were all loaded in new baskets and borne by the servants to Veerabhadrachari’s residence. At one stroke, he had earned on this single day more than he did during four months as a house-builder. This became apparent to Veerabhadrachari as much as to the village priest Narasimhaiya. No one had loaded the astrologer with so many gifts on the occasions of house-warming ceremonies in the village. As Veerabhadrachari, having his body daubed with sandal paste and flaunting a big round vermilion mark on his forehead and wearing new clothes, strode along majestically in the street accompanied by his wife was also wearing new clothes and suitably made up, they indeed looked like Siva and Parvati. The villagers looked on them with admiration and exclaimed: “He looks like a born astrologer: he is equal to any job.”

 

This incident made Veerabhadrachari quite another man altogether. It looked as if a new world had opened before him: a new opportunity, a new calling seemed to beckon to him: an easy and luxurious way of making a living. “An astrologer’s is a lighter job than a builder’s, and there is more profit in it. It also commands greater prestige, and is more noble and dignified. The labour involved is certainly very much less. “I gain as much in one day by following this occupation, as I did by toiling for three months constructing houses.” These thoughts passed through his mind. And in this new-found enthusiasm he forgot the fact that but for him, the scores of houses he had built in the village and the surrounding villages would never have come up. He forgot that hundreds of people always remembered with gratitude the well-built dwelling-places that his labour had provided for them. He was like the pillar–the astrologer was no more than like the paint on the walls. Veerabhadrachari was, for the moment, oblivious of this glaring , difference between his occupations old and new. The desire to become an astrologer became an obsession with him. It lured him as the mirage lures thirsty travellers in the burning desert. The very next day he started collecting textbooks bearing on the astrologer’s trade: Jyotishya Chintamani, Arudha Prashna Chandrika, Vidyaguru and so on. He started memorising the names of planets and stars. His chisel, saw, adze, planning box and other implements–everyone of which could build a kingdom–lay neglected in a corner and gathered rust. They had shone with lustre in his deft hands till the other day: now they retired into obscurity like an old man whose days of active life have come to an end. Veerabhadrachari ceased to look upon them, and instead, became absorbed in calculating favourable conjunctions of planets, and numerous other combinations, as expounded in the treatises on astrology.

 

Veerabhadrachari soon bloomed out as the astrologer of the village. He prescribed auspicious dates for marriages: he cast horoscopes; he officiated at house-warming ceremonies. The people of the village flocked to him to consult him on all things and sundry, on omens, for forecasts of various kinds, and to ward off all kinds of evil influences. This gave him a sense of new prestige and he considered that his status in the village had risen. The village astrologer whose popularity was already declining was almost forgotten.

 

It was not long, however before Veerabhadrachari began to have a feeling that he had also joined the ranks of the unemployed in the world. One house after another in the village stood in need of repair. If a pillar slanted or gave way, there was no one now to set it right. When the handles of the villagers’ implements–the hoe, the spade, the shovel or the pick-axe–broke loose or had to be replaced, there was no carpenter in the village who could attend to it. There was no one to  sharpen the ploughshare, or to retyre the village cart. The barber’s razor needed sharpening but no one could do it in the village. Veerabhadrachari had been carpenter, blacksmith and general mechanic for the whole village: but now that he had given up these jobs, the village began to present a ruined appearance. The villagers had to go to the neighbouring villages for all kinds of minor repairs. Veerabhadrachari was merely on the look out to officiate at house-warming ceremonies: but no new houses came up. The villagers found it impossible to get the services of carpenters and smiths from neighbouring villages. There had been one astrologer in the village. Now there were two: neither of them had enough to do: both became idle, dependent on others: mere star-gazers. If Veerabhadrachari had continued as a builder, scores of houses would have come up every year, and given a living to four such indolent astrologers. In the old days Veerabhadrachari had had no rest: he was busy all the time from early morning till late at night. His hands were always full of work of one kind or another. He toiled in the sun and rain: his skin had become tanned and his body was firm and bright as bronze. His mind was free and light as air. He seemed to be an image of the Creator Himself: ever fashioning, ever creating: and there was no kind of manual work at which he was not handy. In those days there was not a window in the village which stood in need of repair: no beam or rafter which had bent out of shape: no pillar which had slightest slant. When Veerabhadrachari worked as a master-builder he could not bear to see any building containing any part which needed repair, just as rice-eaters cannot tolerate the presence of grit or other foreign matter in a morsel of rice. The title of ‘Viswakarma’ could be literally applied to him. He had toiled for fifty years, and yet showed no signs of being borne down by age. But during the last two years he had a paunch: his limbs were no longer supple and firm but looked unshapely. His face showed signs of indolence: his moustache which had not a speck of grey on it had now turned white. The villagers uttered curses on astrology: “These evil planets have brought ruin on our houses: ruin seize them!” But Veerabhadrachari looked on unconcerned. His astrological treatises were neatly bundled up in a silken scarf, which he tied up and untied regularly. The bundle also contained a slate, and a few cowries. He sat watching through the window and waiting for his clients, and stroking his moustache, oblivious of which was gradually spreading over the village. Crowds of people used to go to him for consultation. Latterly had this had changed, and he had now few visitors.

 

Hardly two years had elapsed, and the roof of Veerabhadrachari’s own house began to leak like other houses in the village. White ants had started making depredations on the roof. The rafters and crossbeams got loose and had moved from their positions. In the old days when there was anything that went slightly wrong, Veerabhadrachari would be ready with his tools and set things right before further damage was done. To remove the tiles from the roof, make necessary repairs and replace the tiles on the roof was just a minor job for him. He was repairing all the houses in the village; to set right something that went wrong in his own house was a very simple matter. The wife had kept the house always spotlessly clean, taking her cue from the enthusiastic energy of her husband. But of late, after the Achari beca preoccupied with stars, planets and astrological literature and cast aside his chisel, plane and other tools, he had ceased to use his hands. His house was showing signs of disrepair and neglect but he was gazing at the roof and at his implements like a cripple looking up wistfully at the cocoanut tree far above his reach. One night it rained so heavily that water soaked into the walls and the roof collapsed. Luckily no one was hurt.

 

He had built so many houses that it should not have been difficult to build a new one for himself. Before he deserted the house-building trade for astrology, he had often thought of

re-building his house; but since it meant a mere eight or ten days’ work for him, he had gone on postponing the undertaking., “I can do this at any time; it is not such a big piece of work to bother about,” he used to say and drop the idea for the time being. Moreover, since his hands were always full of building houses for others, he had no time to attend to this small piece of work in regard to his own house. But now that his house had come down, he had to attend to its rebuilding. He started working on it.

 

Since Veerabhadrachari was now a full-fledged astrologer, he fixed a proper date for digging the foundations, and turning the sod with due pomp and ceremony, scented sticks, cocoanuts coloured powder and rice and flowers were all assembled for the rites which were performed with great punctiliousness. The villagers wondered among themselves, “He has prepared a perfect plan and designed to build a house that can last for ever. Why all this ceremonial fuss!” After turning the sod, trenches had to be dug for the foundation. If it had been the old days, he would have taken up the pick-axe himself and with the help of two or three free labourers he would have dug trenches three or four feet deep, without any further thought. And since his favour was wanted by everybody, there was no lack of willing labour. But now he had to hire labour by paying at full rates. And if the handles of the pick-axes got loose or gave away, he was not now in a position to fix them right. The digging of the foundation which would have taken only three days formerly was not completed even after thirty days. He used to build the walls himself; but now he entrusted it on contract to the Harijan labourers of the village. He knew how to erect them straight and strong; but now the work of the labourers which left the wall full of ugly curves and dents irritated him. After the Walls had been raised, he trusted the wood-work to a carpenter who was not so skilled a worker, but whose greed was unbounded. Even before he commenced his work, he extorted from Veerabhadrachari all the money, in advance, on one pretext or other. The walls were ding in an unshapely way; the beams that had to be raised on to the roof were lying about. It was already four months since the work had begun. There was untimely rain, and as a result the walls were damaged by cracks.

 

The rainy season was coming on; and there was no hope that the carpenter would finish his part of the work before the rains descended. Veerabhadrachari lost his temper, and became wild with everybody. In the old days he would have finished the whole job by himself in about a month. With an adze in hand he would have prepared all the beams required in just one day. He looked wistfully now and then at the tools lying neglected in a corner of his house. His shorts which he wore when he was working at the forge and which he had not cast away as it was still in good shape, was hanging from a peg, and his eyes wandered towards them. He had been working with clay, wood as well as iron in former times. Then his glance went down to regard for a moment the lace-bordered cloth, the bundle of astrological treatises and the cowries–the perquisites of his latter-day occupation. He immediately thought of fixing a day for the house-warming ceremony of the house now under construction, and started making calculations on the slate. Since he was now himself an astrologer, it was his intent to fix up a thoroughly auspicious day for the purpose.

 

Veerabhadrachari was so wholly engrossed in making the calculations, that he was not even aware that the shades of evening had begun to cover the earth. His wife came in and said, “Well, you sit here absorbed in your calculations, and the rain is likely to pour down. Even a little rain will bring down the wall. If that happens, our lot will be to dwell in a thatched cottage for the rest of our lives.” As soon as the Achari heard the word ‘rain’ he started as if a scorpion had stung him. He immediately got up, came out into the street and looked up at the sky. Black clouds were gathering and sweeping on like a herd of elephants moving in single file. The sky was pierced by lightning, and the rumble of thunder shook the earth. But since the lightning flashed in the north-west, Veerabhadrachari thought it would not rain that day, nor for a week more. He looked at the walls exposed to the ravages of the weather. He also saw the logs of wood, collected for being fashioned into beams, lying pell mell, and the bamboos and the tiles of the old house intended for the roofing lying everywhere in disorder. They seemed to complain in mute helplessness: “We are all here: but no one seems willing to put us into proper position.” Just then he heard again a rumble of thunder. Something stirred within him and put a new life into him. His face brightened with a new energy and a new determination. His movements became agile. The silk-bordered cloth he was wearing seemed to him like a fetter round his legs. Like a bee inebriated with honey, he swooped into the house. His wife had been familiar with his vigorous and athletic movements before he turned to be an astrologer. She now watched him with wonder: he hardly noticed her but went straight to the peg from which his shorts were suspended. He snatched it and put it on. The cloth he had worn slipped down and lay on the floor. He took up the bundle of astrological treatises, slate, cowries etc., tied up in a silk scarf and flung it all out. There was a wooden plank on which was a sacred vessel, surmounted by a cocoanut and mango leaves, and daubed with turmeric and red powder, his paraphernalia and equipment in the pursuit of his trade as an astrologer. He made a clean sweep of it all. His wife was noticing him with increasing fear and surprise, and wondering whether he had gone out of his senses. The Achari took up the adze and marched out. “What are you doing? Why?” shouted his wife following his footsteps. The Achari spoke not a word: he was like one possessed and proceeded to sharpen the tool. It had become rusty, and dull, but now it glistened with a new lustre. The clouds by now had scattered and stars appeared in the sky. Before he had taken to the almanac and the trade of an astrologer, the Achari used to work in just such a kind of starlight. And now he was at it again, once more his old self! He started chiselling the log of wood and shaping it into a beam. The strokes of his axe again fell in perfect rhythm, and huge chips went flying about. Hearing this sound, many villagers gathered at the shop. Veerabhadrachari worked on like one in a trance. He worked all through the night. By sunrise he had shaped all the logs lying about there into fine-looking beams. The chips of wood could be put into four cartloads. The villagers who watched were greatly excited over it. “The blight that had fallen on the village is now gone!” they exclaimed in joy. In front of the Achari’s house, which had presented a deserted appearance all these days, there now collected old carts, ploughs and other implements needing repair. The forge was once more lighted, and the bellows were plied. As the villagers depended on the Achari for so many things, many of them laboured willingly for him. They completed the roof of his new house in four days.

 

The smithy in front of Veerabhadrachari’s house had come to life again, and the adze was again in his hand. Noticing this the customary astrologer of the village mused: “I had no job for a couple of years. No one built houses. There were no house-warming ceremonies in consequence. The eclipse that had overtaken Veerabhadrachari has released him. Now new houses will arise and there will be the usual ceremonies for entering them.” With these thoughts passing through his mind, the professional astrologer dusted his almanac, and kept it ready for use.
