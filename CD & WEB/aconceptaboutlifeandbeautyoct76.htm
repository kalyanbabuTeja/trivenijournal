A CONCEPT ABOUT LIFE AND BEAUTY

 

HAR PRASAD SHARMA

 

            Master Sukhdev who was very much interested in philosophy asked a question:

 

            “Which of the historical places of India you liked most?” Miss Schemidt made herself comfortable on the sofa and said:

 

            “I liked Taj Mahal most, which is one of the wonders of the world?”

 

            Master Sukhdev nodded his head in assent and exclaimed: “That is right, that is right. That is the finest specimen of art in India. That is almost the unanimous opinion of all foreigners. Who can deny this fact?”

 

            But suddenly, Miss Schemidt interrupted him to express her own opinion about the philosophy of beauty. She said: “What you say is right but what is beauty and how can a thing of beauty become imperishable and immortal? These are the questions which always arise in my mind. Many scholars, thinkers, philosophers, poets and writers have expressed their ideas about beauty. Romain Rolland has expressed the view: ‘Beauty resides in the eyes of the beholder. If we think over this matter a bit carefully, we shall arrive at the conclusion that only that object is beautiful on seeing which we are bewitched and if anything does not appeal to us in spite of the fact that it is a fine piece of art we shall not find any trace of beauty in it.’ Bacon says: ‘The best part of an object of beauty is that which cannot be expressed through any work of art.’    It means that beauty cannot be expressed perfectly in any way. Ruskin has his own ideas about beauty. He says: ‘Bear in mind that some of the most beautiful things of the world are useless, for example, the peacock and flower’.”

 

            “But when I think of the philosophy of Ninon who says that what is attractive and beautiful is not always excellent but what is excellent is always beautiful, I think myself as to what is excellent which is always beautifu1. What strikes us with wonder is not beautiful, because according to an Italian proverb, the effect of wonder does not last more than three days. Therefore, Friedrich Schiller has said in very clear words that art is extremely difficult but its charm is perishable. Hence how can an object whose charm is lost soon be called beautiful and what is the value of an object which, according to Schiller, is perishable?”

 

            Miss Schemidt cast a questioning look at all, smiled and asked the above question. And then without a pause she began to answer the question herself thus: To understand this thing clearly, take a simple example. To a visitor or to any person who sees the Taj for the first time, it will look extremely beautiful. The Taj will look less beautiful when seen for the second time and much less beautiful when seen for the third time and, before long, it will lose all its charm and attraction. How can then the Taj be called beautiful, which looked attractive at first sight but lost all its attraction when seen again and again? It is a mere illusion. An object which we call beautiful today, we do not care to cast even a look at it after sometime. Then how can it be called beautiful? Now let us see what is really beautiful? It is the Supreme Being who is really beautiful because the more we long for seeing Him, the more we are attracted towards Him. The intensity of this longing never lessens. That is why great seers, sages and saints who are madly in love with God renounce all worldly things – their houses, near and dear ones – and thus snapping all worldly ties, retreat to the solitary caves, attracted by the beauty of Supreme Lord. They are always meditating upon God and their eyes are always fixed upon Him; their mind is not diverted from Him even for a moment. They are always absorbed in thinking of the Supreme Being till their own being is merged in Him. That was why the Gopis were so much attracted by the beauty of Lord Krishna that they always circled around Him, madly. Compared to Lord Krishna’s beauty, nothing in the world looked beautiful to them. That is why Schiller said that it is no use loving work of art which is perishable. If it is perishable, the question of its being eternally beautiful does not arise at all. Lord Krishna has said this thing in very clear terms:

 

           

 

“Arjuna, I am even that which is the seed of all beings. For there is no creature, animate or inanimate, which exists without Me.”

 

Therefore, the whole world is a manifestation of His form. Hence, when everything which looks beautiful in the world derives its beauty from the Supreme Being, is it not, then, the Supreme Being who is most beautiful or is it something else?”

 

            On hearing this, the whole room resounded with the cheers of those who were present there. They looked at one another, nodding their heads in assent. They were astonished with the intelligence of Miss Schemidt and began to shower praises upon her. In the light of the sermon of the Gita, Miss Schemidt had separated the grain of the reality from the chaff of illusion and doubt and placed it before them all. They began to look upon Miss Schemidt as a great philosopher. Then suddenly, a young writer named Ram Swarup raised another question. Aplogetic­ally, he said to Miss Schemidt:

 

            “What is your philosophy of life? What is the object of human life? Some people say that life is meant for action, while others say that it is a hopeless burden. Will you please throw some light upon it?”

 

            On hearing this question, Miss Schemidt became a bit serious. She thought over the matter a bit deeply and then said: “It is not easy to understand the mystery of life. Learned men have looked at life from different angles. Some of them have regarded it as a means to attain salvation; others have said that life is meant for making oneself merry; while still others have described it as a mere bubble of water. Stephen Zewig has said that one who devotes all his energies to benefiting the coming generations leads a really good life. Goethe’s philosophy is that a useless life is no better than death. Another thinker has said about life that he wants to live in this world only once and therefore, he wants to do, once for all, all the good that he can do to his friends, because he would not like to be born in this world again. Hillard’s opinion about life is that life is another name for action that one does not act only exists but does not live. But most of the people in the world live like what H. G. Wells has said: “Our life is just like the spark produced by the friction of two pieces of dry wood. It flashes for a moment and is then extinguished. We do not know where it comes from and where it goes.” Even then, this life has not been created in vain, though we may not understand what it is? Take an example, We go out for a walk in a jungle. There we see lofty mountains and say that God has in vain created these uneven mountains which are very high here and very low there. Descending from the mountains, we see forests spreading far and wide and the earth lying unevenly. Then we start thinking whether all these extensive forests have been created in vain and whether the uneven earth is meant for lying idle. Again we find that at places, the flat earth is extending to a great distance, while at other places there lies the vast expanse of the ocean which has no bounds. Then we ask: what is the use of all these extensive lands and vast seas? But our condi­tion is just like that of an ant which crawls along our breast, tries to rise towards our neck, considering it to be a vertical rock, climbs upon it. Then it descends a little towards the chin and finds a pit there; then, it climbs on to the lips and, taking it for a cavern, it goes back to the nose, but taking the nostrils for a grotto, it gets frightened and runs away from there, and consi­dering the cheeks to be hillocks, it starts ascending towards them, but as soon as it approaches the eyes, it stops and taking them for lakes of water, it flees from there also. Then it goes towards the eye-lashes and taking them for small forests, it climbs upon the forehead and, considering it to be a flat tract of land, it starts running upon it, but as soon as it approaches the hair, it finds that a dreadful jungle of hair is spreading, all around and it runs away from there lest it should be entangled in it. Thus, our eyes, mouth, nose, cheeks and hair have perfect significance but the ant does not realize it and roams about here and there. Similarly, life is not without purpose but if, like an ant, we are not able to understand its significance and if we grope in the darkness here and there, whose fault is it? What is the fault of God in it?”
