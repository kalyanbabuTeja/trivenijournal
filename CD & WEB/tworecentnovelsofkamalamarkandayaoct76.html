TWO RECENT NOVELS OF KAMALA MARKANDAYA

 

DR S. KRISHNA SARMA

 

            Kamala Markandaya has an assured place in the field of post-Independence Indo-Anglian writing, not only as a woman writer but as a writer with a considerable range and talent. While her work is remarkable for its conspicuous absence of any­thing specifically idiosyncratic in theme or technique, perhaps this very absence constitutes the hallmark of Kamala Markandaya’s writing. Another noteworthy feature of her work is the non­repetitive nature of her themes. Apart from distant resemblances and echoes from one novel to another in character or situation, the broad lineaments of her themes vary from work to work, ranging between rural and urban, personal and pseudo-mystical, racial and local, and so on. Her most recent novels, The Nowhere Man and Two Virgins, both published in 1973, show this diversity in theme and structure and in artistic performance. The first is the story of an Indian immigrant in England, and the second of an adolescent girl from an Indian village. But while The Nowhere Man is forceful in narrative and is purposeful, Two Virgins appears to fritter away its artistic opportunities.

 

            The Nowhere Man is about an Indian settler in England and the race conflict which comes from behind to almost overwhelm him. Mr Srinivas was born in an orthodox upper middle-class Indian family which was exposed to Western culture and education through two generations, and he was entertaining dreams of a loyal and fearless life under the old dispensation. But the suspici­ous and tyrannical British overlord would not permit such a quiet and smooth life: the times were troubled ones. So in a kind of despair Mr Srinivas migrates to England to realise whatever dreams he may have had. There, he is permitted to work, buy property and to raise a family. He is accepted without ado as Mr Srinivas of No. 5, quiet, unassuming, though a little queer.

 

            Already exposed to the Western cultural values, Mr Srinivas could easily adapt himself to the British modes of life and thought, and though his wife Vasantha persists in Indian ways of living, dressing and thinking, Srinivas brings up his children, Laxman and Seshu, in British ways. Imbibing the ways of the land as children do, they grow up with a healthy respect for British Constitution, religion, language and British modes of behaviour. Vasantha, with her silk sarees and her dreams of a joint family–­her two sons bringing their wives and living on the two upper floors of the house while she and her husband would live on the ground ­floor suggests the distance which Mr Srinivas and his children have travelled away from the Indian norms.

 

            The War brings disaster to Vasantha’s dreams: both the boys enlist, like every other Briton, in the War. Seshu, the younger, withdraws from the front and plies an ambulance in London–but death finds him nonetheless, driving the ambulance, as surely as it might have on the fields of France. Laxman, shrewder and farther removed from his mother’s dreams, marries an English girl, thus escaping a possible posting in France, and at the end of the War, joins the business house of his father-in-law at Plymouth. Carrying the broken pieces of her dreams, Vasantha too dies soon after, leaving Mr Srinivas forlorn and desolate. As her ashes are merged and drawn into the waters of the English river, it looks as though the last-physical-shreds of Indianness that had clung to Srinivas are absorbed into Englishness. His neighbours, particularly Mrs Fletcher of No.6, look upon this quiet and eccentric Mr. Srinivas as indeed one of them.

 

            Sometime after his wife’s death, Mr. Srinivas strikes a friendship with Mrs. Pickering, another frustrate soul, past middle age. These two destitutes find empathy and solace in each other’s company, and drift into a companionable fellowship. It doesn’t seem odd at all that they set up house together as man and wife, respecting each other’s individual quirks of temperament. They even take some tenants by renting out a part of the house, thus adding to their financial resources.

 

            The central issue of the novel comes into focus with the arrival at this time, on the scene, of Fred Fletcher, the son of Mrs Fletcher. She had despaired of his ever turning out well and had sent him off to Australia. But now he has returned, burdened with a family which he cannot support. Accidentally coming across an anti-immigrant campaign in London, Fred is fired with a fanatic zeal, and forms a one-man anti-Black league in his locality, and adopts Mr. Srinivas as his particular target. He sets afoot a regular campaign against Mr. Srinivas and tries his best to discredit him.

 

            Mr. Srinivas is perturbed, and with all his passivity, he does react on a psychological level. (It is perhaps this which prompts him to put on the unaccustomed Indian dress one evening to assert his individuality.) Nevertheless, he survives the entire smear campaign of Fred, including the excruciating ‘tarring and feathering,’ without a protest. Even though he knows that Fred was his tormentor, he does not care to report him to the police, and refuses to discuss him with Mrs. Fletcher. But this passivity and his unfighting attitude only infuriate Fred further.

 

            When he shows symptoms of leprosy, Mr. Srinivas is convinced that it is his duty–a duty he owed to the society–to warn the tenants about this and evict them. Mrs. Pickering tries to soft-pedal the attempt, but knowing the ineffectuality of her efforts, he visits his tenants in person and, unabashed, explains the reasons for the proposed eviction. The tenants resent not so much the crude attempt of Mr. Srinivas as their need to uproot themselves, particularly as the housing problem is so acute. Fred is quick to exploit the situation, and giving the eviction of tenants a racial bias, he tries to whip up the antagonism of the neighbourhood. This is not very successful, as illustrated by the skeptical remarks of the bar-tender across the street. Unable to rouse the neighbourhood, Fred in despair tries to burn down No.5. This leads to his own death sooner than that of Mr. Srinivas his intended victim, for, operating in secrecy, Fred is hidden from everybody gathered there, and is charred to death–“hoist with his own petard,” as it were–while Mr. Srinivas is rescued by the police Sergeant. But he too dies, from delayed shock perhaps, or as a result of shock on the top of extreme weakness following the attack of pneumonia. But he lives on after being rescued from the burning house long enough to thank the police Sergeant and to look around smiling–long enough to vindicate his stance. It is a triumph of his attitude to England, and a defeat for the senseless and irrational antagonism of the likes of Fred. In the end Laxman comes round to accept his father’s ways of thinking yet another proof of the soundness of the principle that Mr. Srinivas life exemplified: an unostenta­tious and complete yet unservile acceptances of Britishness and identification with the land of adoption.

 

            If Mr. Srinivas has accepted Britain, the people among whom he founded his house too have accepted him: Mrs Fletcher has accepted him on grounds of Christian ethics; Dr. Radcliffe’s acceptance is on a more personal plane: he was seeking human companionship away from the upstart set favoured by his wife, and Mr. Srinivas with his passivity and self-sufficiency, was a refreshing experience for him, so that for once we find a doctor going in search of his patient. Mrs. Pickering’s acceptance of Mr. Srinivas is complete. The way she fills the vacancy in the life of Srinivas, she could be a reincarnate Vasantha–only more completely British. In fact she seems to be closer to Mr. Srinivas than Vasantha, who had always carried a piece of India in her heart and had never agreed to root herself in Britain. (The fact that Vasantha’s dream of a joint family fructifies in a different shape, to include the tenants, in the reign of Mrs. Pickering is a possible pointer.) Mr. Srinivas exemplifies the basic Indian catholicism in its broadest sense, which absorbs and accommodates and shapes itself to new situations. Mrs. Pickering, Mrs. Fletcher, Dr. Radcliffe, the bar tender, etc., represent the acceptance of the immigrants by the British, on different planes. And Fred Fletcher clearly belongs to that absolute minority, whether it is among the native Britons or the restless and numerous coloured immigrants, who tend to disrupt the harmony of the race relations.

 

            The narration is effected mostly through Mr. Srinivas mind reminiscing. He begins to unfold events for himself after learning of his leprosy from the doctor. His life in India before his emigration is unfolded to Mrs. Pickering while he is in bed with pneumonia. It is difficult to see how the narrative in India helps the scaffolding of the novel. We learn of young Srinivas cultural alienation, we learn of the factors contributing to his emigration, and we learn of the ‘atrocities’ of the British officials. In all this we see Mr. Srinivas as an embodiment of passivity. The two occasions when Srinivas “acted” were when he returned the medal awarded to him, and when he reacted violently as the police officer lifts the skirt of young Vasantha with his cane. His life in England is as much a study in passivity, and that which is clear enough in the British part of the narrative need not be laboured through 55 out of 312 pages being devoted to his life in India. Was the writer merely polarising the character of Srinivas into the colonial past, or using this to work out some other contrasts? Beyond a couple of instances the life of Mr. Srinivas in England is more or less explicit and has no need of a remoter foundation. Is the writer recounting the brutalities of the British in India just for the purpose of documentation? Perhaps the appeal to the sensational and anthropological interests in the overseas reader is too strong a temptation to resist: and sensing the current popularity of immigrant writing, Mrs. Markandaya too was probably trying to exploit the market.

 

            Perhaps that explains the title of the book too, modelled obviously on Ralph Ellison’s The Invisible Man. But unlike Ellison’s work, The Nowhere Man does not dramatise the ‘nowhereness’ of the central character. There are weaknesses in the plot too: while it is easy to accept the death of Seshu in a bomb raid, and while the narrative located in India is altogether plausible, though not indispensable. It is not easy to see why Vasantha should be eliminated, through consumption, and why Mr. Srinivas should be made to suffer from leprosy. These are too fortuitous to be plausible­ they do not possess that artistic inevitability, and show the writer’s helplessness in manipulating the events more artistically. One wonders, if only for a moment, how the story would have run if Vasantha had not died, or how Fred would have reacted if Mr. Srini­vas hadn’t had leprosy and wasn’t obliged to turn out his tenants!

 

            In spite of these faults, the novel is readable. Though it belongs to the immigrant writing of the recent times, it helps the reader to come to a set of conclusions different from those drawn from the other novels.

 

            Two Virgins is a story of adolescence and growth to maturity in an Indian village of two sisters, Lalitha and Saroja. Lalitha the more beautiful though the more gullible of the two tastes of sophistication from her school mistress Miss Mendoza, and starts dreaming of a career for herself. When Mr. Gupta’s film unit camps in the village, Lalitha’s dreams take a definite shape, and she succumbs to the irresistible temptation. She disappears from the village and goes after her dream, only to return pregnant. Her parents take her back to the city, confront Mr. Gupta with his foul play and get him to meet the expenses for an abortion. But after the operation Lalitha disappears, irrevocably this time, choosing the glitter of the city life to that of the village.

 

            Saroja the younger and shrewder of the two sisters is the central conscience through which the narrative is unfolded. She does not pass through any direct experience: much of her experience is vicarious; she observes, infers, assumes and puzzles, and very little is told her by way of information. Her only sources of knowledge are Jaya her classmate, Manikkam the milkman’s wife and Chingleput the sweet vendor. It is from these, and of course from the experiences of Lalitha, that Saroja gleans all her wisdom. She is shown growing from a young girl just starting to put on the ‘davani’ (over-garment or half-saree) over her blouse to hide her sprouting breasts into a full-blown woman towards the end of the novel passing, vicariously, through the excruciating experiences of sex, seduction, motherhood, abortion, and the snares of the city.

 

            The polarisation between the vain, pretty, butterfly-like character chasing the pleasures of life and getting caught in the snares of superficiality, and the sober and less pretty but more dependable characters tending to be more shrewd and judicious in similar circumstances, is perhaps older than Jane Austen. Such contrasts are not infrequent in modern fiction too. But what purpose is Lalitha serving in the education of Saroja?–as an object lesson? It is not improbable, as Saroja possesses a number of characteristics common with her sister. Curiosity in sex, for one thing. Again, her initial response to Devraj’s overtures is similar to that of Lalitha to Mr. Gupta, though artless and less deliberate. But what does the disappearance of Lalitha after the abortion signify? Are we to assume that an abortion might remove the growing foetus from a womb but cannot restore the lost virginity? Is that the reason why Saroja refuses to oblige the ardent Chingleput at the end of the novel?

 

            If that is so, if virginity once lost is lost forever, why then the emphasis on that aspect in the title of the book? Or is the novelist de-emphasizing the traditional values of virginity? The domestic debates (pp. l76-179 and pp. 200-202) appear to indicate a positive stance of the writer with regard to pre-marital sex, abortion and unmarried motherhood. The debate is quite topical and the ideas progressive, and would probably please a section of the reading public in this year of Grace when the world is celebrating International Women’s Year. But then, why is Saroja made to react so violently when Devraj touches her (p. 245) on their last visit to Mr Gupta’s house, and reject Chingleput, more soberly though, at the end? After all, Saroja loves children: she suffers when Lalitha gets rid of the unwanted child, and she hates the society–“the beastly tamer” of such violent passions–for this.

 

            While the ardour of Devraj for Saroja has been carefully prepared for step by step, as it were, the reader is not prepared, even by a hint, for the final incident involving Chingleput and Saroja. Chingleput is perhaps the most negative character (even more than Manikkam) in the novel. And when the narrative opens with an account of Chingleput, the reader is misled into expecting that Chingleput would figure prominently in the tale to follow. Barring the occasions when Saroja went on her bicycle to buy sweets from him or when she was shown his anatomy without embarrassment to either-because it was “ordinary and natural” (p. 43) – he never enters the narrative even edgewise. So the final scene appears rather crude, inartistic and indeed unwarranted. It only shows an unwillingness of the author to let go an opportunity of referring to sex. One is in the danger of being called prudish for saying so. Yet in the works of some other writers where sex is used freely, as in Updike or Murdock, the writer is seen trying to integrate it into whatever scheme of values he is endeavouring to uphold. There is no such artistic integration here. Saroja rejects the way of life and values chosen and pursued by Lalitha and accept the traditional values upheld by Aunt Alamelu. The final encounter does not add to this impression. For the rest the novel looks more like an exercise in genteel sensationalism, and there appears to be no co-ordination between the title and the theme.

 

            Lalitha is described (on the blurb of the book) as rebelling against and breaking away from “the constrictions of village life,” giving the impression that village life as such is ‘constricting.’ Saroja too has been surrounded by the same atmosphere, same life and values, but she never felt cooped up: on the contrary, after her experiences in the city, she was only too eager to return to the green fields, spaces and the buffalo and so on. She is completely at home in this world, as Lalitha could never be. Obviously it is Lalitha’s temperament which was impatient for the surface glitter of city life and sophistication, and ‘restraints’ or ‘freedoms’ of rural life, or even the traditional values practised by the middle class households, have had no major impact on the progress of action or in the shaping of the events.

 

            The intense generalisation the author indulges in, on the level of time and locale and moral codes suggests that it could be typical experience that is being narrated or recorded. The author knows that such is not the case, and yet encourages the non-Indian reader to see the usual so-called “world-wide” phenomenon operating in the locale the author had chosen. If only she had cared to individualize the characters she might have shown the tale for what it is, a local, inconsequential tale with a moral at the end.

 

            Moreover, the novelist seems to be equally unsure of her language. Was she writing this for the non-Indian reader? If so, many native terms like ‘davani’ (p. 129) and ‘tope’ ( p. 167) would not mean anything, and may, like “Mysore pak” (p. 83) –­but, “Bombay hulva” (p. 102) – (with its queer italicising), easily confuse the reader. Is it addressed to the Indo-Anglian reader? We notice then that characters like Aunt Alamelu and Amma not only speak in English (a necessity in an Indo-Anglian novel), but they use such English idioms and cliches as would he beyond the normal comprehension of tradition-ridden and illiterate village women (‘basilisk’, p. 47; ‘chokra’, p 58; and some conceptual usages that figure in their conversation; pp. 94, 136, 158, 180).

 

            Thus, despite certain topicality of the theme, the novel fails to convince on the artistic plane, and it lacks authenticity of expression and character.      The other novel, The Nowhere Man,    is more forceful, and brings a fresh outlook to the immigrant problem. In addition, the few faults in the construction could be glossed over because basically the novel is a grim comedy. Two Virgins fails where one would expect it normally to succeed, and throws up the weaknesses in the artistry of the writer. Surely one could expect a better performance from a writer in her eighth book!
